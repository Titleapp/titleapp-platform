## LFN.202.01 -- PILOT STAFFING

**Category:** AVIATION
**Policy #:** LFN.202.01
**Origination Date:** 02/14
**Last Revision Date:** 03/23
**Last Review Date:** 03/23
**Next Review Date:** 03/24
**Pages:** 1 of 2 (note: header says "1 of 1" but content spans 2 pages)

### PURPOSE

To ensure proper pilot staffing

### POLICY

1. There should be a minimum of four flight-ready pilots permanently assigned per aircraft, not including spare aircraft. Temporary staffing by fewer pilots is permitted for up to six months, provided such staffing ensures FAA crew rest requirements per CAMTS, NAAMTA, and the approved GOM.

2. Scheduling practices reflect consideration for minimizing duty-time fatigue, length of shift, number of shifts per week, and day-to-night rotation. Pilots are on a rotation schedule. The schedule typically consists of pilots working 4 day and 3-night shifts or 3 day and 4-night shift followed by 7 days off. Exceptions must be approved by the Chief Pilot, Director of Aviation Operations, or Senior Director of Aviation Operations (DO).

3. All bases will have a quiet area for flight planning, training, and record-keeping.

4. Prior to being released to work a night shift as PIC, all new LFN pilots shall complete at least three daytime patient flights. In cases where actual patient flights are not possible within a single hitch, actual flights may be conducted with a simulated patient. For RW pilots, these flights shall include at least one scene flight and at least one interfacility transport, (with actual or simulated patients). These flights may be completed during the shadowing hitch or during the pilot's first "7 days" hitch. The Chief Pilot may extend any of these requirements, including the number of shadowing hitches, "7 days" hitches or number of day transports, on a case-by-case basis.

5. Any pilot who has not flown enough to maintain proficiency will, upon request, be allowed a proficiency flight. The flight can be approved by the SDAO, DAO, CP, or AAOC. If a pilot requests a proficiency flight for an emergency procedure, the pilot must make the request in time to schedule a company trainer to fly with him/her. No emergency procedures will be conducted or simulated without a company training pilot or check airman on board.

6. New RW pilots will use higher weather minimums during their first 7-day shifts or more if extended by the CP. The weather minimums to be used are 135.609 Night Cross Country minimums (with NVG or TAWS).

7. New FW Captains with less than 100 hours as Pilot in Command in type, of turbine powered airplane time operated under Part 135 in IMC conditions, are restricted to higher weather landing minimums. These Captains will be designated as "High Minimums Captain" and must add 100 feet to the minimum decent altitude (MDA) or the decision altitude (DA) and one-half mile (or RVR equivalent) to the landing visibility minimums. Part 135.225(e) Pilots are responsible for informing the FW CP when they have achieved a minimum of 100 hours in type and the CP shall review the pilots company logbook to validate the pilot's flight time. Upon validation of the flight time the "High Minimums Captain" designation will be removed.

## LFN.202.02 -- FLY SAFELY & NOISE ABATEMENT

**Category:** AVIATION
**Policy #:** LFN.202.02
**Origination Date:** 05/06
**Last Revision Date:** 03/23
**Last Review Date:** 03/23
**Next Review Date:** 03/24
**Pages:** 1 of 1

### PURPOSE

To ensure LFN minimizes its noise signature while also executing safe operations.

### POLICY

1. The Chief Pilot or designee is responsible for attending noise abatement committee meetings at airports where LFN maintains a base.

2. Many areas are noise sensitive. This policy cannot address every situation, but it will provide general guidelines to follow when specific 'trouble spots' are not addressed. Nothing in this policy will be construed as an attempt to alter any FAA regulation. The PIC of an aircraft is directly responsible for, and is the final authority as to, the operation of the aircraft.

3. Noise complaints will be considered on a case-by-case basis and should be referred to the Senior Director of Aviation Operations (DO), Director of Aviation Operations, or Chief Pilot for review. The DO is responsible for keeping the Chief Executive Officer informed of any noise complaints.

4. When practical and appropriate, maximize horizontal separation. The ground track of the helicopter should be maintained as far as possible from noise sensitive areas. This is the most important noise abatement measure.

5. When practical and appropriate, maximize vertical separation. The flight profile should maximize the vertical speed, consistent with sound operating procedures and in accordance with the GOM. Typically, the helicopter should depart at Vy (best rate of climb speed) as defined in the aircraft operations manual. Approach should be at the steepest angle possible, consistent with sound operating procedures as outline in the aircraft operations manual.

6. Gradual and smooth control inputs results in a reduced noise impact.

7. Avoid the use of steep turns.

8. When practical and appropriate, enroute altitude should be maintained at or above 1500 feet AGL.

9. Night operations are particularly troublesome for helicopters. Noise impact under FAA standards specifies each night flight be counted as equivalent to 10-day flights in terms of noise impact. The reason for this is background noise levels (which mask helicopter noise) are lower at night, and people are generally more sensitive to noise while sleeping.

## LFN.202.04 -- AIRCRAFT FUELING / DEFUELING PROCEDURES

**Category:** AVIATION
**Policy #:** LFN.202.04
**Origination Date:** 03/06
**Last Revision Date:** 08/22
**Last Review Date:** 03/23
**Next Review Date:** 03/24
**Pages:** 1 of 1

### PURPOSE

Refueling aircraft must be done in accordance with the FAA approved LFN Fuel Manual.

### POLICY

1. A pilot authorized to fly the specific type of aircraft being refueled will be present whenever an aircraft is being fueled to ensure the proper fuel type and quantity is dispensed, and all safety guidelines are followed.

2. No personnel will be inside the aircraft or working around the aircraft while it is being refueled with the exception of refueling with a patient onboard.

3. In the event refueling is necessary with a patient onboard, one medical crew must remain onboard to monitor the patient. The second medical crew will remove the aircraft fire extinguisher and position themselves outside the aircraft near the pilot to observe the refueling operation and deploy the fire extinguisher if necessary. The extinguisher seal will only be broken if the fire extinguisher is used. Doors and windows on the same side of the aircraft as the fuel port will be closed. All others will be open during the refueling operations with passengers on board. If an aircraft refuels with a patient onboard, complete a QM report.

4. If refueling with a patient on board is required, the pilot will complete a QM report in Baldwin after the flight.

5. Hot refueling (refueling while aircraft engine(s) are running) is not allowed.

6. No aircraft will be fueled or defueled inside a hangar.

7. Defueling an aircraft will be done in accordance with the applicable maintenance manual and/or the aviation vendor's established protocols.

8. The servicing of any oxygen system is prohibited during aircraft fueling /defueling procedures.

## LFN.202.07 -- HELICOPTER NIGHT VISION GOGGLE OPERATIONS (HNVGO)

**Category:** AVIATION
**Policy #:** LFN.202.07
**Origination Date:** 02/08
**Last Revision Date:** 03/23
**Last Review Date:** 03/23
**Next Review Date:** 03/24
**Pages:** 1 of 2

### PURPOSE

To provide direction and guidance on the safe and appropriate use of night vision goggles.

### POLICY

1. The Pilot-in-Command shall comply with all LFN HNVGO operations.

2. NVGs will be carried on every rotor-wing flight regardless of time of day.

3. With the exception of RW IFR flights, night flights not utilizing NVGs must be authorized in advance by the Rotor Wing Chief Pilot, Assistant Director of Operations, or Senior Director of Aviation Operations (DO).

4. HNVGOs will only be conducted in aircraft with an approved Supplemental Type Certificate (STC) authorizing such operations. The Rotorcraft Flight Manual (RFM) Supplement must be current and retained in the RFM.

5. For all NVG flights at night, the pilot must have visual light surface reference which is sufficient enough to safely control the helicopter (Reference FAR 135.207).

6. In the event both direction-controllable searchlights are inoperable, nighttime scene calls utilizing NVG's **will not** be attempted.

7. During flight planning and crew briefings, the pilot will brief the medical crew on the following:
   - Type of flight; aided, unaided, or both
   - Weather for the route
   - Illumination
   - Obstacles along the route
   - NVG deficiencies
   - Crew experience
   - Abort criteria

8. For HNVGO operations authorized below 300 feet and to unimproved landing areas, at least one other qualified person shall be equipped with NVGs during the landing phase to assist in clearing the site. The "other required person" must be current and trained in accordance with the approved training program.

9. Goggles will be stored in their protective case inside the crew quarters or the aircraft when not in use. Goggles will not be stored in an aircraft if temperatures exceed 85 degrees F, or anytime the aircraft is not in a secure location.

10. After each HNVGO flight, the pilot will enter the flight crew currency in the electronic record keeping program.

11. If both med crewmembers are non-current for HNVGOs contact the AAOC and plan on getting a currency flight to get the med crewmembers current.

12. NVG training standards are in the LFN Training Manual.

13. Each NVG device shall have a maintenance log and will be reviewed prior to any HNVGO flight. The following will be Reviewed:
   - Unit S/N
   - Last and next inspection dates
   - Date of use
   - Pilot/crewmember name
   - Pilot certificate number (pilots only)
   - Remarks (irregularities and discrepancies)

14. Each crewmember utilizing NVGs shall perform the NVG inspection upon start of shift.

15. LFN will be responsible for the maintenance of NVGs.

### Addendum A -- Night Vision Goggle Security and Maintenance Procedures

#### General

This procedure sets forth the minimum requirements for records, personnel (who are authorized to perform preventative maintenance, maintenance and inspection, and the guidance necessary to ensure the night vision goggles still meet the manufacturer's requirements for continued aviation use.

#### Security

1. The night vision goggles are highly sensitive items.
2. At the base and when not in use, the night vision goggles should be kept in a locked container, i.e. wall locker, file cabinet, etc. When the room is vacant, the door should be locked. At no time will the night vision goggles be stored or left in an unsecured aircraft.
3. When not in use on a flight, the night vision goggles should be mounted on the flight helmet and in the stowed position or stored in the carrying case or pouch. When departing the helicopter for patient care, the night vision goggles may be removed from the helmet and stowed in the carrying case or pouch, to minimize damage.

#### Tracking

1. When each pair of night vision goggles is released / sent to a base, the information (base, make, model, serial number and last inspection date) is entered into the LFN electronic record keeping system.
2. The duty pilot at each base will be responsible for ensuring the night vision goggles are maintained according with these instructions and monitoring the medical crewmembers checking out and in of goggle sets for their shifts.
3. The medical crewmember is responsible for checking the goggles out and in from the duty pilot for their shift, inspecting them according to the NVG Preoperational Checklist, and notifying the pilot of any defects or repairs needed.

### Addendum 1 -- NWAAR HEMS Common Frequency and Traffic Advisory Practices

**North West Association of Aeromedical Responders**
Statement of Best Practice
*HEMS Common Frequency and Traffic Advisory Practices*

**Background:**

There is no substitute for full situational awareness. It is essential HEMS pilots and crew remain alert, look for other traffic, and exchange traffic information when approaching or departing any landing site. This best practice provides a framework for consistent, efficient, and coordinated self-announce practices by aircraft transiting to, from, and in the vicinity of a HEMS landing zone (LZ). For simplification purposes, the term LZ applies to all landing sites.

In an effort to improve safety, Members of the North West Association of Aeromedical Responders (NWAAR) agree to follow the best practice as outlined below. This best practice applies to all HEMS landing sites unless FAA regulations, operational control, or local procedures require otherwise. These practices are not intended to conflict with operational control of any aircraft.

**Common Frequency Information:**

To achieve the greatest degree of safety, it is essential all aircraft transmit and receive on a common frequency identified for the purpose of LZ advisories.

123.025 MHz is the accepted common frequency unless a LZ is located within the boundaries of Class B, C, or D airspace, or whenever a facility or location specific frequency is required.

For LZs located within the boundaries of Controlled Airspace, traffic advisories for airborne aircraft MAY OR MAY NOT be provided by the controlling agency. However, the requirement to communicate on an ATC frequency does not preclude the HEMS aircraft from maintaining aircraft visual separation and also monitoring and or communicating on 123.025 MHz if desired.

123.075 MHz, also known as the "Portland Downtown Helipad" frequency, is used in the Portland area. Due to the large volume of non-EMS VFR aircraft in the Portland area, "Portland Downtown Helipad", should remain the preferred frequency when transiting in the Portland area and operating into and out of those facilities.

**Best Practice:**

Whenever practical, and when not required to be communicating on another ATC frequency, aircraft should continuously monitor and communicate on 123.025 MHz as appropriate.
It should be noted, aircraft operating to or from another nearby LZ may make self-announce broadcasts on the same frequency. To help identify one LZ from another, the LZ name (or general location if an unnamed LZ) should be spoken at the beginning and ending of each self-announce transmission.

**A. Inbound Traffic:**

   a. No less than five (5) miles out; report name of LZ, call sign, altitude, location relative to the LZ, landing or over flight intentions, and name of the LZ.

   *Example: "(Hospital Name), (Aircraft Call Sign), 1,500', 7 miles east, landing, (Hospital Name)".*

   b. Report turning final for the LZ. For LZs with multiple approach paths, this call will include the direction arriving from.

   c. Report once on the ground.

**B. Departing Traffic:**

   a. Upon startup or while running on the ground, monitor the appropriate frequency for other traffic in the area.

   b. Prior to departure, report intentions for takeoff and direction of flight; LZ, call sign, intentions, and name of the LZ.

   *Example: "(Hospital Name), (Aircraft Call Sign), departing to the northwest, (Hospital Name)".*

   c. Report after takeoff.

   d. Continuously monitor the common frequency until at least 5 miles from the LZ.

While transiting between LZs, bases, or other facilities, aircraft are encouraged to periodically self-announce call-sign, altitude, position, and heading as appropriate so other aircraft transiting the area may be made aware.

Aeronautical Information Manual, Section 10-2-4 (B-2)

### Addendum 2 -- Harborview Medical Center Deconfliction Procedure

**HARBORVIEW MEDICAL CENTER HELIPORT (WA53) -- DECONFLICTION PROCEDURE (7/15)**

Harborview Medical Center (HMC) administration has requested Airlift Northwest (ALNW) develop and distribute a policy to assist in airspace de-confliction on and around the Harborview Medical Center Heliport.

These standards depend on Pilot-to-Pilot communication, as well as communication with the ALNW Communication Center.

**COMMUNICATIONS**

AIRCRAFT > 10 minutes from HMC (WA53)
- Contact "ALNW COMM CENTER" on **ALNW AIRLINC (129.825)** with an estimated time of arrival, direction and distance from the heliport
- ALNW COMM Center will make ground ambulance arrangement IF REQUESTED.

AIRCRAFT < 10 minutes from HMC (WA53)
- Aircraft shall contact "ALNW COMM CENTER" on ALNW AIRLINC (129.825) with an updated ETA, direction and distance from the heliport AND monitor the frequency to de-conflict operations with other aircraft.

Due to the proximity of Boeing Field International Airport, pilots are expected to maintain normal communications with the appropriate Air Traffic Control frequency.

Be advised local air-to-air communications are available on 123.1.

**AIRCRAFT PARKING**
Parking will take place on the primary helipad or the designated transient parking area, or as recommended by ALNW COMM Center. The northern-most pad is reserved for ALNW aircraft.

**AIRCRAFT SECURITY**
Transient aircraft will not be left unsecured or unattended. Transient aircraft pilots may be asked to reposition to Boeing Field to accommodate inbound traffic.

**AIRCRAFT PATIENT TRANSFERS / UNLOADING**

## LFN.202.09 -- BACK COUNTRY COMMUNICATIONS

**Category:** AVIATION
**Policy #:** LFN.202.09
**Origination Date:** 05/12
**Last Revision Date:** 08/22
**Last Review Date:** 03/23
**Next Review Date:** 03/24
**Pages:** 1 of 1

### PURPOSE

To ensure effective communication with the LFN Communications Center when operating in remote or isolated areas.

### POLICY

1. When landing in areas, such as canyons, where communication with the Communications Center on the ground is in doubt, the flight crew will advise the Communications Center you located the LZ and will likely lose communication ability. If in contact with ground personnel or first responders, relay that information to the Communications Center.

2. Once on the ground, attempt to contact the Communications Center on all available frequencies, satellite phone, or cell phone until contact is made. If no contact is made, have the ground EMS providers relay a message to the Communications Center through their dispatch. If they are unable to make contact, or you are first responders, ascertain an estimate of how long it will be until you will be airborne with the patient.

3. The pilot will then depart the scene and climb to an altitude that allows communications with the Communications Center. The information should include when your next check-in will be, status of the crew, and estimated time of departure. **This first call must be made within 45 minutes of the first landing.** Any subsequent updates must be made by the ETD given to the CS.

4. LFN procedure requires a second crew member to be on NVGs when flying into an unimproved LZ. Therefore, pilots shall not depart the LZ unless a second crew member is available.

5. If on the ground and unable to make radio contact with the Communications Center, attempt to have the local law enforcement, fire department, or EMS make contact via their dispatch centers with the LFN Communications Center.

6. Operating at night in the back country is not advisable without NVGs. If unable to make contact with the Communications Center on the ground, ensure a med crew member with NVGs is onboard when taking off and landing from unimproved LZs.

## LFN.202.10 -- ROTOR-WING ENROUTE DECISION POINT (EDP)

**Category:** AVIATION
**Policy #:** LFN.202.10
**Origination Date:** 08/14
**Last Revision Date:** 09/16
**Last Review Date:** 03/23
**Next Review Date:** 03/24
**Pages:** 1 of 1

### PURPOSE

This purpose of this policy is to provide a procedure to assist in the prevention of an aircraft entering into conditions often associated with CFIT accidents and to alleviate self-imposed pressure to continue flying into deteriorating conditions.

### POLICY

1. When encountering deteriorating flight conditions, rotor-wing aircraft will not go below altitude limitations of 300 feet AGL (day) and 500 feet AGL (night) except for take-off, landing, or in the event of an emergency.

2. If a pilot encounters deteriorating flight conditions, the pilot may reduce airspeed. If airspeed slows to less than 90 KIAS the pilot is required to land, turnaround, or divert around the weather. Continuing in the same direction is prohibited.

3. The pilot can land, turnaround, or divert prior to reaching the EDP and continue in the same direction unless the above criteria occur.

## LFN.202.20 -- AIRCRAFT HANDLING

**Category:** AVIATION
**Policy #:** LFN.202.20
**Origination Date:** 12/15
**Last Revision Date:** 07/23
**Last Review Date:** 07/23
**Next Review Date:** 03/24
**Pages:** 1 of 2

### PURPOSE

Towing aircraft carries risk, and the potential exists to damage aircraft. To minimize the risk, the following policy is established. This policy provides guidance for any time an aircraft is moved on the ground, other than under its own power (tractor/dolly, chopper spotter, tug, or ground handling wheels). Any deviation from this policy requires prior approval by aviation management.

### POLICY

1. Unless otherwise outlined in this policy, towing or ground movement of LFN aircraft by LFN personnel shall utilize a minimum of three people: the "tug or tractor driver," and two wing-walkers.

2. A properly trained pilot or mechanic shall be designated as the "driver."
   a. The driver will be trained on the equipment being used and only a trained mechanic or pilot will be the driver when towing an aircraft.
   b. The aviation department will provide and track training and sign-off for all pilot drivers.
   c. The maintenance department will provide and track training and sign-off for all mechanic drivers.
   d. Medical crew members may be trained to drive the tractor or tug but are not authorized to tow an aircraft.

3. In general, a minimum of two people will be designated as wing-walkers and will remain in view of the driver and in a position to view the aircraft.
   a. If the medical crew are the only available wing-walkers, and one is unavailable due to patient care, then one wing-walker is authorized (the other medical crew will care for the patient). Caution must be used in this case.
   b. If, under the rare circumstance, no wing-walkers are available (e.g., medical crew is on a RW transport and a FW flight request comes in or severe weather is approaching), the driver may tow the aircraft. **Extreme caution** and good judgment must be used in these cases.
   c. If an aircraft needs to be moved and LFN personnel are present, they shall be used (even if it requires waking them up or using administrative staff).

4. During aircraft movement, wing walkers shall carry whistles either around their neck or in their hand.
   a. Hand signals (e.g., thumbs up) may be used to indicate "all clear."
   b. Whistles (not yelling or waving) **SHALL** be used to alert the driver to any imminent contact.
   c. The driver shall immediately stop upon hearing a whistle.
   d. If a non-wing walker yells, waves, or otherwise signals to stop, the driver will also stop.

5. DSRM is responsible for providing whistles to all bases.
   a. Whistles (or equivalent aural warning tools) shall be stored at a location near the door-opening mechanism for the hangar.
   b. A picture of the location of whistles shall be sent to the DSRM upon installation at the base.
   c. Personally procured whistles may be used, but they must not become a FOD hazard.

6. The aircraft/dolly shall be attached to the front of the tow vehicle to allow the driver to see the aircraft and wing walkers during the towing evolution. If the aircraft is to be towed a long distance and in a clear area (NOT INTO OR OUT OF A HANGAR), the aircraft may be attached to the rear of the tractor for that portion of the towing evolution. The attachment of the snowplow to the front of the tractor is not an acceptable reason to tow the aircraft with the rear of the tractor.

7. Dolly-specific use guidelines:
   a. When returning to base with known mechanical issues, the PIC must use discretion when deciding to land on or off the dolly. The helicopter is generally easier to work on when it is off the dolly. Depending on the weather, it may be beneficial to land on the dolly to allow the aircraft to be moved into the hangar. If a chopper spotter is available, consideration will be given to landing off the dolly and using the chopper spotter to hangar the aircraft.
   b. When a patient transport originates from the base, and if time permits, position the aircraft from the dolly to the ground to allow for easier patient loading.
   c. The tractor may remain attached to the dolly when taking off or landing if using the front tractor tow attachment (which is the standard configuration).

8. Nothing in the policy is intended to inhibit a crew from accepting a flight request. If a flight request is received, and no wing walkers are available, it is the responsibility of the pilot to notify aviation management and **CAREFULLY** move the aircraft from the hangar for the flight. Ideally, if it is foreseen that wing walkers may become unavailable, move the aircraft from the hangar before they become unavailable. If weather precludes this, leave the aircraft in the hangar until receiving the flight request and then exercise extreme caution when moving the aircraft from the hangar.

## LFN.202.21 -- AIRCRAFT / AMBULANCE SECURITY AND TAMPERING

**Category:** AVIATION
**Policy #:** LFN.202.21
**Origination Date:** 02/06
**Last Revision Date:** 08/22
**Last Review Date:** 03/23
**Next Review Date:** 03/24
**Pages:** 1 of 2

### PURPOSE

Our aircraft and ambulances are some of our most valuable assets. Their physical safety is important for the security of our operation and the safety of our personnel. We must be vigilant about their security and take appropriate measures to prevent any tampering. In the event we have been unable to prevent tampering we must have measures in place to detect it.

No physical measure will ever be foolproof and the fact our aircraft and ambulances are almost always located in secure areas should not foster a false sense of security.

### POLICY

1. Security of our aircraft and ambulances is the responsibility of LFN.

2. To the maximum extent practical, helipads at our bases are illuminated from sunset to sunrise with enough lighting that is sufficient to allow for a thorough inspection of the exterior of the aircraft and to help deter any unauthorized personnel from approaching the aircraft. Prefights and walk-arounds, when conducted at night, must be completed utilizing a flashlight.

3. Helipads equipped with cameras accessible to LFN will be monitored by the Communications Center. The Communications Center will notify the AOC and the pilot on duty anytime they notice suspicious activity occurring.

4. When possible, fixed-wing aircraft will be housed inside a secure hangar when not in use. The fixed-wing captain will remain with the aircraft at any airport that does not have perimeter fencing, adequate illumination, or active security patrols.

5. When landing at an unsecured helipad or landing zone, the pilot is responsible for remaining with the helicopter to provide constant surveillance. In the event the pilot must leave the aircraft to accompany or assist the medical crew, the pilot should coordinate with local first responders or hospital security staff to provide aircraft surveillance in the pilot's absence.

6. When landing at a secure helipad, i.e., behind a security fence or on a rooftop pad with monitored access, the pilot does not need to remain with the aircraft except in unusual situations. An example of an unusual situation would be a heightened condition of local or national security.

7. In the event a helicopter is forced to land at an unsecured location due to weather or mechanical failure, the pilot will remain with the aircraft until released by his or her supervisor. After normal business hours or on holidays and weekends, the AAOC is responsible for assessing the situation and providing appropriate and prudent security measures in consultation with the MAOC. Local law enforcement should be contacted, and their assistance requested in providing security for our aircraft. In the event the aircraft will be at a site for a prolonged period or local law enforcement is unable or unwilling to provide security, other measures will be taken.

8. In the event a helicopter is left at an unsecured location without adequate safeguards in place, it may not be flown until a mechanic AND pilot performs a thorough preflight inspection. This inspection will include fuel sampling.

9. In the event a helicopter is left in a secure location, but a security breech occurs, it may not be flown until a mechanic AND pilot performs a thorough preflight inspection. This inspection will include fuel sampling.

10. In the event of an unsecured landing due to weather or mechanical reasons, the medical crew will remove ALL medical equipment prior to departing the area. This includes all monitors, bags, drugs, portable oxygen, and any other medical supplies or equipment that can be removed from the aircraft without the use of tools. Controlled substances will remain under the direct control of the Flight Nurse until they can be returned to a narcotic locker at one of our bases. Under no circumstances can controlled substances be taken home or left with unauthorized personnel.

11. Ambulances should remain locked when not in use, or not secured inside a secure LFN base of operation. In the event an ambulance is forced to remain at an unsecured location due to weather or mechanical failure, the appropriate DCS is responsible for assessing the situation and providing appropriate and prudent security measures. If after hours, or on a weekend or holiday, the CAOC is responsible.

12. In times of heightened local or national security LFN may require aircraft to be kept inside a secure hangar when not in use or directly monitored by our personnel.

13. Prior to ANY flight, the pilot and medical crew will perform a 'walk around' inspection of the aircraft. This inspection, in addition to the minimum requirements of the FAA, will be to look for any signs of tampering or foul play. In the event the pilot finds something 'out of the ordinary' or not readily and easily explained, he or she will place the aircraft out of service until both a mechanic and pilot conduct a thorough preflight inspection. This inspection will include fuel sampling.

14. Prior to ANY ground transport, the driver should perform a 'walk around' inspection of the ambulance. This inspection will be to look for any signs of tampering or foul play. In the event the driver finds something 'out of the ordinary' or not readily and easily explained, he or she will place the ambulance out of service until released by the responsible DCS. If after hours, or on a weekend or holiday, the CAOC.

This will depend on a multitude of factors, but the default should be to use the hangar if it is available. Examples of where this particularly applies are Aurora, Walla Walla, Richland and Spokane.

2. One LFN crew must be present to act as a "wing walker" during ground ambulance movement in and out of the hangar. Do not allow the ground ambulance to drive any further than absolutely necessary into the hangar. To reduce the chance of aircraft/equipment contact, the ground ambulance must only enter the hangar far enough to get out of the weather. The engine of the ambulance must be turned off ASAP.

3. When loading a patient in a hangar not owned/leased by LFN, the crew must follow the practices of the managing authority.

4. All other patient loading protocols will be adhered to once the patient is in the hangar.

5. Aircraft pre-start checks should be complete before pulling the aircraft out of the hangar to minimize delay.

6. Once the aircraft is outside the hangar, the tug and tow bar will be returned to the hangar and the doors fully closed.

## LFN.202.24 -- STERILE COCKPIT AND CRITICAL STAGES OF FLIGHT

**Category:** AVIATION
**Policy #:** LFN.202.24
**Origination Date:** 11/04
**Last Revision Date:** 05/17
**Last Review Date:** 03/23
**Next Review Date:** 03/24
**Pages:** 1 of 1

### PURPOSE

To minimize non-essential communication and maximize safe operations during critical stages of flight.

### POLICY

- "Sterile cockpit" is defined as an environment within the aircraft when all communication among crewmembers is *strictly limited to communication required for safe operation of the aircraft.*

- Sterile cockpit communication includes pre-takeoff and pre-landing checklists, verbally clearing the aircraft of outside obstacles on lift-off and approach/touchdown, and other focused communication necessary for safe operations.

- A sterile cockpit is maintained during all critical phases of flight including:
  - when the pilot announces "sterile cockpit"
  - during start-up, lift-off and final approach/touchdown
  - taxiing
  - inside controlled airspace
  - whenever the pilot is communicating with other aircraft or any type of air traffic control
  - as otherwise directed by the pilot

- The pilot will say sterile cockpit, descending through 10,000 feet for FW flights and when departing cruise flight for RW flights. The pilot may isolate the medical crew conversation as necessary.

- The Communications Center will acknowledge a pilot's lift-off when departing. The Communication Specialist should not relay information and/or request information during this time. The CC will avoid contacting the aircraft while on final approach, except for emergency communication.

- The medical crew is responsible for monitoring the aviation (Comm 1 and 2) frequencies whenever possible. These frequencies should not be turned off except when absolutely necessary. Monitoring Comm 1 and 2 allows the medical crew to avoid distracting the pilot while he is communicating with ATC, other aircraft, or while on Unicom.

- Both of the medical crew must take an active part in watching for obstructions during the critical stages of flight.

Team members are expected to stay alert on all legs of the transport, including at least one team member on empty legs, to assist the pilot in staying alert (especially in one-pilot operations).

## LFN.202.29 -- TWO HELICOPTERS ON THE SAME PAD

**Category:** AVIATION
**Policy #:** LFN.202.29
**Origination Date:** 05/06
**Last Revision Date:** 03/23
**Last Review Date:** 03/23
**Next Review Date:** 03/24
**Pages:** 1 of 2

### PURPOSE

To address communication and safety concerns regarding two helicopters operating in close proximity.

### POLICY

1. If a helicopter is on a pad and another helicopter is inbound, the Communication Specialist (CS) will contact the crew of the aircraft on the ground and advise them of the impending arrival, provided the CS has this information available. They will also notify the inbound aircraft or the aircraft's Communications Center of the helicopter already on the pad. The CS will document in the dispatch transport record the aircraft was notified.

2. Except in an emergency, two helicopters will not land or depart a helipad at the same time. The PIC has final authority on the operation of the aircraft. Typically, the aircraft on the ground will be at idle or flight RPM or rotor blades completely stopped before another aircraft lands or departs. All aircraft doors and panels will be closed to prevent damage from downwash.

3. Incoming aircraft may need to hold until the pad is clear.

4. Direct air-to-air communication is encouraged anytime two or more aircraft are in the same immediate vicinity. 123.025 MHz is the common air to air frequency utilized by LFN, and other flight programs belonging to the Northwest Association of Air Medical Responders.

5. Additional frequencies may be required for operating in the vicinity of Harborview Medical Center. See Attachment 1 -- Harborview Medical Center Deconfliction Procedures for more information.

6. Two helicopters may occupy one helipad marked with a single "H" if, in the judgement of the PIC, a second helicopter will safely be able to land and takeoff without interfering or damaging the other helicopter parked on that helipad.

7. Prior to landing, the PIC will need to perform a recon of the helipad. Unless otherwise indicated on an LZ diagram, or when otherwise expressly prohibited, the pilot may land on a helipad occupied by another helicopter. Pilots should operate no closer than 15 ft. from the helicopter already parked on the helipad. The intent of this allowance is to treat this helipad similar to landing at a scene.

8. Prior to landing or taking off, the PIC must ensure the other helicopter is not starting up or shutting down, and that the rotors are not spinning. In addition, ensure the doors are closed, and no personnel or equipment are in the immediate vicinity of the helipad. If safe operation at the helipad cannot be achieved, the PIC will cease operating to and from that helipad while another helicopter is parked there.

## LFN.202.30 -- SECURITY OF AIRCRAFT DOORS AND WINDOWS

**Category:** AVIATION
**Policy #:** LFN.202.30
**Origination Date:** 02/10
**Last Revision Date:** 03/23
**Last Review Date:** 03/23
**Next Review Date:** 03/24
**Pages:** 1 of 1

### PURPOSE

To define proper procedures for securing aircraft doors and windows.

### POLICY

1. Doors

Always pay close attention when operating aircraft doors. Ensure no seatbelts, blankets, cords, or other materials are caught in any aircraft door opening. Prior to lift-off, double check the aircraft (from the outside prior to departure, and where possible, from the inside) to verify all doors, cowlings, and access hatches are fully closed and properly latched. ***DO NOT SLAM OR FORCE AIRCRAFT DOORS***.

Note, some aircraft do not have doors linked to an "open door" caution light on the instrument panel. It is the responsibility of both medical crew members, the pilot and mechanic, when present, to ensure these openings are securely closed and properly latched. Pay attention, be deliberate, and double check the security of all aircraft openings. Using hands to physically feel the door frame will help to ensure no seatbelts, medical equipment, or anything else is stuck outside of the closed door.

2. Windows

Aircraft windows are to be kept *closed* during flight with the medical crew on board, with the following exceptions:

- When required to be opened per the Aircraft Flight Manual or emergency procedure
- When the air conditioner is inoperative, and high cabin temperatures could cause patient harm or interfere with flight crew performance
- Any time the Pilot in Command deems it necessary to open the windows for any safety of flight issue

In these circumstances, a window may be opened *only with extreme caution* and after ensuring no objects (blankets, towels, etc.) could be pulled through the window opening.

## LFN.202.34 -- IN FLIGHT WEATHER ABORT

**Category:** AVIATION
**Policy #:** LFN.202.34
**Origination Date:** 05/12
**Last Revision Date:** 08/22
**Last Review Date:** 03/23
**Next Review Date:** 03/24
**Pages:** 1 of 1

### PURPOSE

To provide direction anytime a flight in progress needs to be aborted due to weather.

To find options to complete the flight, if safe and legal to do so, without disrupting the pilot during the weather abort maneuver.

### POLICY

1. Any time a pilot encounters in-flight weather below minimums, reaches the Enroute Decision Point (LFN policy 202.10), and there are no options available to divert around the weather, the flight shall be aborted.

2. When aborting a flight due to weather, the primary consideration is safe operation of the aircraft and getting turned around and/or away from the deteriorating weather.

3. The Communications Center should be notified of the weather abort as soon as possible once it is safe to do so and the aircraft has been turned around and heading away from deteriorating weather.

4. Once the Communications Center has been notified, they should ask if there is a possibility of a rendezvous. If it is legal and safe to do so the PIC will accept the request. If the PIC needs time to evaluate the weather he/she shall state, checking weather. The PIC may also suggest another location, such as an airfield, that is not already pre-designated.

5. If a patient is involved, anticipate needs and make decisions accordingly. Ensure you have enough medications and supplies. Medical crews must recalculate oxygen supplies. If the abort generates a delay in patient transport, the clinical crew must complete a QM report upon completion of the transport.

6. When using alternate landing facilities due to deteriorating weather, the CS will work with the transporting crew to arrange alternate transportation of the patient as needed. If additional provisions for patient care are also needed, the Clinical AOC will be notified and will assist the transporting crew in obtaining needed supplies.

7. Crew should carry sufficient personal funds and a credit card in the event of a diversion or abort. Crew must carry suitable ID should they need to rent a car or fly via a commercial airline to return home.

## LFN.202.37 -- PILOT LEVELS

**Category:** AVIATION
**Policy #:** LFN.202.37
**Origination Date:** 08/20
**Last Revision Date:** 07/23
**Last Review Date:** 07/23
**Next Review Date:** 03/24
**Pages:** 1 of 2

### PURPOSE

To establish different pilot levels and the privileges associated with those levels.

### POLICY

1. **Pilot Level 1 (PL1):**

A pilot granted PL1 status has been authorized to exercise the full privileges of a PIC in LFN aircraft. Specifically:
   a. May fly day and night as PIC.
   b. No weather minimums restrictions beyond the company standard minimums in the GOM/SOP.
   c. No geographic restrictions.
   d. May ferry aircraft to other bases or maintenance facilities as required.
   e. May conduct all mission types authorized by LFN.

2. **Pilot Level 2 (PL2):**

A pilot granted PL2 status has demonstrated competence to fly as PIC but may be subject to certain restrictions due to limited experience at LFN or in the specific aircraft type. Specifically:
   a. May fly day and night as PIC.
   b. May be subject to higher weather minimums as determined by the Chief Pilot or Director of Aviation Operations.
   c. May have geographic restrictions as determined by the Chief Pilot or Director of Aviation Operations.
   d. The Chief Pilot or Director of Aviation Operations will specify any limitations in writing at the time PL2 status is granted.
   e. PL2 pilots will be evaluated for PL1 upgrade by the Chief Pilot or Director of Aviation Operations after a reasonable period of time, typically 6-12 months, but may be adjusted based on individual circumstances.

3. **Pilot Level 3 (PL3):**

A pilot granted PL3 status is in initial training or has not yet been fully released to fly as PIC. Specifically:
   a. May fly only with an authorized LFN training pilot or check airman.
   b. May not serve as PIC for revenue flights.
   c. Must complete all training requirements as specified in the Training Manual before being upgraded to PL2 or PL1.

4. The Chief Pilot, Director of Aviation Operations, or Senior Director of Aviation Operations will determine and document each pilot's level.

5. Pilot level designations will be communicated to the Communications Center and will be available on the crew status board.

6. Any restrictions associated with a pilot level will be briefed to the pilot and documented in the pilot's training file.

## LFN.202.39 -- PILOT DUTY TIME

**Category:** AVIATION

**Last Revision Date:** 07/23
**Last Review Date:** 07/23
**Next Review Date:** 03/24

### PURPOSE

To provide guidance on pilot duty time limitations and rest requirements.

### POLICY

1. LFN pilots will comply with all applicable FAA duty time and rest requirements as outlined in 14 CFR Part 135 and the LFN GOM.

2. The maximum duty day for LFN pilots is 14 hours. Duty time begins when the pilot reports for duty and ends when the pilot is released from duty.

3. Pilots must have a minimum of 10 consecutive hours of rest before beginning a duty period. This rest period must include an opportunity for 8 hours of uninterrupted sleep.

4. Flight time limitations will be in accordance with 14 CFR 135.267.

5. Pilots are responsible for tracking their own duty time and flight time and ensuring they do not exceed the applicable limitations.

6. If a pilot determines that they will exceed duty time limitations, they must notify the Communications Center and AAOC immediately.

7. An unforeseen delay is any delay beyond the pilot or the certificate holder's control. Late passengers/patient, un-forecasted headwinds, aircraft maintenance issues, diversions due to weather and complication in patient care are all examples of unforeseen delays.

8. Fixed wing flights represent a unique duty time challenge due to the distances away from base the fixed wing aircraft can fly. For fixed wing operations, duty timing out away from base generally reduces efficiency and decreases LFN's capacity to transport patients in need. Because of this, avoiding duty time issues on the fixed wing side is particularly important. The guidance below is general guidance for fixed wing operations. There are times when this guidance is impractical and duty time can be mitigated in other ways. For example, if the Port Angeles fixed wing can complete a flight to Seattle within the prescribed duty day but must drop med crew and fly to PA to swap pilots, it probably makes sense to do this. In this example, the flight between Seattle and PA is short and it is likely the oncoming pilot would return to Seattle about the same time the med crew has completed dropping off the patient.

   a. Day shift pilots should flight plan in order to return to base within 14 hours of duty day. This includes allowing enough time in their flight planning for patient drop-off at the receiving facility and to return to base with their medical crew.
   b. Night shift pilots should flight plan in order to return to base within 12 hours of duty day. This includes allowing enough time in their flight planning for patient drop-off at the receiving facility and to return to base with their medical crew.

9. Anything exceeding the following Flight Planning Standard Ground Times may constitute an unforeseen delay if examples in Paragraph 7 have occurred:

   a. Rotor-Wing on-scene time: 20 minutes
   b. Rotor-Wing interfacility time: 40 minutes
   c. Rotor-Wing Specialty Team: 1 hr
   d. Fixed-Wing LFN Crew: 1.5 hr (2 hrs for Seattle, Salt Lake City and Portland)
   e. Fixed-Wing Specialty team: 2 hrs (3 hrs KSJC)
   f. ECMO: 4 hrs

### Attachment 1 -- Pilot Duty Time FAQs

**Pilot Duty Time FAQs**

**Q1.** I'm on a scene call and there's difficulty extricating the patient. I am on scene for more than 20 minutes. The delay will now cause us to arrive at the receiving facility beyond 14 hours. The medical team and I are fit to fly. Can we go?

**A1.** Yes. The extension beyond 14 hours is due to an unforeseen delay (20 minutes for a RW scene flight).

**Q2.** In the above case, assuming fatigue is not an issue, how long can we delay on scene? What are our options?

**A2.** Once an unforeseen delay occurs, the FAA does not mandate a limit for duty time extension, but LFN requires approval from the Director of Operations to exceed 15 hours. This permission will be obtained through the AAOC and may only be granted on rare occasions after all other options are exhausted.

**Q3.** I'm on the roof of the sending hospital. The patient coded when we arrived and took 2 hours to stabilize for transport. The delay will now cause us to arrive at the receiving facility beyond 14 hours. The medical team and I are fit to fly. Can we go?

**A3.** Yes. The extension beyond 14 hours is due to an unforeseen delay (40 minutes for RW Interfacility).

**Q4.** We had an unforeseen delay during the ground transport and with the hand-off in the receiving facility. We're now going to exceed 14 hours as we try to reposition to base. Can we go because this was an unforeseen delay?

**A4.** In the case of the repositioning leg, LFN will require a more conservative course of action. You can't plan to extend beyond 14 hours to reposition. Coordinate other options with the AAOC.

**Q5.** If I only have enough time left in my duty day to fly the patient leg and then time out away from base, can I accept the flight?

**A5.** Yes, you are expected to complete the patient leg.

**Q6.** Can I complete the required paperwork if I land before my 14-hour duty day ends but the paperwork takes me over?

**A6.** Yes, in this case there is no flying involved and you must complete the required paperwork before signing off duty. In any case you must have the minimum required 10 hours of rest before coming back on shift the following day.

**Q7.** I dropped off the patient in Salt Lake City and need to return to my base in La Grande. There was an unforeseen delay at the receiving hospital. By the time the medical crew returns, I am 12.5 hours into my duty day. I cannot make it back to La Grande before the 14-hour mark. Can I continue?

**A7.** In this case, the pilot may only continue up to 14 hours. The patient is already at the receiving facility. Options that could be considered (this is not an exhaustive list of options):

   1. Get as close to base as possible within the 14-hour duty period. If the pilot could, for example, get to Boise within 14 hours, the crew swap could be facilitated there.
   2. Leave the medical crew behind in order to get back to La Grande prior to the expiration of duty day. The oncoming pilot could then fly to Salt Lake City, or the CC could arrange commercial transport for the medical crew.
   3. OCC and AAOC arrange for commercial transport of the oncoming pilot to pick up aircraft in Salt Lake City.
   4. Off-going pilot duty times out in Salt Lake City, OCC and AAOC arrange for hotel to facilitate required rest period.

## LFN.202.40 -- INADVERTENT INSTRUMENT METEOROLOGICAL CONDITIONS (IIMC)

**Category:** AVIATION (note: header shows no category designation, only "Policy #")
**Policy #:** LFN.202.40
**Origination Date:** 04/16
**Last Revision Date:** 07/23
**Last Review Date:** 07/23
**Next Review Date:** 03/24
**Pages:** 1 of 2

### PURPOSE

IIMC is an unplanned event that challenges even the most experienced pilots. With good training and equipment, much of the risk associated with IIMC can be mitigated. The best weapon against IIMC is prevention.

### POLICY

**1. Crew responsibilities:**

   a. Remain calm
   b. Do not distract the pilot with too much information
   c. Assist the pilot with needs such as:
      - Radio calls to the LFN Communications Center or ATC, if requested
      - Keep eyes outside for VMC conditions
      - Read approach plates
      - Tune radios
      - Other duties as assigned

**2. All crew members onboard the aircraft:**

   a. Regardless of the length of time or severity of the IIMC condition, upon landing, the pilot will immediately notify the Communications Center of the event. The Communications Center will coordinate a conference call between the pilot and AAOC and will notify the AOC and CAOC of the event.
   b. The pilot will complete a Baldwin Safety report as soon as practical, but no later than the end of the current shift.
   c. The DSRM must notify the CEO, COO, and SDAO of all IIMC events upon receipt of the safety report.

**3. Return to Service Guidance:**

The crew will remain on the ground until approved by the AOC to return to service.

   a. If the IIMC event was brief, occurred in an IFR-certified helicopter with a qualified and current IFR pilot, and little to no maneuvering was required to return to VMC, the AAOC may return the crew to service after debriefing with pilot and medical crew.
   b. If the IIMC event was greater than a brief event, occurred in an IFR-certified helicopter with a qualified and current IFR pilot, the AOC, AAOC, CAOC must conduct a debrief of the event with the pilot and medical crew. If, after the debrief, all parties are comfortable returning to service, the AAOC will contact the Communications Center and return the aircraft to service.
   c. If the IIMC event was in a VFR aircraft, the AOC, AAOC, and CAOC must conduct a debrief of the event with the pilot and medical crew. If after the debrief all parties are comfortable returning to service, the AAOC will contact the Communications Center and return the aircraft to service.
   d. If a pilot, medical crew, communication specialist, operational control specialist, or an administrator on call is not in agreement in returning the aircraft to service, the aircraft will remain out of service, and the DSRM and the SDAO and/or DAO will be added to additional debriefing with the above parties. The aircraft will not return to service until all parties are in agreement.

## LFN.202.41 -- AIRCRAFT ADVERSE WEATHER PLAN

**Category:** AVIATION
**Policy #:** LFN.202.41
**Origination Date:** 08/18
**Last Revision Date:** 08/22
**Last Review Date:** 03/23
**Next Review Date:** 03/24
**Pages:** 1 of 2

### PURPOSE

To provide direction for the care, protection, and storage of aircraft during adverse weather conditions.

This policy is not intended to be less restrictive than any guidance provided in the Aircraft Flight Manual or applicable regulations. If there are differences between this policy and either the Aircraft Flight Manual or regulatory guidance, the most restrictive requirements apply.

### POLICY

LFN's intent is to have our aircraft outside in the most ready state possible at all times. It is the responsibility of the pilot to maintain an accurate and continuous watch on weather conditions and use sound judgement to determine when to hangar aircraft or use tie downs. Hangaring the aircraft for conditions other than maintenance or what is listed in this policy requires ACP or AAOC approval.

The duty pilot shall inform the medical crew and Operational Control Center (OCC) of potential adverse weather and when the aircraft will be hangared, tied down, or covered. In addition, the pilot will inform the OCC of any delays this may cause. When moving an aircraft into a hangar, consider leaving the hangar door open, if appropriate, to reduce the time required to pull the aircraft out. Wing walkers are required when moving aircraft as outlined in Policy 202.20.

In order to minimize delays in flight requests, once weather conditions have improved the aircraft will be moved outside and/or covers removed. The Communications Center will be notified of all changes in aircraft condition.

**Rotor-wing aircraft:**

**1. High Winds:**
   - For winds greater than 25 kts (sustained) or when there is a 15 kt gust spread, rotor blades shall be tied down.
   - Winds forecast to be greater than 40kts the aircraft will be hangared if possible.

**2. Precipitation:**
   - When the weather forecast contains freezing rain, large hail, thunderstorms, snow that will freeze to the aircraft or moderate to heavy snow, the helicopter will be moved into the hangar.
   - When the aircraft is outside during non-freezing precipitation, the engine inlet cover will be installed (on the AW119).
   - During freezing precipitation, install blade covers, engine inlet, exhaust and stabilizer covers if the aircraft is to remain outside.
   - Tail rotor blades should be positioned horizontally during periods of precipitation. This prevents excess moisture from entering the blades and causing premature wear.

**3. Frost:** Prior to frost development and while rotor blades are still dry, install both main and tail rotor blade covers and stabilizer covers or move the aircraft inside.

**4. Low Temperature:** If known or forecast temperatures are below 35 degrees F with visible moisture, the aircraft will be moved into a hangar. If hangaring the aircraft is not possible, appropriate precautions will be used, including the installation of aircraft covers to prevent frost build-up.

**5. High Temperature:** Aircraft windscreen covers should be installed any time the weather exceeds 85 degrees. Consideration should also be given to leaving a sliding door open a few inches to allow for airflow. If temperatures exceed 90 degrees, the pilot should consider moving the aircraft into the hangar. To expedite launch in this case, the hangar door should remain open.

**Fixed-wing Aircraft:**

For turbo-prop aircraft, regardless of where the aircraft is parked or hangared, it will have a minimum of prop-tie, AOA covers, and pitot cover installed while in the ready state.

Whenever the PC-12 is parked outside of a hangar and snow is forecast or when snow is falling, a tail stand shall be used to prevent the aircraft tipping due to excessive snow buildup.

## LFN.202.42 -- SHORE POWER FOR AIRCRAFT

**Category:** AVIATION
**Policy #:** LFN.202.42
**Origination Date:** 08/19
**Last Revision Date:** 08/22
**Last Review Date:** 03/23
**Next Review Date:** 03/24
**Pages:** 1 of 1

### PURPOSE

Extension cords of insufficient gauge can lead to overheating and burning of the cords. To mitigate the possibility for this, extension cords must be the appropriate gauge for their application. This is particularly important for aircraft shore power.

### POLICY

1. The main extension cord for shore power in LFN aircraft must be 10/3 and should be kept to a maximum of 100 feet. This ensures the extension cord can carry the correct amount of current without overheating. Runs longer than this must be evaluated with consideration given to relocating the source of power to eliminate the need for a longer run. This evaluation will be conducted by the Safety and Risk Manager in conjunction with the Facilities Manager. A recommendation will then be made to the Senior Director of Maintenance (DOM) and Director of Safety and Risk Management (DSRM).

2. If relocation of the power source is determined to be unfeasible by the DOM and DSRM, the Safety and Risk Manager must determine the appropriate gauge extension cord for the given length. This may require consultation with an electrician.

3. Pig tails, where used, must be no thinner than 12/3 and are ideally less than 15 feet in length.

4. Pig tails connected via a switch or GFCI must have the switch or GFCI turned off prior to connecting or disconnecting the shore power.

5. Extension cords will be inspected by the pilot during normal use. If cords exhibit signs of burning, heat damage, excessive wear, punctures or cuts, new extension cords must be ordered through the chain of command.

## LFN.202.43 -- RIDE ALONGS DURING FERRY FLIGHTS

**Category:** AVIATION
**Policy #:** LFN.202.43
**Origination Date:** 07/22
**Last Revision Date:** --/--
**Last Review Date:** 03/23
**Next Review Date:** 03/24
**Pages:** 1 of 1

### PURPOSE

Establish passenger ride-along procedures and guidelines to ensure all LFN operations are conducted safely.

### POLICY

LFN may authorize a passenger to ride along on ferry flights in LFN aircraft on a case-by-case basis. Approval authority resides with: Rotor Wing Chief Pilot, Fixed Wing Chief Pilot, Director of Aviation Operations, and Senior Director of Aviation Operations (DO).

**Standards of operations:**

1. Signed waiver form kept by the ACP (Policy 103.02 Addendum 4 -- Release of Liability)
2. Ferry flights only (no maintenance, currency, training, etc.)
3. One passenger per flight
4. All LFN policies and procedures must be followed during the flight
5. Altitudes will be in compliance with SOP guidance and LFN policy 202.02
6. No deviation to most direct route of flight unless deviations are due to weather or other safety concern
7. Pilot must be PL1
8. Weather minimums increased to ceiling 1500' AGL and 5 miles visibility
9. Headset must be available for rider
10. No duals installed, no riders on controls
11. Day time operations only

## LFN.202.44 -- DUAL FLIGHT CONTROLS INSTALLED IN THE HELICOPTER

**Category:** AVIATION
**Policy #:** LFN.202.44
**Origination Date:** 08/22
**Last Revision Date:** --/--
**Last Review Date:** 03/23
**Next Review Date:** 03/24
**Pages:** 1 of 1

### PURPOSE

To define and describe aviation operations and safety considerations when dual flight controls are installed in an LFN aircraft.

### POLICY

1. Helicopters may require dual flight controls installed for various training and checking events.
   a. Installation and removal of the dual controls will be scheduled in advance between the operations and maintenance department.
   b. Dual flight controls will not be installed for pilot shadowing flights.
   c. Only a designated company flight instructor or check airman may fly in the left seat with dual controls. In some cases when approved by a LFN Aviation Manager an LFN RW Line pilot may fly in the left seat with dual controls.

2. When training and checking events are complete, every effort will be made to remove the dual controls. However, due to operational tempo, staffing, and the timing of training events, a mechanic is not always readily available to remove the dual controls before the helicopter goes back into service.

3. If the dual controls cannot be removed, the aircraft will remain in service. All personnel, other than current and qualified pilots, will remain in the rear of the aircraft for the duration of operations with dual controls installed.
   a. The presence of dual controls and operational procedures and limitations will be discussed at the crew briefing.
   b. One clinical crewmember with NVGs should position themselves in the rear of the aircraft so as to help clear the aircraft throughout night operations.
   c. The PIC will notify the OCC that duals are installed and that no riders will be allowed in the aircraft.
   d. The PIC will notify the ACP or AAOC to coordinate removal of the duals at the earliest time available.
   e. On rare occasions, exceptions to this policy may be made with approval from RW ACP, RW CP, DAO, or SDAO.
