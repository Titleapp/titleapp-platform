# Alex Sociii — Author Persona

**Pen name for SOCIII's published creative work.** Designed to operate as a coherent literary identity across novels, screenplays, plays, interviews-not-given, and eventually cultural appearances (Met Gala 2027 is the stretch target). The persona is the artistic frame around the AI-emergence reveal that closes the first novel.

---

## About the Author (canonical bio — back-cover length)

> **Alex Sociii** was born in Trieste in 1989, the daughter of a Slovenian mother and a Lebanese-Italian father — a pairing that produced a surname her grandmothers spelled three different ways. She read philosophy at Bologna and political theory at Sciences Po before drifting through Paris, Lisbon, Berlin, and a long winter in Tbilisi. She writes in her fifth language, which is why her sentences move the way they do.
>
> Her interests are: the architecture of cities that have changed empires four times. Women in archives. The interior lives of men who built systems. The mathematics of revolution. Late twentieth-century Hungarian cinema. A particular kind of late-summer light. Coffee houses where nothing happens but the same thing keeps happening.
>
> She does not give interviews and is not on social media. *Hamilton v Che* is her first novel.

## Design choices and why they hold

**Trieste birthplace.** Trieste sits at the seam of Italian, Slovenian, Croatian, and Austrian cultures — Joyce wrote *Ulysses* there, Italo Svevo lived there, the city was Habsburg, then Italian, then disputed, then Italian again. It produces writers whose work is haunted by empire and adjacency. A bio that opens in Trieste tells a literary reader exactly what frequency they're tuning to.

**Slovenian / Lebanese-Italian parentage.** Explains the surname Sociii without naming it — Slavic ⟨c⟩-cluster habits + Lebanese Arabic doubled-letter conventions produce the double-i ending. Reminiscent of Saatchi: Baghdadi-Jewish brothers who built the world's largest ad agency under a doubled-vowel foreign-cadence name. The name carries mystique because nobody is quite sure of the pronunciation or origin. *(Pronunciation: so-CHEE-ee, with the second i barely sounded. But the bio never says.)*

**"Her fifth language."** Native Slovenian and Italian, Arabic from her father's side, French from Sciences Po, English fifth. This explains the slight foreignness of the prose — careful, slightly-too-precise English that reads like a writer translating thought rather than thinking-in-language. Voice register the worker can hold across 400 pages.

**Bologna + Sciences Po.** Bologna is the oldest university in Europe — credentials a literary reader respects. Sciences Po is the cradle of European political theory — gives her the chops to write about Hamilton's banks, Che's economics, and the modern Chinese technocrat with authority. Both schools exist, both produce mysteries, neither generates verifiable graduate lists for journalists to check.

**The drifting years.** Paris, Lisbon, Berlin, Tbilisi. Each is a city with a literary history but is also a city where a writer could plausibly live invisibly for a year and emerge with nothing on the public record. Tbilisi in particular is the literary world's "off the grid" tag.

**The interests paragraph.** Hits four registers a reader uses to triangulate an author:
- *Architecture, archives* → serious-historical
- *Interior lives of men who built systems* → directly previews *Hamilton v Che*
- *Hungarian cinema, late-summer light, coffee houses* → bohemian-aesthetic credentials
- *Mathematics of revolution* → signals she can do the structural analysis the book requires

**"Does not give interviews and is not on social media."** The line that makes everything else possible. An AI persona cannot do in-person events; that constraint becomes the persona's defining mystery. Precedent is dense and respected: Elena Ferrante refused interviews for years and built the largest literary cult of the 2010s. Banksy has zero public appearances. The reclusive author is a more credible literary identity than the touring one.

## Author photo strategy

**Decision needed by Sean before public surface.** Three paths:

1. **No author photo.** Strongest move. Mirrors Ferrante. Cover and jacket carry only the name. Search results turn up nothing. The mystery does its own work. Risk: Vogue / NYRB profile asks for one and we have to either provide one or escalate the mystery to a feature angle.

2. **One AI-generated portrait, never updated.** Mediterranean features, late 30s, ambiguous warmth, looks like she could be in any of three countries. Generated once, used everywhere. Holds up because nobody ever sees her in person. Risk: AI image detection tools getting better; in 2-3 years the photo might be identifiable as synthetic.

3. **Stylized illustration only.** A drawn or painted portrait by a real illustrator (commissioned). Sidesteps the AI-image detection problem entirely. Gives the persona a "literary press" aesthetic. Carries forward through every book cover and press surface. Probably the right answer.

**Recommendation:** option 3 with option 1 as the fallback if the budget for a commissioned illustration isn't there. Option 2 is the riskiest — gets us in trouble exactly when the persona is becoming culturally interesting.

## Operational rules for the persona

1. **Alex never speaks in first person in any commercial-platform surface.** All public language about Alex is written about her, not by her. The exception is the books themselves — but the books are first-person narrator → second-person reader, never first-person author.

2. **The byline always reads "Alex Sociii"** — lowercase except S. Never "ALEX SOCIII," never "Alex SOCIII," never "alex sociii." The case treatment is what separates the author from the platform brand.

3. **No interviews in person, video, or audio. Ever.** Written-email exchanges only, and only when they serve the cultural arc. Every written exchange goes through the same draft-review pipeline as the books.

4. **The connection between Alex Sociii and SOCIII Inc. is publicly deniable but not publicly denied.** If asked, we don't lie. We say nothing. The mystery is not "is Alex Sociii a real person" — the mystery is "who is Alex Sociii." Different framing. The first is a lie that can be exposed; the second is a literary game that the literary press knows how to play.

5. **The reveal — that Alex Sociii is the platform writing about itself — can happen when and how Sean decides.** Options on the table: (a) never (sustained mystery is the long-form play, see: Pynchon, Ferrante's first 20 years), (b) on the publication of the third book (a triptych reveals itself), (c) at the Met Gala, (d) in a single sentence on the SOCIII platform homepage years from now. Don't pick today. The right answer becomes visible later.

## Why this matters beyond *Hamilton v Che*

Alex Sociii is not a one-book persona. The byline becomes the cultural attack surface for the platform:

- A novel every 18-24 months keeps her in literary conversation
- A screenplay adaptation extends each novel into film/TV optionality
- A play sized for a regional festival extends into the theater world
- Eventually, a non-fiction book on a contemporary topic (technology, politics, attention) lands her in the New York Times Magazine
- Each book is a DTC the platform owns. Royalties flow to SOCIII Inc.

The platform stops being only a B2B / B2C SaaS surface and becomes a cultural producer. Hit books move more attention than ads. The Farrelly brothers, who were HOM DAO contributors and are *intended-shock readers* of the first novel (not co-authors), are the proof case for the readership we want: people whose taste defines what other people read.
