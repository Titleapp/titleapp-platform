# Chapter 1 — The Island

The island in those years smelled of two things, and a boy growing up on it learned the smells before he learned the names of them. The first smell was the smell of cane in the boiling-house, which carried for half a mile in any direction when the wind was right and which a person who had been raised inside that smell could not, in later life, encounter in any other latitude without something in his chest answering before his mind had translated what it was. The second smell was the smell of the harbor at Christiansted at low tide, which was the smell of fish and tar and brackish water and the slow rot of timbers that had been brought across the Atlantic in ships and would not be brought back, and which mixed, as one walked away from the water and into the streets, with the smell of horses and of the offal in the gutters and of the perfumes the Danish wives wore against the heat, which did not work and were never expected to work and which were applied, every morning, in the way certain rituals are applied: not because they accomplish the thing they purport to accomplish, but because the application is the proof that the people performing it have not yet been beaten by the place they are living in.

He was, at the beginning of the period this chapter is concerned with, eleven years old, or thirteen, depending on which of the two birth years one wished to credit, and he was small for either age. His brother James was older by two years and larger by considerably more than the years would account for, the way brothers in poverty often diverge in size for reasons no doctor of that century could have given a name to, and the two of them lived with their mother in a set of rooms above a store on Company Street in Christiansted, on the small Danish-administered island of St. Croix, which lay about forty miles south of St. Thomas and about a thousand miles south and east of the colonies whose name he would later, by an accident of correspondence, help to write.

His mother's name was Rachel Faucette. She had been born on Nevis, of a French Huguenot father who had come out to the islands to be a doctor and had stayed to be a planter, and an English mother who had come out for the climate and had stayed for reasons that were not given in any letter that survived. Rachel had married, at sixteen, a man named Johann Lavien, who was Danish or German or both and who was, by every account anyone bothered to put on paper, a man of small character and large pretensions, the worst possible combination in a husband in any century but particularly in that one. He had beaten her. She had left him. She had taken with her, in the leaving, the only currency a woman of her class and her century could carry into the next chapter of her life, which was her body, and she had given it, in the next chapter, to a Scotsman named James Hamilton, the fourth son of a fourth son of a small laird in Ayrshire, who had come out to the islands to make a fortune and had not made one and would not make one, and who lived with Rachel for fifteen years and gave her two sons and left her, in 1765 or thereabouts, in the way men of his class and his disappointments tended to leave: not in a single moment of decision but in a slow withdrawal of presence, a series of departures on business that became one departure that did not end.

The boy did not, in those years, hate his father. He would not, in any later year of his life, write down a hatred of his father. He would write to his father. He would, when he had a little money and his father had less, send what he could. He would, in his last letters before the morning at Weehawken, name his father with the formality reserved for men whose absence one has decided, by an act of will renewed every morning, to forgive. But the absence was the central fact of his childhood, and the absence operated on him the way the climate operated on the buildings: not all at once, in a hurricane, but every day, in the small constant pressure of heat and salt and damp, which over years took the paint off the wood and bent the boards and rusted the nails and gave the houses the look of having been built by people who had then forgotten about them and gone elsewhere.

His mother kept them alive. This needs to be said clearly because the records on her are thin and the records that exist were written, mostly, by men who had business reasons to disparage her or family reasons to forget her, and the picture that descends out of the records is a picture of a woman who lived loosely and died young and left two boys to fend for themselves on an island where two boys, undefended, could not be expected to amount to very much. The picture is not entirely false. It is also not the picture the boys would have drawn if anyone had ever asked them, which nobody did. She kept a small store. She rented out a portion of the rooms above it. She sold provisions on credit to sailors and to small planters and to the wives of the Danish officials, and she kept the books for the store herself, which was unusual for a woman in that town in that decade and which would matter, the way certain small inheritances matter, in the life of the smaller of her two sons, who watched her keep the books and learned, before he had learned to spell his own name reliably, that a column of numbers was a kind of language and that the language was the language in which the people who ran the town spoke to each other when they were not pretending to be speaking about anything else.

She read to them. This also needs to be said. She had brought with her, into the rooms above the store, a small library of perhaps thirty-four books, which was, on St. Croix in 1766, a library of unusual size for a woman of her station, and which included Plutarch and Pope and a volume of Machiavelli and a French translation of something by Cicero and several novels which had been the books of her girlhood and which she had not, in all the displacements of her life, consented to leave behind. She read them aloud in the evenings. The smaller boy listened. He listened in the way certain children listen, which is not the way most children listen. He listened with the door of his attention all the way open, in the manner of a person who has understood, very early, that the words coming in through that door are the only material out of which the future he intends for himself can be built.

It is necessary, before going further with the boy, to say something about the island itself, because the island was not a background and cannot be treated as one. It was the condition under which everything else in his early life took place, and a writer who treats it as scenery has already misunderstood what it did to him.

St. Croix in those years produced sugar. That is what it produced. It produced sugar at a scale and an intensity that would, by the standards of any later century, be difficult to describe without resorting to the vocabulary of industrial atrocity. Sugar was made from cane. Cane was cut by hand. The hand belonged, in nineteen out of every twenty cases on the island, to a person who had been born in West Africa or whose parents or grandparents had been born in West Africa and who had been brought across the Atlantic in a ship and sold on a wharf, the same wharf at which the smaller boy's mother had once landed when she came across from Nevis, and the person had been sold to a planter and the planter had set the person to work in the cane, and the work in the cane killed people on a timetable so reliable that the planters had, by the 1760s, learned to incorporate the timetable into their financial projections, the way an accountant incorporates an actuarial table into a calculation of insurance.

The boy worked, from an early age, as a clerk in a counting house. This means that he sat on a stool, behind a counter, in a small room with two windows that looked out onto the harbor, and he wrote in a ledger the numbers and names that were dictated to him by the older clerks above him, who had been dictated them by the younger partners, who had been dictated them by the senior partners, who had received them by the inbound packet from New York or Philadelphia or Bristol. The numbers were the numbers of barrels of sugar and of molasses and of rum, and of sacks of flour and of salt cod and of cornmeal, and the names were the names of ships and of captains and of consignees, and occasionally, in a separate ledger which the boy did not at first understand was a separate ledger, the names were the names of human beings, and the numbers next to those names were the prices at which the human beings had been bought or sold or hired out for the cane season. He understood, after the first six months, that the second ledger was a separate ledger. He understood, after the first year, why it was separate. He did not write about that understanding, in any letter that survived, until very late in his life and even then only obliquely, in language that did not quite name what he had understood, although a careful reader of the late letters can find the place where the understanding sits inside the sentences, the way a stone sits under water.

He saw the wharf. He saw the wharf many times. He walked past it on his way to the counting house in the morning and he walked past it on his way back to the rooms above the store in the evening, and on the days when a ship came in he saw, from his stool at the counter through the two windows that looked out onto the harbor, the unloading of the ship's hold, which was a thing that, if it had not been arranged in a way that no person at the windows of any counting house in Christiansted ever permitted himself to call by its name, would have been the central fact of every day of every year of every life lived on that island in those decades.

A person was unloaded the way a barrel was unloaded. That is the thing that has to be said. The person was thinner than a barrel and walked, where a barrel did not walk, but the unloading was the same unloading and the wharf was the same wharf and the men at the wharf moved with the same incurious efficiency with which men in any port of the eighteenth-century Atlantic moved freight. The boy at the window saw it. He saw it every time a ship came in. He saw it as a child sees a thing he has not yet been given the words for, which is the only way a child in his position could have seen it, because the words he had been given by the people around him were not words for the thing he was looking at. The words he had been given were words for cargo and for inventory and for the loss reasonably expected during transit. The thing he was looking at was something else. The gap between the words and the thing was the first thing on the island that taught him that language was a tool and that the tool could be turned in either of two directions, and he did not, in those years, know yet which of the two directions he would turn it in, but he knew, in the way certain children know certain things before they have any vocabulary to test the knowledge against, that the turning of the tool would be the work of his life.

◊

He was not, in those years, alone in the rooms above the store. He had his mother and his brother and an older cousin who came and went and whose name was Peter Lytton, and there were, in the household and around the edges of the household, women who worked for his mother in the running of the store and in the keeping of the rooms, and one of those women was a girl perhaps three or four years older than he was whose name in the records that survive is not given.

He called her Ajei. That was not her birth name. Her birth name was a name in a language that had crossed the Atlantic in the hold of a ship when she was four years old and which had not survived the crossing in the mouths of the people who bought her on the Christiansted wharf, and the name she answered to in his mother's store was a Danish-sounding diminutive that the previous owner had given her and that nobody had ever consulted her about. He called her Ajei because, one afternoon when he was eight or nine and she was eleven or twelve, she had told him, very quietly, in the back room of the store while the two of them were stacking sacks of flour against the wall, that her grandmother had called her something that sounded like Ajei, although the sound she made when she said it was not exactly Ajei, and he had not been able, with the equipment in his own mouth, to repeat the sound back to her precisely, and they had agreed, in the way two children agree on a secret, that Ajei would be near enough, and that nobody else would be told.

She was the property of his mother. This needs to be said in the same plain voice with which the wharf has to be said, because to soften it is to participate in the softening. Rachel Faucette had inherited her from a Faucette uncle on Nevis, along with two other women whose names will not figure in this chapter because the boy did not, at the age this chapter is concerned with, particularly know them. Ajei worked in the store and in the rooms. She fetched water from the cistern in the courtyard. She carried laundry to the line on the flat roof of the building next door, where the heat dried the cotton in an hour and bleached the linen in an afternoon. She slept on a pallet in the back room of the store and not in the rooms above with the family, and the distinction between the back room of the store and the rooms above was the central architectural fact of her life on the island, and the boy understood it without anyone having explained it to him, which was the way most of the important facts about that island were communicated to the children who grew up inside it.

He talked to her. He talked to her more than he talked to anyone else in the household, including his brother, who was larger and louder and less interested in talk, and including his mother, who was tired in the evenings in a way the boy understood not to test. He talked to Ajei in the back room while she folded linens. He talked to her in the courtyard while she drew water. He talked to her, in particular, on the afternoons when the senior clerks at the counting house had let him go home early because there was nothing to do, and he came in through the back of the store and found her at the table behind the counter and sat across from her without taking off his coat and told her, in long sentences that he was already learning to build, what he had read the night before in one of his mother's books.

He told her about Plutarch. He told her about Alexander, the boy-king who had wept at twenty because there were no worlds left to conquer. He told her about Caesar, who had crossed a river and changed an empire. He told her about Cicero, who had written sentences so well constructed that the constructions had outlasted the man and the city and the language. He told her these things in the voice of a boy who had decided, in some chamber of himself he did not yet have access to, that the men in those books were the men he intended to resemble, and the resemblance would be the way he got off the island.

She listened. She listened the way she did everything, which was carefully and without making a noise that would attract any attention from the parts of the household that she had learned, very early, not to attract attention from. She listened with her hands moving over the linen and her eyes mostly on the linen, and every once in a while she looked up and looked at him for a moment longer than she needed to, and looked back down at the linen, and went on folding.

She saw him. This is the thing that has to be said about Ajei, because if it is not said the chapter has failed her, and the chapter will fail her in any case, but it can at least fail her with the failure visible. She saw him in a way that nobody else in the household saw him, including his mother, who loved him, and including his brother, who did not entirely know what to make of him, and including the senior clerks at the counting house, who had begun to suspect that he was unusually quick at the columns of figures and who had not yet decided what to do with the suspicion. Ajei saw him the way a person who has been required, from a very young age, to read the faces of the people who own her body, learns to see other people. She saw the terror in him. She saw the ambition in him. She saw the desperation in him, which the ambition was the surface of and the terror was the foundation of, and she understood, in the part of herself that did not say things out loud, that the boy across the table from her was going to leave the island, and that the leaving was going to be the only thing about him that mattered, and that everything else about him — the talk, the books, the long sentences, the kindness he believed himself to be paying her by sitting at the table and telling her about Alexander — was going to be, in the end, a thing he did in order to make the leaving possible.

She did not tell him any of this. She was eleven, or twelve, or thirteen, and she had been on the island her whole conscious life, and she had been the property of a Faucette and now of a Hamilton, and she had learned the things a girl in her position learned, which included the rule, written nowhere and enforced everywhere, that the seeing-through of a white boy by a Black girl was a kind of seeing that the girl was not permitted to disclose, even to the boy, even in the back room of a store on an afternoon when nobody else was in the building. She kept the seeing inside her face. She kept her face above the linen.

He did not notice. This is the second thing that has to be said about Ajei, and it goes together with the first thing in a way that the rest of his life will go on demonstrating: that the people who saw him most clearly in the early period of his life were the people he was least equipped to recognize as seers, because the seeing came from positions he had been taught, by the structure of the island, not to look toward for that order of knowledge. He looked toward the senior clerks. He looked toward the merchants who came in from New York with their good coats and their watch chains. He looked toward the Danish governor's secretary, whose handwriting he had begun, by the age of twelve or thirteen, to imitate in the privacy of his ledgers, because the handwriting was the handwriting of a man who had been somewhere the boy intended to go. He did not look toward the girl at the table across from him with the linen in her hands, because the structure of the island had taught him, without anyone having had to say it, that the seeing he wanted was not the kind of seeing that came from there.

It is worth noting here, as a small observation that will become a larger observation later, that human beings begin building myths about themselves almost immediately. He was, at eleven or thirteen, already building his. He told Ajei about Alexander and Caesar because he was practicing, on the only listener safely available to him, the story he was preparing to tell about himself when a more dangerous audience presented. She knew this. She knew it before he did. She folded the linen.

In February of 1768, or thereabouts — the records are imprecise on the month and the parish register is one of the documents that did not survive the long history of the island's hurricanes and fires and indifferences — Rachel Faucette became ill. The illness came in the form of a fever, which was not unusual on the island, and it was probably yellow fever, which was the fever the island made most reliably, although it might have been one of the other fevers, of which there were several, and the distinguishing of one fever from another was not, in 1768 on St. Croix, a science that had been developed to a degree that helped any of the people who had the fevers.

The boy had it too. He and his mother had it at the same time, and they were put into the same bed in the rooms above the store, because there were not enough beds in the household to separate them, and because the doctor who attended them, a man named Heering, believed, like most doctors of his century, that a fever was a thing the body would either survive or not survive and that the placement of the body during the survival or non-survival was not of medical consequence. They lay in the same bed for five days. The bed was a four-poster of dark wood that had come with Rachel from Nevis and that had, in better years, been the central object of the largest room in whatever house she had been living in, and which on Company Street in Christiansted in 1768 was the only object in the room other than a chair and a small table and a basin and the books, which sat on a shelf above the bed where, during the five days of the fever, the boy could see them by turning his head.

He turned his head to see them. He turned his head, too, to see his mother, who lay beside him and who was, on the second day, still able to speak and who said certain things to him on that second day that he would, in his entire adult life, never write down, but which can be reconstructed by anyone who has ever sat beside a parent in a bed of that kind on a day of that kind: instructions about the brother, about the cousin, about the store, about the books. By the third day she was no longer speaking. By the fourth day her breathing had taken on the rhythm that the body adopts when it has begun the work of finishing, which is a rhythm anyone who has heard it once recognizes the second time without anyone having to tell them what they are listening to. On the morning of the fifth day she stopped breathing. The boy was beside her. He was conscious. The fever had broken in him the night before, by one of the unpredictable mercies of his particular constitution, which would extend that mercy to him many times in his life, in fevers and in battles and in winters and in epidemics, the mercy that allowed his body, for reasons no doctor of his century or of any later century would ever be able to explain, to survive the things that killed the bodies around it. He was beside her when she died. He was alone in the room with her at the moment she died, because the brother had been sent downstairs and Ajei had been sent to fetch the doctor and the doctor had not yet arrived and would not arrive in time and would, when he did arrive, examine her for thirty seconds and pronounce what everyone in the household had already pronounced, and would charge a fee for the pronouncement, which would be added to the long list of debts that the estate of Rachel Faucette did not have the resources to settle.

He did not cry. He would not cry, in the records that survive, for any death in his life until the death of his son thirty-three years later on the ledge above the river, and even then he would not exactly cry, by the accounts of the people who were in the room, but would make a sound that those people would later describe with the same word his wife would use, which was not a word at all but a refusal to give the thing a word. On the morning of his mother's death he lay beside her body and looked at the books on the shelf above the bed and understood, with a clarity that the fever had perhaps sharpened rather than dulled, that the woman who had read those books aloud to him in the evenings was no longer in the room, and that the books were now in the room without her, and that the books would have to do, from that morning forward, the work of being the part of her that did not die.

The estate was settled in the manner of estates of women of her class. The Lavien marriage had not, in the eyes of the Danish court that had granted her separation, conferred any rights of inheritance on her sons by Hamilton, who were the issue of an unsanctioned union and therefore, in the legal vocabulary of the island, illegitimate. The library was claimed by Lavien's son from Rachel's earlier marriage, by an instrument of the court whose only function, as far as the boy could ever afterward determine, was to ensure that the thirty-four books which had been the only thing she had managed to keep through the entire arc of her displaced life were the things her sons by Hamilton would not be permitted to inherit. The books were taken. They were taken out of the rooms above the store within a month of her death, packed into a wooden crate, and carried down the stairs by two men whom the boy did not know and who did not look at him as they passed him in the doorway. He stood in the doorway. He watched the crate go.

He did not get the books back. He would, in later years, in better circumstances, acquire other copies of most of those titles, and he would build, in New York, a library that exceeded his mother's many times over in volume and in range. But the original thirty-four books left the island in the crate and did not return, and the leaving of them was the lesson, the second lesson the island taught him after the lesson of the wharf, that the law was not a neutral instrument. The law was an instrument that had been built, over centuries, by certain men for certain purposes, and the purposes did not include the protection of a woman of her birth or of the sons she had borne to a Scotsman who had not married her, and any boy who proposed, by the work of his life, to alter what the law did or whom it protected would have to learn the law from inside and turn it from inside, because turning it from outside was not a thing that could be done by a person who had been born where he had been born.

After the burial — which took place in a small lot behind the house of a man named Lytton who had been a connection of his mother's and which was attended by perhaps eleven people, of whom one was the boy and one was his brother and one was Ajei, standing in the back, in a clean apron she had washed the night before — the household above the store was dissolved. The boys were sent to live with the cousin, Peter Lytton, who was, in February of 1768, twenty-two years old and who would, in the summer of 1769, kill himself in a room of the small house he had taken on the edge of town, by a wound either of a knife or a pistol depending on which account one credited, and which the surviving members of the family would not afterward discuss, because the not-discussing was the only kindness that remained available to be paid him. The boys, after the cousin's death, were taken in by a merchant family named Stevens, who had a son the boy's age, Edward, with whom the boy had been friends since they were both five or six years old. He moved into the Stevens house. He kept his stool at the counting house. He continued to write in the ledgers and to read in the evenings, no longer his mother's books but other books, borrowed where they could be borrowed and read where they could be read, and he continued, by every account that survived from the period, to be the quietest of any three boys in any room he entered, which was, in the boys he was being compared with, not the quiet of timidity but the quiet of calculation.

He did not see Ajei often after that. She had been claimed, in the same estate settlement that had claimed the books, by Lavien's son, and had been removed from the rooms above the store and sent across the island to a property whose location was not, in any record the boy ever afterward had access to, written down. He saw her once, perhaps a year after his mother's death, when he was walking through the market on a Saturday morning and she was crossing the square with another woman, in the company of a man on horseback whose face he did not know. She saw him. She looked at him for the length of time it took her to cross his line of sight. She did not lift her hand. He did not lift his. They were children still. They were also no longer entirely children, in the way that the island accelerated the conversion of certain kinds of children into certain kinds of adults. He did not, in any letter that survived from the rest of his life, ever again mention her. He did, when he was twelve or thirteen, in a letter to Edward Stevens which has not survived but which Edward in old age claimed to remember a phrase of, write a sentence that began with the words *I contemn the grovelling condition of a clerk*, and which went on to express what Edward, half a century later, would describe as the boy's burning wish to escape. Edward did not, in the half century, attach the wish to any one event or any one person on the island. The biographers afterward would attach the wish to the wharf, to the counting house, to the books removed in the wooden crate, to the mother in the bed of dark wood. They would not attach it to the girl at the table behind the counter with the linen in her hands. They could not have. There was nothing in the surviving record to attach it to.

She saw him. He did not see that she had seen him. He saw, instead, what he had been arranging himself to see, which was the line that ran from the ledger on the counter in the counting house through the desks of the senior clerks and through the corresponding houses in New York and Philadelphia and Bristol and out into the world that lay beyond the harbor of Christiansted, and he understood, by the spring of 1769 or thereabouts, with the clarity that came on him the way clarity came on the men in his books at the moments his mother had read aloud, that the line was a line he was capable of walking along, and that if he did not walk along it the island would do to him what the island did to boys who did not get off it, which was to keep them on it until the fevers or the hurricanes or the slow constant pressure of the heat and the salt and the damp finished them in the way it had finished his mother, and his cousin, and his uncle, and the unnamed men and women whose names had been written in the second ledger at the counting house and whose timetables the planters had learned to incorporate into their projections.

He was going to leave. He did not yet know how. He did not yet know when. He knew only that the leaving was the work of his life, and that everything he did from the spring of 1769 until the morning he stepped onto the wharf with the letter that had been written by men who had read another letter he had written, and which had moved certain of those men to raise a fund to send him north — everything he did between those two mornings was going to be in the service of the leaving. The island was going to kill him if he stayed. He had understood that. He had understood it from the bed of dark wood with his mother beside him and the books on the shelf above. He had understood it from the wharf and from the second ledger and from the wooden crate carried down the stairs by two men who did not look at him. He had understood it from the courtyard with the cistern, and from the back room with the linen, and from the girl whose name he had been allowed to call by an approximation of the name her grandmother had once called her, and whom he had not, in the year and a half since his mother's death, been able to find any way to think about that did not require him to think about a great many other things he was not yet prepared to think about.

He was eleven, or thirteen. He sat at his desk in the counting house with the harbor behind him through the two windows. He wrote, in the steady deliberate hand he had been imitating from the Danish governor's secretary, the names of the ships that had come in that week, and the consignees in New York and Philadelphia who would receive their cargoes, and the dates by which payment was expected in the offices on Pearl Street and on Front Street, in a city he had never seen and which he had decided, without telling anyone, including his brother and including Ajei and including himself in any words he would have permitted himself to write down, that he was going to live in by the time he was twenty.

He kept writing. The harbor at low tide gave up its smell behind him. A ship was being unloaded. He did not turn his head.
