# Chapter 22 — The Feed

His name was Caleb Reeve. He was forty-one years old, in good general health, possessed of a square jaw and the particular hairline that men of his class and his decade had begun to maintain through a regimen of small interventions they did not discuss with one another at dinner and did not deny in interviews and did not, in any reliable sense, conceal. He stood six feet and one inch tall in the morning and a measurable fraction less than that by evening, and he weighed, on the scale that he kept in the bathroom of the apartment he had taken on the upper floor of a building on the south end of a city that for purposes of this chapter does not need to be named, somewhere in the range that a man of his height was supposed to weigh, although the range had been adjusted, in his case, by a private nutritionist whose number he kept in a drawer in the kitchen and whom he called when the figure on the scale displeased him, which it did, in those years, on most mornings of most weeks.

He had been born in a small inland city in a state in the middle of the country in a year that does not concern us. His father had owned a chain of small businesses in that city, and the businesses had done well enough, in the period during which they had done well, to send him to a private secondary school in the East and afterward to a university that the families he had been raised among regarded with the wary respect they reserved for institutions they had not themselves attended. He had not, by any account given by the people who had known him at the university, particularly distinguished himself there. He had been on the periphery of a fraternity and the periphery of an undergraduate publication and the periphery of a small group of students who were, at twenty-one, already speaking to one another in the cadence of men who intended, by forty, to be known. He had absorbed the cadence. He had absorbed it the way certain young men absorb cadences, which was to imitate them well enough that the imitation, by his middle twenties, had become indistinguishable from the thing imitated, including in his own ear.

He had gone, after the university, into the small adjacent businesses that men of his exact profile had begun to be hired into in those years: a consultancy, a brief stint in a public-affairs office in the capital, a position at a media company whose product he had been hired not so much to make as to be photographed in front of. He had been good in front of the camera. This was the first specific gift the chapter wishes to credit him with, because the gift had been operative since he was twenty-three and is operative still at the moment this chapter finds him, and the rest of his life has been organized, in ways visible and invisible to him, around the management of it. He was good in front of the camera in the particular way that certain men of his generation were good in front of the camera, which was that he did not appear to be performing in front of one. The composure that earlier generations had spent decades cultivating in the parlors of New York and London — the composure that the small man in the boat at Weehawken two centuries ago had imitated until the imitation became the man — Caleb Reeve had absorbed at twenty-three from the screens around him, and had reproduced into the screens that recorded him, in a closed loop that had been operative in his nervous system since before he had any opinions of his own to broadcast across it.

He had opinions now. He had several. He had built, by the time the chapter opens, a public position from which the opinions were broadcast across the rooms in which the country talked to itself, and the broadcasting was the central daily activity of his life, and the position itself — a programme he hosted, an enterprise he had founded around the programme, a small constellation of secondary surfaces on which his face and his voice appeared in the form he had calibrated — generated a stream of income that was, in the period this chapter is concerned with, sufficient to maintain the apartment and the second house and the staff of approximately fourteen and the children from the first marriage and the wife from the third, and to support, also, a category of expenditure that the chapter will get to in due course but does not need to introduce now.

It was, by any reasonable measure, a successful life. The chapter wishes to be careful about this, because the impulse to begin the description with the costs is the wrong impulse, and the costs do not become legible if the success is not first granted. He had built it. He had built it himself, in the sense that men of his class and his decade who built things built them, which was to say: he had assembled, over twenty years, a set of arrangements that other men with similar advantages had failed to assemble, and the assembling was a thing he had done and that not every man could have done, and the chapter records the fact before going on to the rest.

The chapter opens on a morning in autumn. He woke at five forty-seven, three minutes before the alarm was set to go, in the manner of men who have trained their bodies, over years, into the rhythm of the device that wakes them. He lay on his back in the bed. He listened, in the half-light, to the sound of the city below the windows, which at that hour was the sound of trucks and the small distant sirens and the air-conditioning of the building cycling on, and he listened, also, to the noise inside the apartment, which was the sound of nothing because his wife was in the second house and the children were not with him this week, and he was alone in the bed and in the rooms and in the long quiet that began in the apartment at the end of the broadcast each night and lasted until the alarm.

He reached for the device on the table beside the bed. This is the small action the chapter wishes to take its time describing, because it is the action that organized every morning of his life and most of his evenings and a non-trivial fraction of the hours in between, and a chapter that does not slow down for it will misunderstand the shape of his days. He reached for it before he opened his eyes. He brought it to his face. He held it at the angle his hand had memorized, which was the angle that allowed the screen to recognize him without requiring him to perform recognition himself, and the screen recognized him, and opened, and presented him with the small ordered surface of numbers and notifications and images and short sentences which had accumulated, in the night, in the rooms in which the country talked to itself.

He read. He read for forty minutes. He read in the way he had taught himself to read, which was not the way other men of his class read in their early adulthood, and not the way the boys in Alta Gracia or the boys in St. Croix had read, but a way that had been built, in his nervous system, by the surface he was reading on. He read in a fast scan that paused on certain words and certain images for a fraction of a second longer than it paused on others, in a calibration his own mind had developed without his conscious authorization, and the calibration had become, by the morning the chapter is recording, the principal organ by which he related to the world outside the apartment. He read the numbers attached to his own name and his own face. The numbers were arranged into a small column. The column told him, in the language that surface had developed for the purpose, how many of the rooms had been talking about him in the night and in what tones, and the tones were summarized in the column by a small set of indicators which he had learned to read at a glance, and the glance was the first piece of information he absorbed each day, and the absorption preceded any other piece of information by some margin.

He absorbed the column. The column, that morning, was good. The numbers were up from the previous morning. The tones were, in net, favorable. He felt, in the upper chest, the small physical sensation that the favorable column produced in him, which was a sensation he had felt for the first time perhaps twelve years before, on an earlier surface in an earlier room, and which had become, in the intervening twelve years, the principal substitute in his interior life for several of the other sensations that men of earlier generations had organized their mornings around. The sensation was located precisely at the top of the sternum. It lasted, by his rough internal accounting, between four and six seconds. It was followed, often but not always, by a slightly hollow sensation in the same location, which the favorable column did not address and which the next favorable column would not address either, although the next favorable column would, when it arrived, produce the same four-to-six-second pleasure as the one before it. He had noticed the hollow sensation. He had not, in any conversation with anyone, named it. He had developed, by the time the chapter opens, a small set of arrangements for moving past it as efficiently as the structure of his mornings permitted.

He got out of the bed. He went to the bathroom. He stood on the scale. The figure displeased him. He noted the figure, mentally, and added a small mental subtraction to the day's arrangement of meals. He showered. He dressed. He went into the kitchen, where the assistant who came in at six had already started the coffee, and he stood at the counter with the device in his hand and continued the reading he had begun in the bed, in a posture that an observer in the kitchen would have recognized as the posture men of his decade adopted when they had transferred the centre of their attention into the surface in their hand and were running the rest of their body on a kind of standing autopilot.

The assistant did not look at him. The assistant had learned, in the eighteen months of the position, not to look at him during the morning reading, because the morning reading was the period of the day in which his face was the most legible to anyone in the room and the legibility was not a thing he wished to be observed. The assistant moved coffee and small plates and folders across the counter without making eye contact and without making any noise that could be construed as the kind of noise that interrupted what the man at the counter was doing.

The assistant's name was Jess Aaronson. She was twenty-eight. She had a degree from a respectable university and a year of graduate work in a discipline she had not finished, and she had been hired into the position from a smaller position at a research foundation eighteen months earlier on the recommendation of a woman they both knew, and she had taken the position because the position paid roughly double the previous one and because the proximity to the man at the counter was the kind of proximity that, in the rooms in which her career would later be conducted, was assumed to be valuable. She had not, by the morning the chapter opens, decided whether the assumption was correct. She had noticed, in the eighteen months, several things about him that the rooms which would later need to know about him did not know, and the knowledge sat in her, in the morning by the coffee machine, with the small particular weight of knowledge that has not yet been spoken to anyone.

She was the foil. The chapter is not going to elaborate her in this opening — Chapter 23 will do that work, and will do it for several women, of whom she will be one. But she should be introduced now, by name, in the kitchen, because the chapter cannot proceed honestly without the reader being told that she was there, on the morning the chapter opens, watching him, and that her watching was a watching of a particular kind. She saw the column on the device. She did not see the screen directly. She saw it on his face, in the small fractional changes in the muscles around the eye and the mouth, which she had been reading for eighteen months and had become, by accident and by professional necessity, the most reliable existing instrument for reading. She knew, that morning, before he had said anything, that the column was favorable. She knew, also, that the favorability would carry him through approximately ninety minutes of the day and that he would, around nine-thirty, need to be handed the next thing, and that the next thing would have to be calibrated in advance. She had calibrated it. The folder was on the counter. She had put it there at five fifty-five.

He noticed her, in the way he noticed her on those mornings, which was as a piece of competent furniture that knew what he needed before he had asked for it and that did not require him to thank it. He did, in fact, thank her, in a small phrase he used without looking up. She nodded without looking at him. He took the folder. He left the kitchen. The day, in the form the day was going to take, began.

It is necessary now to say something general about the surface that had organized his morning, because the chapter cannot continue with the day without first acknowledging what the surface was, and the acknowledgment must be made in literary language, because the language that other writers have used for the surface in the period this chapter is concerned with is the language of journalism, and journalism is not the register in which this book has been written, and is not the register in which this book will end.

The surface had developed, over thirty years, from an early arrangement by which the rooms in which the country talked to itself had been organized along a small number of broad channels — newspapers, broadcasts, public squares of the older kind — into an arrangement by which the rooms had been disaggregated into a continuous moving aggregate of small particular voices that arrived at any one face in any one apartment in any one city in the country in the form of a stream. The stream was the surface. The surface was the stream. There was no single room any longer in which the country talked to itself. There were several billion small simultaneous rooms, and the surface was the arrangement that gathered the noise of all of them and presented it, at any given moment, to the eye of any single one of the people who was attending to it, in an order that had been arranged by mechanisms whose operation was not visible to the person attending.

This will be the language the chapter uses. The chapter is not going to name the surface by the names that the surface goes by in the period this chapter is concerned with. The chapter is not going to name the proprietors of the surface or the small companies that arrange the orderings. The chapter is going to call it the feed, or the stream, or the rooms, or the aggregate, or the network, and the chapter is going to do so partly because the names by which the surface is presently known are not the names by which it will be known in fifty years, when the surface is either gone or so altered that the names will read as quaint, and partly because the names are not the level at which the chapter wishes to operate. The level the chapter wishes to operate at is the level of what the surface did to the nervous system of the man who was, that morning, reading it in his kitchen, and what the doing was, and what the doing led to.

A reader from an earlier century would not, the chapter wishes to suggest, have found the doing entirely unfamiliar. There have been, in the records the narrator has had occasion to consider, several earlier arrangements by which the noise of a country was concentrated into the eye of a man who had set himself to be talked about by it. The pamphlets in London in the seventeenth century. The salons in Paris in the eighteenth. The penny papers in New York in the nineteenth. The radio in Berlin in the nineteen-thirties. Each of those arrangements had taken a particular kind of man — and the man had usually been a man — and had made of him a particular kind of figure, and the making had involved, in each case, a small consistent set of effects on the figure's interior life. The effects had been documented by writers who had stood, sometimes, beside the figures. The effects were not new. What was new, in the surface that organized Caleb Reeve's morning, was the rate. The rate was the new thing. The pamphlets had arrived in the parlors of London once a week. The penny papers had arrived in New York once a day. The radio had arrived in Berlin perhaps four times a day. The surface in Caleb Reeve's hand arrived continuously, in a stream that did not pause, in a frequency that exceeded the body's capacity to digest the arrival, and the body, denied the pause, had begun to organize itself around the continuous arrival in ways that the bodies of pamphleteers and penny editors and radio announcers had not been required to organize themselves around. The rate was the new thing. The chapter will return to the rate.

He arrived at the studio at seven forty-two. The studio was in a converted warehouse on the west side of the city, on a street that had been industrial twenty years before and was now the address of three production companies and a private members' club and a restaurant that had, on the evening the chapter is moving away from, a reservation in his name. He went up in the elevator. He greeted the security guard by first name. He greeted the receptionist by first name. He greeted the producer by first name and by a small joke they had been telling each other for three years and that neither of them found funny but that both of them performed each morning because the not-performing of it would have been more conspicuous than the performing of it.

He went into the small room where the makeup was done. He sat in the chair. The woman who did the makeup, whose name was Bea, applied to his face the substances that had been calibrated, over four years, to the skin tone the cameras were calibrated to receive. She did not talk during the procedure. He looked at the device. He continued to read.

The reading, at that hour of the morning, was the reading of the column as it had moved in the ninety minutes since he had left the apartment. The column had moved. The numbers were higher than they had been at six. The tones were slightly more mixed than they had been at six. There was, attached to his name, a small piece of writing that had been produced by another man in another city in the night, which was unflattering, and which had begun to be circulated in one of the rooms he kept track of, and the circulation was the thing he was watching, in the chair with the woman applying the substance to his cheek, and the watching had the small quality of the watching he did during attacks of a different kind, except that there were no attacks of the other kind in his life, and the watching was the only kind of watching of that quality that he had ever done, and he did not know, because no man of his decade had had occasion to know, that the watching was a watching he had been doing for fifteen years and that the doing had altered, in those fifteen years, the structure of his attention in ways that could be measured, although nobody had yet measured them on him.

A flash of something passed through him in the chair. It is a small thing and the chapter wishes to record it carefully, because it is the kind of thing that he had, in recent months, begun to experience more often, and which he had not yet found any language for. It was a small vertigo. It lasted perhaps two seconds. It came on him with the question, which arrived not in words but as a kind of empty space behind the eyes, of whether the thing he was about to feel about the piece of writing in the room he was watching was a thing he was feeling because the piece of writing had produced it in him or because the room he was watching had organized the producing in advance, and the question, when it arrived, did not have an answer that he could supply from inside the chair, and the answer, in any case, would not have changed what he was going to feel, because the feeling was already underway by the time the question had finished arriving. The vertigo passed. The makeup was completed. He got out of the chair. He went into the studio.

He did the programme. The programme was an hour and a half long. He was good at it. He had been good at it for nine years. He was, by the standards the surface he operated on had established for the work he did, the most accomplished practitioner the surface had produced in his particular sub-genre, and the accomplishment was a thing that several million people in the country could be relied upon to attend to, three times a week, in the slot he had been broadcasting in for the last four. He spoke. He listened to the guest. He spoke again. He raised his voice at the calibrated moment. He lowered his voice at the calibrated moment. He made the small movements with his hand that the camera had been positioned to catch. He did the thing he had spent his adult life learning to do, and he did it that morning, by his own internal estimate and by the estimates of the producer who watched him through the glass and the assistant who watched him on the screen in the apartment and the wife who watched him on the screen in the second house, well.

He came off the air at nine-thirty. He went into the producer's office. He read the numbers from the broadcast. The numbers were good. The numbers were better than the numbers from the broadcast two days before. The numbers from the broadcast two days before had been better than the numbers from the broadcast four days before. The numbers, for some weeks, had been moving in the direction of the favorable end of the column, and the moving had become, in his interior life, the principal indicator of the worth of the work he was doing, and the worth of the work he was doing had become, in his interior life, the principal indicator of the worth of the man he was, and the chain of indicators had been completed, by the time the chapter opens, into a closed loop, and the loop was the one he ran his day along.

He was happy. The chapter records this, because the chapter has been moving toward an examination of him that may, if not carefully balanced, appear unfriendly, and the chapter is not unfriendly to him. He was happy, that morning, in the producer's office, with the numbers on the desk and the producer telling him in the small phrases the producer had developed for the purpose how well he had done. He felt the small physical sensation at the top of the sternum that the favorable column produced in him, and the sensation lasted, that morning, for slightly longer than its usual four to six seconds, because the numbers had been particularly good, and because the man on the desk was a man whose praise he had organized himself, for years, to receive. He sat in the chair. He let the sensation last as long as the sensation was willing to last. It lasted, by his rough internal accounting, perhaps eleven seconds. It was followed by the hollow.

The chapter is going to compress the rest of the day, because the rest of the day was the day, and the day was, in its general shape, the shape every day of his recent life had taken, and a long description of the rest of it would not produce, in the reader, any understanding that has not already been produced by the description of the morning.

He took meetings. He had lunch with a man whose money he was attempting to attract into a venture that was adjacent to but not the same as the programme, and the lunch was conducted in a restaurant in which both men were known to the staff, and the staff, who were professionals in the management of the lunches of men of that description, allowed the two men to perform, for the duration of the meal, a small ritual which the staff had observed enough times in enough variants to be able to anticipate the order of its movements before the men in the booth made them. He came back to the studio. He made a recording for a smaller surface, addressed to a different room. He took a call from a member of an institution in the capital whose name he did not say out loud in any public context but whose number he kept in the same drawer in the kitchen as the nutritionist's. He approved certain decisions about the next morning's programme. He approved certain decisions about a piece of his image that had been licensed, for the purposes of an advertisement, to a company whose product he did not personally use. He signed the documents that had been left on his desk. He left the studio at four-thirteen.

He went, at four-thirty, to an apartment two streets away from the studio, on the upper floor of a building he did not own, in which a woman lived whose name was not Jess Aaronson and was not his wife and was not the name of any of the other women whom the rooms in which the country talked to itself had occasionally attached to his name, and the woman was twenty-six years old, and she was a translator, and the visits to the apartment had been organized over the last seven months in a pattern she had begun to be able to predict and to which she had, by the autumn the chapter is concerned with, made a small set of accommodations in her own week. The chapter is not going to describe the visit. The chapter will say only that the visit was the third operational metabolism of the man, alongside the column on the device and the figures in the producer's office, and that the metabolism had been, in his interior life, organized into the same closed loop as the others — the small sensation at the top of the sternum, the slightly longer duration than usual, the hollow afterward — and that the woman in the apartment, whose name was Catalina Ríos, had begun, in the seven months, to be able to predict the temporal arc of the visit with the same accuracy with which the producer at the studio predicted the temporal arc of the programme, and that the prediction in her case had been arrived at without her ever having been consulted about whether she wished to arrive at it.

Catalina Ríos is, for the purposes of this chapter and the chapters that will follow, the specific woman the outline has called for. She is closer to him than the rooms in which the country talked to itself yet knew, and she sees him in the way that women in her position have, for several centuries, seen the men whose interior arrangements they have been required, by the structure of the position, to read in order to be able to operate in it. She translates, by profession. She translates documents and at occasional larger events the speech of men who have arranged to have her present in the small booth at the side of the room. She is good at her work in the particular way the work requires, which is that she carries, into the spoken language she produces, none of the texture of her own person, and the absence of the texture is the technical virtue by which her work has been distinguished from the work of less skilled practitioners. She has carried the same technical virtue into the visits with him. She is not present, in the apartment, as a person; she is present as the receiving surface for a particular thing he comes there to do, and the absence of her own texture, in those hours, is the same technical virtue by which she earns her professional living.

She sees him. She sees him in the small interval between his arrival and the beginning of the visit, in the moment in which his face is the most legible, and the legibility is not unlike the legibility that the assistant in the kitchen at six has learned to read at the counter. She sees the column on his face. She sees the hollow. She sees the small vertigo behind the eye that has, in the last seven months, begun to appear with greater frequency, and which she does not, in the apartment, ever mention. She sees, in particular, a thing that she has seen for some weeks and that she has not yet decided what to do with, which is that the man in front of her is afraid. He is afraid in the form that men of his sort, in his decade, are afraid: not of death, which is far away, and not of poverty, which is closer than the rooms know but is still not the central fear, but of irrelevance, which is the form fear takes for men whose lives have been organized around the column on the surface, and which arrives, when it arrives, in the form of a small drop in the numbers and a small change in the tone of the rooms that they have been watching themselves in. He is afraid. She is the only person, that autumn, who is seeing the fear clearly, including him. He does not see it. He cannot see it. He is inside it. She is the only one outside it who is close enough to be looking at it.

She does not tell him. She does not tell anyone. This is the thing the chapter records about her, the same thing it has recorded about other women in earlier chapters in different costumes, and the recording, by this point in the book, should not need to be explained.

He left her apartment at six-twenty. He went to a dinner. He went to a thing after the dinner. He came home, to the apartment with the bed and the scale and the device on the table, at eleven forty-eight. He undressed. He sat on the edge of the bed. He looked at the device. He read the column. The column, at eleven forty-eight, was good. He felt the small physical sensation at the top of the sternum. He was held, by it, for a moment.

He was sitting on the edge of the bed. He was in his undershirt. The device was in his right hand. The screen was lit. The column was favorable. The number attached to his name had moved, in the hours since the broadcast, by a margin that he had not, in the lifetime he had been watching such margins, often seen. The piece of writing that had been circulating in the morning had been answered, in the afternoon, by a larger piece of writing in his favor in a room he had not expected the answer to come from, and the larger piece had moved into the central position of the surface, and the central position was the position that the surface had been engineered, by mechanisms he did not personally understand, to weight more heavily than any other. He was the central position. He was, that night, in the central position. The country had, in the period of the last six hours, been talking about him in the way the country had been talking about other men, in earlier decades, who had occupied the central position of earlier surfaces, and the talking had been, in net, favorable, and the favorability had moved the number, and the number was the number on the device in his right hand.

He looked at the number. He held the device. He felt the small pleasure at the top of the sternum, and he felt it last longer, that night, than it had lasted at any point in the day, because the number was higher than it had been at any point in the day, and because the central position of the surface had been, for those six hours, his, and because the man on the edge of the bed in the undershirt with the device in his right hand had, that night, by every internal indicator he had constructed for the purpose, won. He had won. He had won in the only sense the word had any operational meaning for him. The number on the device confirmed it. The pleasure at the top of the sternum confirmed it. The room around him, which was empty, did not confirm it, because the room was not the surface, and the surface was the thing the confirming was done in, and the surface was in his right hand, and the surface was glowing.

He held it. He held it longer than was strictly necessary. He held it past the duration at which the pleasure usually became the hollow, because the number was high enough that the pleasure was, that night, lasting somewhat longer than the usual duration. He was holding the device in the way a man might hold a small living thing, if a small living thing had agreed, for a moment, to be held by him without resisting, and he was making the small unconscious movement with his thumb across the surface of the device that men of his decade had developed in their nervous systems without ever having been taught it, and the movement was a movement that produced, on the screen, a small refresh of the column, and the column refreshed, and the number was a fraction higher than it had been a moment before, and the small pleasure at the top of the sternum, which had been about to begin its decline into the hollow, was renewed by the refresh and held in place by the new number, and was renewed again, by the next refresh, into the next number, and was renewed again.

He thought, in the half-thought that flickered through him at the edge of the renewing, that the device was confirming, by each refresh, that he had not yet stopped being the man at the centre of the central position of the surface, and that the not-yet-stopping was, for the duration of the holding, the thing he was. He thought it without exactly thinking it. He thumbed the surface again. The number moved. He felt the small pleasure at the top of the sternum hold.

He was, in that moment, mistaking the number for the man. He did not know he was doing it. There was no one in the room to point it out. The country, in its several billion small simultaneous rooms, was not in a position to point it out either, because the country was, by the structure of the surface, in the same arrangement with respect to its own numbers, in its own decade, and was, that night, holding its own devices, in a great aggregate of small renewals of small pleasures at the tops of several million sternums, and the aggregate was the country, and the country had, in the period this chapter has been concerned with, become a country that could not be told, by any voice that the surface would carry, that the holding was not the same as the having.

He held the device. He thumbed it. The number moved. He smiled, slightly, at the small bright surface in his hand, in the same posture in which other men of other decades had smiled at portraits of themselves on the walls of their studies, with one important difference: the portraits had not refreshed. He smiled. The pleasure at the top of the sternum, by the third or fourth refresh, was no longer a single sensation but a small continuous one, organized into the pulse of the refreshes the way a heartbeat is organized into the pulse of a body, and he was, in the moment of the third or fourth refresh, with the small continuous pleasure running in him and the device in his right hand and the room around him empty, as happy as he had been at any moment of his adult life, and the happiness, which would not, even by his own later accounting in the chapters that followed, withstand much examination, was, for the duration of the holding, the only thing in the room with him, and was the only thing he required to be in the room with him, and the surface, which had brought it, was glowing in his hand at the precise level of brightness it had been calibrated to glow at, and the man on the edge of the bed in the undershirt, whose name was Caleb Reeve, was holding the surface, and the surface was holding him, and the night around them, in the apartment, in the city, in the country in which the rooms had become the surface and the surface had become the country, was very still.
