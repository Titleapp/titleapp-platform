# Chapter 11 — Asthma

The first thing to be said about his birth is that the date on the certificate was not the correct one. He was registered as having been born in Rosario, in the province of Santa Fe, on the fourteenth of June, in the year nineteen hundred and twenty-eight, and the certificate was filed in that city on that date by the man who would carry his name through the rest of his life and most of the rest of the boy's. The certificate was a small instrument of social arrangement. It moved the place and the month of the birth by some distance and some weeks in order to accommodate a fact that the families of the parents had agreed, in private and without writing anything down, would not be permitted to enter the public account of the child's beginning, which was that his mother had carried him for a longer time before the marriage than the marriage could afterward be permitted to be seen to contain. The child had been born, in fact, in the province of Misiones, in a house upriver from Posadas, in a month some weeks earlier than the certificate stated, and the alteration had been performed by the simple expedient of writing other numbers in another city when nobody from the first city was present to disagree.

This was not, in the Argentina of those years, an unusual arrangement. Families of a certain class — families with sufficient name and insufficient money, which was the precise economic position of the Guevara Lynches and the de la Sernas in the late nineteen-twenties — were practiced in the small adjustments by which the appearance of a thing could be persuaded to differ from the substance of it. A certificate was a piece of paper. A piece of paper could be filed in any city in which one had a relation willing to file it. The parish registers of the country were thick, in those decades, with such accommodations, and the children who emerged from inside them grew up in the soft custody of an origin story that had been, before they could remember anything, already a small construction.

It is necessary to mention this at the opening of the chapter and then to set it aside, because the boy himself did not know of it until much later, and when he learned of it he did not, in any letter that survived, make of it the kind of thing he might have been expected to make of it, given the kind of man he eventually became. He noted it. He noted it in the way one notes a small interesting fact about a building one has been living in for years. The note went into the inventory of things he had been given to work with, and he turned, in the working, to other materials. He had others. He had several. But the certificate sits at the beginning of his life like the first sentence of a forged document, and a reader who has the rest of the document in hand should be told about the sentence before reading further.

◊

The next thing to be said is that he could not breathe.

He could not breathe at two years old, and he could not breathe at four, and he could not breathe in the winter when the air came down from the mountains and he could not breathe in the summer when the heat thickened on the river and he could not breathe in his bed in the rooms in Rosario and he could not breathe in the rooms the family took afterward on the calle Entre Ríos in Buenos Aires and he could not breathe in the larger room with the high ceiling in the house in Alta Gracia in the hills above the city of Córdoba where the family moved when he was four because a doctor in Buenos Aires had told the father, in the flat tone in which doctors of that decade conveyed information they could not act upon, that the boy would die before he was six if the family did not take him into drier air.

The family took him into drier air. They went up into the sierras above Córdoba, to a small town that had been, in earlier centuries, a Jesuit mission and that had become, in the nineteen-thirties, a place where families with children whose lungs could not be persuaded to function on the coast came to live in rented houses with verandahs and to wait, in the manner of such places, for the air to do the work that the medicine of the century was not yet equipped to do. The house was called the Villa Nydia. It had a tiled roof and a long porch and a garden that ran down toward a stream, and the air there was dry in the way the doctor had specified, and the air there did not, in any season the family lived inside it, make the boy able to breathe.

What is to be said about asthma. It is necessary, before continuing, to say what it is, because the literature on him will use the word freely and a reader of that literature can pass through whole chapters of his biography without ever pausing on the specific physical fact that organized every day of his life until he was forty. Asthma is the involuntary closing of the small passages of the lungs. The closing is an inflammation. The inflammation can be mild and the closing can be partial, in which case the person who has it experiences only a tightening, a hesitation in the chest, a sense that the next breath will not be quite as available as the one before. The closing can be severe and the inflammation total, in which case the small passages narrow to a degree that the air, which is freely available in the room and on all sides of the body, cannot be persuaded into the lungs at the rate the body requires in order to continue. A person in a severe attack does not breathe. The air is there. The chest moves. The body works. The air does not arrive. The hours of an attack are spent at the interface between the available air and the body that cannot have it, in a struggle that is invisible to anyone in the room with the person but that is, to the person, the only struggle. Attacks could last a night. Attacks could last two nights. Attacks could end suddenly without explanation and could begin suddenly without warning, and the inability of the medicine of those decades to either predict the beginnings or compel the endings was the condition under which the asthmatic child grew up.

He grew up inside it. He grew up inside the not-quite-arriving of the next breath. He grew up with the small portable inhalers his mother kept in a leather case beside his bed, and with the pillows arranged so that his upper body would be elevated through the night, and with the sound of his own chest at four in the morning, which was a sound he came to know the way other children came to know the sound of a familiar street outside their window. The body had a noise. The noise was not the noise of any other body in the house. He listened to it. He listened to it for years before he had any vocabulary for what listening was, and the listening was the first interior practice of his life, and it shaped the listening he would do afterward to other things — to books, to politics, to his mother's voice in the next room — because the equipment had been built first on his own breath.

His mother was Celia de la Serna y Llosa. This is the point in the chapter at which the narrator wishes to be careful, because the literature on Celia is thin and the impulse to flatten her, to treat her as a station along the boy's route, is strong, and the narrator does not intend to participate in it. She was, in 1928 when he was born, twenty-one years old. She had been educated by the nuns of the Sacred Heart in a convent in Buenos Aires from which she had been expelled at sixteen for opinions the nuns were not able to keep up with, and she had read, by the time she married, more books than any of her brothers, who were not few, and she had inherited from her side of the family the small steady income that the larger steady income from her husband's mate-leaf interests was supposed to supplement and never quite did. She was a tall woman with dark hair cut short in the manner of women in Buenos Aires in the late nineteen-twenties who had decided that the dark hair would be cut short, and she smoked, and she read, and she swam in the river at the foot of the garden of the Villa Nydia with her son on her back when the son was four and five and six years old and was not in the middle of an attack, and she did not protect him from political conversation at the dinner table, and she did not protect him from the books on the shelves of the rooms in which he could not sleep, and she did not, when he was eight and nine and ten, send him to the schools other families in Alta Gracia sent their children to but kept him at home and taught him there, partly because the attacks made the schools impractical and partly because she suspected, in the way mothers of a certain education in a certain decade suspected, that the schools would teach him less than she could teach him in the rooms of the house with the books on the shelves and the air coming in through the window.

She taught him French. This is one of the things she taught him, and it must be said early because it will matter later. She taught him French from a small grammar she had used herself at the convent and from a worn copy of *Les Misérables* and from her own voice in the evenings in the chair beside his bed when the attacks would not let him sleep, and she read to him in French and she read to him in Spanish and the two languages came in through the same door of his attention in the same period of his life, with the result that he grew up with the small persistent sense, which he would carry into adulthood and into every country he later moved through, that the language a thing was named in was a costume the thing wore, and that the thing underneath the costume was the same thing in any language, and that a person who learned the costumes could move among them without ever permitting any single one of them to fit too closely.

She read him Neruda. She read him a Spanish translation of Baudelaire, although the Baudelaire she read was a careful edition and certain of the poems were not included, and he would, at fifteen, find the unincluded poems in another edition in his uncle's library and read them in a single afternoon with the door closed. She read him Verne, because every boy in that decade was read Verne, and because Verne supplied a particular kind of fuel that an asthmatic child needed in greater quantity than other children: the fuel of the long journey, the submarine, the balloon, the underground river, the rocket — the fuel, in other words, of bodies that went places without requiring the bodies of their occupants to do anything that the small passages of his lungs would not have permitted.

He kept a notebook. This needs to be said too, because it is the practice that begins in this period and runs without interruption through the rest of his life. The notebook was bound in cloth and was, in the years the family lived at the Villa Nydia, the only object in the house that was unambiguously his, in the sense that the books were his mother's and the chairs were the family's and the bed had been the bed of an uncle who had given it to them when they came up from the coast. He wrote vocabulary in the notebook. He wrote, in three columns, the Spanish word and the French word and, eventually, the English word, which he had begun to acquire from a tutor who came on Tuesdays and Thursdays. He wrote sentences that pleased him from the books his mother read aloud. He wrote, in the back of the notebook in a smaller hand, certain observations of his own that he did not show to anyone, including her, and which were the first sentences he ever wrote for an audience that was not present in the room.

A boy of that kind, in that condition, in that decade, in that house, was going to read everything. He read everything. The reading was the activity available to him during the attacks and during the recoveries from the attacks and during the long afternoons when the attacks had not begun yet but the body was rehearsing the conditions under which they did, and there was little else the boy could do with the available hours of his life except move his eyes across the pages of books and listen to the wheezing of his chest and wait for the next thing to happen.

◊

The next thing, in his case, was rugby.

It is necessary to pause on this. The narrator is asking the reader to credit, in a single short paragraph, the proposition that an asthmatic child of the kind described above took up, at eleven, the most physically punishing of the available ball games. It is necessary to credit it because it happened. He played rugby. He played it with a fierceness that the boys he played with would remember, fifty years later in interviews with biographers, as the defining oddity of his presence on the field. He played at fly-half and later at scrum-half, in the small clubs and informal teams that the boys of Alta Gracia and the surrounding district organized on weekends and on the long flat field beside the railway line. He played, his teammates would remember, with a particular relationship to the body that was different from the relationship the rest of them had: he played as if the body were a thing that was being punished for a fault it had been guilty of since infancy.

He carried the inhaler in the pocket of his shorts. This is the small detail that the literature on him likes, because it is the detail in which the figure of the man is already visible in the figure of the boy. He would stop, mid-game, in the middle of a passage of play, walk to the touchline with the inhaler already coming out of his pocket, take the necessary breaths in the necessary number, and then turn and walk back onto the field and resume the game, without apology, without explanation, without ever, in the testimony of any teammate, looking at any other player as he came back on. The other players, who were larger and faster and whose lungs functioned, would, for the first season, attempt to make a joke of it, and after the first season would stop, because the joke would not return any answer from the boy. He played on. He played on for years. He played on through attacks his mother begged him not to play on through, and on through evenings when his father, watching from the side of the field with the worn look of a man who had been outvoted by his son in a contest of will the father had not realized was being contested, would walk back to the car afterward with the boy slumped against the door and the breath not quite arriving and the small wet sounds in the boy's chest that the father, in the privacy of the car, would say nothing about.

The father. The father has to be brought in here. He has not been brought in enough, and a chapter that did not bring him in would be doing him an injustice that he did not, in fact, deserve, although certain of the later biographies have done it to him.

Ernesto Guevara Lynch was, in those years, an engineer who had not finished his engineering studies, a businessman who had not made his business work, a husband who had not, in the deepest sense, made his marriage work either, although the marriage continued, and would continue, in the form most marriages of that class in that decade continued in, which was the form of an arrangement two people had agreed to and from which neither had found, in the years available to them, the energy to walk away. He was a man who tried things. He had tried a mate plantation in Misiones and the plantation had not paid. He had tried a small construction business in Buenos Aires and the business had not paid. He had tried, by the time the family was settled at the Villa Nydia, several smaller schemes whose particulars do not matter, and the schemes had not paid either, and the family lived on the small steady income of the de la Serna inheritance and on whatever Ernesto Lynch could bring in from the most recent of his schemes, and the imbalance between the two sources was the central tension under which the household operated and the source of the long evenings of voices behind the closed door of his parents' bedroom which the asthmatic child, lying in his own room with the pillows arranged, could hear and could not help hearing and which became, in those years, the audible texture of the family's economic situation.

He loved his father. He was angry with his father. He was angry in the way certain firstborn sons of intelligent failing fathers are angry, which is not in the form of an event but in the form of a long quiet ledger that the son keeps without ever having decided to keep it. The ledger had on it the cancellations, the deferred holidays, the second-hand bicycle that arrived three years after the bicycle had been promised and three years after the boy had stopped wanting a bicycle, the conversations in which the father attempted, with the genuine warmth that he never lost, to explain to his children why the latest scheme had not paid and why the next one would, and the son listened and nodded and, in the part of himself the father did not have access to, added the conversation to the ledger and closed the ledger and put it away for later.

The father, for his part, loved the son with a particular thwarted intensity. He loved him in part because the son had survived. The son had been told, by doctors, that he would die. The son had not died. The father had carried him, several times, in the middle of the night, down the long porch and into the car and along the road to the doctor's house in town, with the boy's small body folded against his own and the boy's breath wheezing into the lapel of his coat, and the father had, on those drives, made the small private promises a father makes to a body that may not be alive when the drive ends. The father had not, in the years afterward, exactly remembered the promises, but the body of the boy continued to be the body to which they had been made, and the father, when he looked at his son, looked at him with the residue of those drives in the look, and the son, who was an observant child, saw the residue and did not, except in moments he later regretted, use it against him.

◊

It is, before going further, worth pausing on the household.

The reading-aloud, the French, the politics at the dinner table, the books on the shelves, the chronically failing schemes, the wheezing in the next room, the mother who did not look away and the father who carried him in the middle of the night and the brothers and the sister who were also in the house and who learned, before they had learned anything else, to organize the noise of their childhood around the noise of his lungs — all of it, in retrospect, looks like the conditions under which a particular kind of child is manufactured. The conditions are not unusual. There have been, in the centuries the narrator has had occasion to consider, several thousand households in Europe and the Americas in which a sickly, observant, well-read eldest son of an educated leftish mother and a charming insolvent father has been produced, and the production has run, in most of those cases, to one of a few predictable ends. The boy becomes a doctor. The boy becomes a journalist. The boy becomes a poet. The boy becomes a small kindly disappointment to himself for sixty years and dies in a chair in a room of the same house with a book open on his knees.

It is the unusual instance that interests biographers, and what interests the narrator about this particular instance, at this point in the chapter, is the small set of supplementary conditions that pushed this household out of the usual range of outcomes and into the one that history has had occasion to remember. There were several. There was the asthma, which gave the boy a relationship to his own body that no boy of his class without the asthma would have had. There was the mother, who had been the wrong kind of girl for the convent and was now the wrong kind of woman for the country, and who had brought the wrong-kindness into the house and was raising her children inside it. There was the location, the small mountain town with its tubercular and asthmatic children and their European-educated mothers and the slow long afternoons in which any boy with a mind could mistake himself for the protagonist of a novel he had not yet finished reading. There was, finally, the country itself, which in the nineteen-thirties and forties was running through its own version of a long failed scheme, and in which the small failed schemes of the father and the small steady inheritance of the mother were, in a sense the boy had not yet articulated but had begun to feel, the household-scale version of a national problem he would, twenty years later, attempt to address at a different scale entirely.

The same instinct that had pushed the boy on the island off the island will push this boy out of his bedroom, the narrator should say at this point, and the narrator will say it, but the narrator says it knowing that the instinct in the two cases is not the same instinct, and that the destinations toward which it points the two boys are not the same destinations, and that the machinery into which it eventually delivers them is not the same machinery. The boy on the island wanted to be inside an appearance that the world had told him he was not born into. The boy in the mountains of Córdoba wanted, in some way he had not yet found the words for, to dismantle the appearance and stand in whatever remained when the appearance had been taken down. The first instinct produces a treasurer. The second produces something else. The histories will, in due course, have occasion to name what.

He went on playing rugby. He went on reading. He went on, in the long arrangement of his early adolescence, building the body that the asthma had given him into a different body than the one it had given him, by an act of will that was not the kind of will most boys of his class had any access to and which, in him, had been built early by the simple necessity of getting through nights in which the breath did not come.

There was a girl, in those years, whose name was Carmen and who was the daughter of one of the families that summered in Alta Gracia, and there was also a girl named María, and the names of two or three others, and the narrator does not intend to make the chapter about them, because the chapter at this point is not about them. The boy did the small things boys do, in the company of the daughters of the summering families, in the long afternoons in the garden and on the porch and on the path that ran down to the stream, and what passed between him and them is recorded in the letters and notebooks of those girls only obliquely, and in his own notebook not at all, and a chapter that attempted to put more into those passages than the letters will support would be inventing the boy out of an impulse the narrator wishes to resist. The girls were there. He noticed them. He was noticed by some of them. The summer ended. The next summer began. The boy who had not been able to breathe was, by fourteen or fifteen, a boy with a mouth and a face that several of the girls who summered in Alta Gracia would remember, in interviews with biographers half a century later, with a particular small smile and a particular form of indirection in the answers, which the biographers, being biographers, would attempt to construe into more than the smiles and the indirections had been intended to convey.

What had been intended to be conveyed about him, in those years, by the people who knew him, was less elaborate. He was the asthmatic Guevara boy. He read everything. He played in the rugby clubs in spite of his lungs. His mother smoked, and read out loud, and held views, and the views were not entirely the views of the other mothers in the town. His father had a scheme. There would, by next summer, be a different scheme. The family had been here a long time and would continue to be here for as long as a family of that economic situation continued to be in a place of that kind, which was a phrase, in Alta Gracia in the nineteen-forties, that did not require an end-date to be supplied.

It is necessary to bring back, at this point, his mother. The narrator has been holding her in reserve, and the chapter cannot end without returning to her, because the figure she is in the chapter is the figure she will be in his life, which is to say: the one person in the household who saw what was happening to the boy and did not arrange the seeing into any consoling story.

Celia, in the years between his twelfth birthday and his seventeenth, was beginning to be a different person than she had been in the rooms of the Villa Nydia in the years he could not remember. The household had moved, by then, into a slightly larger house, and would, before the decade was over, move down to a flat in Córdoba where the older children could go to school, and Celia had, by the time the boy was fifteen, become a member of small political circles in the city which her husband was not always told the precise meeting times of, and she read at those meetings papers she had written in the evenings at the kitchen table after the children had been put to bed, and she came back from them, on certain nights, with the small hard-set expression of a woman who has spent the evening in the company of people who took her seriously and was now coming back into a household in which the taking of her seriously was not the default state of the air.

She watched her son. She watched him in the way only she watched him, because the father watched him with the residue of the night-drives in the look, and the brothers and the sister watched him with the small wariness younger siblings reserve for an elder who has been organizing the family's attention since they were born, and the rugby teammates watched him with the half-laughter that had been replaced, by the second season, with a quiet that none of them ever named. She watched him with none of those. She watched him with the cleared sight of a woman who had been the wrong kind of girl for the convent and had paid the cost of having been the wrong kind of girl and who was, by the time he was fifteen, watching her son build the wrong-kindness of his own that he was going to spend the rest of his life paying the cost of.

She saw the rugby. She saw what the rugby was. She saw that her son was punishing the body that had punished him, and that the punishment, in the architecture of the boy's interior, had been organized into a kind of theology before the boy had had any access to a theology in any other form. She saw the reading. She saw what the reading was. She saw that the boy was constructing, out of the books on the shelves and the books she read aloud and the books he found in his uncle's library when nobody was watching, an inventory of selves he was rehearsing in his own room without yet having committed to any of them, and that the rehearsal was the rehearsal of a boy who intended, in some way the boy himself had not yet permitted himself to articulate, to enter a public stage on which the right self, when arrived at, would be performed. She saw the notebook. She saw the three columns of vocabulary. She saw the smaller hand in the back of the book where he wrote the sentences he did not show her, and she did not, on any of the occasions when she had the opportunity to read the smaller hand, do so, because there were certain forms of mothering she had elected not to perform, and one of them was the form that read what its son did not show.

She saw the boy building himself. And she did not stop him.

This is the painful thing, and it has to be said in the language the chapter has built up to it, because it is the form love took between the two of them and would continue to take until the end. She did not stop him. She did not stop him for the same reason she had not been able to be stopped, when she was sixteen, by the nuns who had attempted to make of her the kind of daughter the Sacred Heart was equipped to certify. She knew, by the long instruction of her own life, that a person who has set herself to build herself out of the materials available cannot be stopped from doing it by another person who loves her, and that the only effect the loving person can have, if the loving person attempts the stopping, is to be cut out of the building. She did not intend to be cut out. She intended to remain in the room while the building was performed, and to hand the boy, on the occasions when he reached for them, the materials, and to refrain, on the occasions when she could see clearly what he was making, from telling him what she could see. The refraining was the love. It was not a soft love. It is, the narrator wishes to suggest, one of the harder forms love takes in this kind of household, and a reader who has been the recipient of it will recognize the description without it having to be elaborated.

He felt it. He felt the seeing under the refraining, and he felt the love under the seeing, and he did not, in any letter that survived from his adolescence, ever name what he was feeling, but he wrote to her, when he was finally away from her, in a voice he wrote to nobody else in, and the voice would persist in the letters from Mexico and from the mountains and from the embassies and from the small rooms in the last months in Bolivia, in a register that he reserved, through everything that happened to him in the next twenty years, for her.

In January of nineteen forty-four, when he was fifteen, the city of San Juan, in the province of San Juan, in the foothills of the cordillera on the far western side of the country, was destroyed by an earthquake. The earthquake came in the early evening on a Saturday. It lasted, according to the seismographs that survived, less than half a minute. In that half a minute it brought down the greater part of the city of San Juan, in which approximately ninety thousand people lived, and it killed approximately ten thousand of them, depending on which count one credited, in the way that earthquakes in poor provinces of poor countries in the middle decades of the twentieth century killed: by the falling-in of walls, by the collapse of small adobe houses on the people sleeping or eating or sitting in them, by the slow death afterward of those whose injuries the medical facilities of the province were not equipped to address, and by the small additional deaths from the fires and the broken water lines and the inability of the relief, when it came, to reach in time the places it had been dispatched to.

The boy, in Alta Gracia, in the room with the books on the shelves above the bed, heard about the earthquake the way the rest of the country heard about it, which was by the radio in his father's study. He listened. He listened for several hours. He listened the way he had learned to listen to his own chest, which was to say: with the door of his attention all the way open, and with the small sense that the noise he was attending to was a noise he would be required, by some part of himself that was not yet in command of him, to do something about.

He went, in the next weeks, to the collection points that had been set up in Córdoba for the relief of the city, and he carried boxes, and he sorted clothing, and he did the small useful things that a boy of fifteen with weak lungs could be permitted to do in a city far from the damaged one. He did them with a particular concentration that several of the adults in the collection points would, later, remember without being entirely able to name what it had been. He did not, in the weeks he worked at the collection points, talk about the earthquake at the dinner table at home, although the earthquake was talked about there by everyone else. He did not write about it in the notebook, or did not write about it in the part of the notebook that was preserved. He worked. He carried. He sorted. He went home in the evenings and lay on his bed and listened to his chest, and the chest, in the weeks of the relief work, was no worse than it usually was, and was, by his account in a single letter to a cousin which survived, perhaps a little better.

It was the first time, in his life, that the noise outside his body had been larger than the noise inside it. This is the small observation the chapter wishes to make about the San Juan earthquake, and it makes it carefully, because the literature on him will, in due course, attempt to attach to the earthquake a great deal of weight that the earthquake cannot bear. The earthquake did not radicalize him. The earthquake did not, in any letter that survived, produce any sentence about politics or empire or the poor that he had not already been producing in some form. The earthquake did one thing, and only one thing, and the one thing matters. It moved the noise. It moved the noise from the interior, where it had been since he was two years old, to the exterior, where there was, in January of nineteen forty-four, suddenly more of it than even his chest could supply, and the boy who had been listening to his own breath since he was old enough to listen to anything turned the listening, for the first time, all the way outward, and found that the listening continued to work, and that the equipment he had built on his own breath was equipment that would also receive the noise of a city he had never been to that had fallen down at the foot of mountains he had not yet seen.

He went back to Alta Gracia. He went back to the rugby. He went back to the books. He sat at the table with his mother and the conversation at the dinner table resumed, with the small additional ingredient, after January of nineteen forty-four, of a noise that had been let in from outside and that did not entirely go back out.

In nineteen forty-eight he matriculated at the medical school of the University of Buenos Aires.

The choice of medicine was not, in his own letters and in the letters of his mother, the obvious choice that the later biographers would make it. He had thought, for a time, of engineering, which was his father's discipline and which the family expected. He had thought, briefly, of literature, which was his mother's discipline and which he had been practicing in the notebook for ten years. He had chosen medicine, in a conversation with his mother in the kitchen of the flat in Córdoba in the spring of nineteen forty-seven, for reasons that he gave to her then in the language available to a nineteen-year-old, and the reasons were the small reasons: he wished to be useful, he wished to understand the body that had been the central instrument and the central problem of his life, he wished to do something his father would not be able to disparage as a scheme and that his mother would not be able to disparage as a pose. Those were the reasons he gave her. There were others. He did not give her the others because he did not, in the spring of nineteen forty-seven, have access to them.

The narrator, having been given by the rest of his life access to them, can give them here, and will give them, with the caveat that the giving is the narrator's and that the boy, at nineteen, was not in possession of the sentences that follow.

He chose medicine because medicine was the discipline in which the body, which had spent twenty years closing its small passages against him without explanation, could be approached by the mind, which had spent the same twenty years building itself in the room next door. The choice was the first move in the larger move that the rest of his life would be the continuous performance of, which was the move of taking the weakness that the body had given him at two years old and converting it, by an effort of will renewed every morning for the next thirty-nine years, into a purpose that the weakness was not permitted to outlast. Medicine was the first form of the conversion. It was the form a nineteen-year-old could see. The other forms were behind it in a line he could not yet see the end of, but the line was already drawn, and the drawing of it had been performed in the rooms of the Villa Nydia and in the long nights of the breath that did not come and in the rugby field beside the railway line and at the collection point in Córdoba in January of nineteen forty-four, and it had been drawn, more than by anyone else, by the woman who had read aloud in the chair beside his bed and had not stopped him.

He went up to Buenos Aires. He took a room. He bought the books and the white coat and the small instruments and the apron of the medical student, and he went to the first lecture in the great cold lecture hall of the Faculty of Medicine on the calle Paraguay, and he sat at the back, where his chest could find the door, and he opened the notebook, and he began to write.

He wrote, in the back of the notebook in the smaller hand, a sentence he did not show to anyone. The sentence is not preserved in any letter and is not, in fact, known to have been written, and the narrator is, by introducing it here, taking a small liberty that the chapter is willing to take because the sentence is the sentence the chapter has been moving toward and because the boy, at nineteen, in a lecture hall of the Faculty of Medicine in Buenos Aires in the early winter of nineteen forty-eight, was at the point in his life at which a sentence of this kind becomes available to be written, even if it is not written, and even if the sentence that is written in its place is a different sentence and a smaller one.

The sentence the chapter wishes to suggest he was, in that hour, in some not-yet-articulate way, beginning to be able to write, is this: that the body's weakness was the thing he would have to convert into a purpose, or it would eat him.

He did not write it that way. He wrote, in a postcard to his mother from the room he had taken on the calle Araoz, that the lectures were dull and the professors competent and the city colder than he had remembered and that he was well, and the asthma was no worse than it had been in Córdoba, and the books were on the shelf above the bed, and he had begun, in the evenings, a new notebook. He did not write to her what the new notebook was for. He did not, on the evidence of the postcard, yet know.

But the notebook was the form the conversion was going to take, in the next years and in the years after them, in the form of the journal of a journey not yet undertaken and of a self not yet entered, and the conversion was already in motion in him, and his mother, when she received the postcard in the flat in Córdoba and read it twice and folded it and put it on the shelf above the radio, saw the conversion in the sentences he had not written and looked, briefly, at the window, and did not, when his father came in from the office and asked her what was in the postcard, tell him.

She had been, for the longer part of fifteen years, the one person in the household who saw what was happening to the boy. She continued, in the postcard on the shelf above the radio, in the early winter of nineteen forty-eight, to be the one person who saw it. The not-telling was the love. The boy, in the room on the calle Araoz, opened the notebook, and began.
