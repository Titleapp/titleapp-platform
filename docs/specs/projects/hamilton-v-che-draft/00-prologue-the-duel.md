# Prologue — The Duel

The morning came up over the Hudson with the slowness of a thing reluctant to arrive, and there was on the river a low fog that did not lift but only thinned, and through it the cliffs of Weehawken stood as a darker shade of the same gray, and the sound of the water under the boat as it crossed was the sound water makes when no one is listening to it and the men who hear it have stopped pretending to. He was not a tall man. He had never been a tall man. In the boat, sitting forward with his coat folded across his knees and his hands set one upon the other in the way of a man who has been told all his life to compose his hands, he was, that summer morning, the same approximate size he had been when he stepped onto the wharf at New York eight and twenty years before, a boy from an island nobody on this shore could find on a map without looking, and what he carried into the boat with him on the morning of the eleventh of July was not so different from what he had carried into the city as a younger man: a name he had made himself, a debt of language, a fear that did not have a name in any of the languages he spoke, and the particular kind of pride that, in a certain order of man, does the work that other men ask of God.

The boatmen rowed. They had been told not to look and they did not look, or they looked at the water, which is the same thing. There were two seconds — Pendleton with him in the boat, Van Ness across the river with the other party — and there was a surgeon, Doctor Hosack, who had agreed to attend and who had been instructed, by a custom older than the country whose laws he served, to turn his back when the moment came, so that he might later, in a court of law that nobody intended to convene, swear truthfully that he had seen nothing. A man could be killed in this country, by another man, in front of witnesses, provided the witnesses had agreed not to witness. That was the arrangement. That was the practice. That was, in the summer of the year eighteen hundred and four, the way certain quarrels between certain kinds of men were resolved on the wooded ledges above the river that separated the two states which between them had made him.

It is worth saying, before going further, that he was afraid. He was very afraid. The records will say later, and his second will write later, that he was composed, that he was steady, that he spoke pleasantly of the morning and remarked upon the river. He did speak pleasantly. He did remark upon the river. That is what a certain kind of man does when he is afraid. The composure is a kind of nakedness, if you know how to look at it. He had spent his life teaching himself the composure of men who had inherited it from their fathers, and he had, by the morning of the eleventh of July, mastered the imitation so completely that even he had begun to mistake it for the thing itself. But the body knows. The body always knows. His hands were dry where they should have been damp and damp where they should have been dry, and there was at the back of his throat a thinness, a kind of altitude, which is the body's way of saying that it understands the situation better than the mind that rides upon it.

He had written letters the night before. He had written to his wife and he had written, separately, to be opened only in the event, a longer letter that was a kind of accounting — of his finances, of his beliefs, of his decisions — and which he had composed in the voice of a man already addressing the world from the other side of an event he had not yet permitted himself to name. He had told Eliza in the letter that he loved her, which he did. He had told her that he had done what conscience required, which was a thing he had not done and could not have done, but which he believed at the moment of writing because the writing was the only place in his life he had ever told the truth without first translating it into the language of the room in which he stood. He had written, also, that he had resolved not to fire. He had resolved, he wrote, to throw away his shot. He had resolved this for moral reasons, for reasons of Christian conviction, for reasons of personal integrity, for reasons which would, in the letter, stand as the public face of a private calculation that could not be written down because the writing of it would have destroyed the man who wrote.

The private calculation was this. If he fired and missed, he was a man who had tried to kill another man and had failed, and the failure would be the public memory of him. If he fired and struck, he was a man who had killed the sitting Vice President of the United States, a man with whom he had served and dined and corresponded for nearly two decades, and the killing would be the public memory of him. If he did not fire, and was struck, he was a man who had walked, with open eyes and open hands, into a death that had been arranged for him by a quarrel he had not been able to put down, and the walking would be the public memory of him, and the public memory would, by a kind of alchemy he understood better than any man of his generation, become the private memory of the country, and the private memory of the country was a thing he had spent his adult life arranging.

He had not written that. He could not have written that. But it was the shape of the thing under the letter, the way a stone is the shape of the thing under the water that the water is moving over.

The boat touched the rocks. There was a small landing there, and above it a path that climbed through scrub and laurel to a ledge perhaps twenty feet above the water, a clearing of perhaps twenty paces in length and ten in width, which had been used for this purpose many times before and would be used for it many times after, including, three years later, for the death of another man, who had been the elder son of the man who would step out of the boat now. He did not know that. He could not have known it. But the place knew it. Places, in his experience, often knew the things that the men who walked across them did not.

He climbed the path. His shoes were the shoes of a lawyer, thin-soled, made for parlors and benches, and they slipped on the wet stone, and Pendleton went up behind him with the case of pistols under one arm, and the surgeon went up behind Pendleton, and behind them all the boatmen waited at the landing with their hats off, because they had been instructed not to look, and a man who has been instructed not to look will often take his hat off, as if the not-looking required a kind of reverence in order to hold.

At the top of the path he stopped and put his hand briefly against a tree. He stood there a moment. He had not eaten. He had not slept, or had slept the way men sleep before such mornings, which is not sleep but an imitation of sleep, the way his composure was an imitation of the composure he had spent his life learning. He thought, in the moment with his hand against the tree, of his son. Philip had been twenty when he died on this same ledge, struck by a ball from a man named Eacker, in a quarrel about a speech, in a quarrel, that is, about words, which were the things he had given his son to fight with because they were the only weapons he himself had ever owned. He had told Philip, three years before, to delope. To throw away his shot. To stand and not fire and trust the morality of the gesture to carry him through. Philip had stood. Philip had not fired. Eacker had fired, and Philip had died slowly, at his father's house in Greenwich Village, in a bed where his mother and his sisters and his brothers had stood around him and watched him go, and the father, who is the man with his hand on the tree at the top of the path now, had lain across his son's body and made a sound which his wife, in the only remark she would ever publicly make about the day, would describe simply as "the sound." She did not give it any other name. There was no other name for it.

He thought of that. He thought of it briefly, the way a man on the gallows thinks of his childhood. Then he took his hand from the tree and walked into the clearing.

The other party was already there. Burr stood at the far end of the ledge with his second beside him, both of them in dark coats, both of them composed in the way that men who have been there before are composed, and Burr lifted his hat in a small grave courtesy and the man at the near end of the ledge lifted his hat in return, and the courtesy was real. The courtesy was always real. That was the strange and terrible thing about these mornings, which the men who arranged them understood and the women who survived them never quite did: that the men who came to kill each other on these mornings often loved each other, or had loved each other, or had at any rate spent the better part of their adult lives shaping themselves in response to each other, which is a thing not entirely distinguishable from love by anyone who has ever tried to draw the line. He had served with Burr. He had argued with Burr. He had, in print and in private, said things about Burr that no man in the eighteenth century could say about another man and expect to remain alive, and he had said them because he believed them, and he had said them, also, because saying them had become the shape his ambition took when ambition no longer had any other shape available to it. He was not sure, in the moment of lifting his hat, which of the two he had been doing. He suspected, in a part of himself he did not consult often, that there was no difference. That belief, in a certain kind of man, is the costume ambition wears so that it may walk in public.

It is necessary to say something here about why men do this. Why this particular kind of man, in this particular kind of country, in this particular generation, walks into the morning toward a death that has been arranged for him by his own pen. The histories will offer answers. They will say it was the code of honor, which had migrated from the parlors of London and Edinburgh into the wilderness republics on the western edge of the Atlantic, and which by the early years of the new century had become the way a certain class of man performed the seriousness of his own existence. They will say it was the politics, the elections, the pamphlets, the dinner table remarks reported back through chains of correspondence until they arrived, days or weeks later, in the form of an offense that could no longer be set aside. They will say it was the personal: that the two men disliked each other, that they had reasons to dislike each other, that the dislike had accumulated over twenty years until it became a substance heavy enough to require disposing of in the way men of that class and that century disposed of such substances, which was to carry them across a river at dawn and burn them in the flesh of one of the two parties to the quarrel.

All of those things are true. But under all of those things is another thing, which the histories will be less comfortable saying, and which the men themselves would have been unable to say even if asked, because the saying would have unmade them: the willingness, in this particular kind of man, to choose a symbolic death over an ordinary survival. It is not common. Most men, given the choice between living and dying for the appearance of a thing, will choose to live. But some will not. Some, when the cost of withdrawing from the appearance becomes higher than the cost of standing inside the appearance until it kills them, will stand inside it. They will stand inside it knowing what it costs. They will stand inside it precisely because of what it costs. The cost, for that order of man, is not the price. The cost is the proof.

He had been, all his life, an outsider who had taught himself to stand inside. He had come to this country from a small island in the sugar latitudes where the only future available to a boy of his birth had been a clerk's stool in a counting house and a death from yellow fever before the age of thirty. He had escaped that future by a letter, a piece of writing about a hurricane, which a few men in a small West Indian town had read and decided was the writing of a boy who deserved to be sent away from the island and educated in the North. He had been sent away. He had been educated. He had married a woman whose family had been in this country for one hundred and fifty years before his arrival in it. He had founded a treasury, a banking system, a coast guard, a manufacturing policy, a national debt, a financial architecture which would, three centuries after his death, still be running underneath the daily life of every person who used a piece of paper money on the continent. He had done all of that, and he had done it knowing that the men around whom he had done it had never, not once, not in a single private letter or public speech or table remark, forgotten that he had been born on a small island to a woman who was not married to his father. He had done all of it from the inside of an appearance that could be taken from him at any moment by any man who chose to remind the room that the appearance was not the same as the substance underneath it.

Burr had reminded the room. Or had been said to remind the room. Or had been said, by another man, to have said something in a dinner that was said to have meant a thing about him, and the said-thing, traveling through the postal arteries of the republic, had arrived at his door in the form of an offense he could not absorb without losing the appearance he had spent his life standing inside. He could not absorb it. He could absorb almost anything. He had absorbed the bastardy and the island and the death of his mother in a bed beside his own when he was eleven or thirteen, depending on which date you believed for his birth, and he had absorbed the disappearance of his father and the cousin who hanged himself and the aunt who died and the uncle who died and the grandmother who died, which is to say that he had absorbed an entire family disassembling around him before he had finished growing into the body that would carry him through the rest of his life. He had absorbed Maria Reynolds and the pamphlet he had written about her and the public ruin of his domestic reputation, which he had brought down on his own head by the act of writing it. He had absorbed Philip on the ledge above the river, in the same place where he was standing now, struck by a ball in the body which he had given his son and which his son had given back to him in pieces over the course of fourteen hours in the bed in Greenwich Village. He had absorbed all of that. But he could not absorb the suggestion that he had not, after everything, become the man he had spent his life pretending to be. That was the absorption his life could not survive. The absorption was the death of the appearance, and the death of the appearance was the death of the man, because there had never been, since the morning he stepped onto the wharf at New York at the age of seventeen or so, any other man underneath.

Pendleton and Van Ness paced off the ground. Ten paces. Ten paces is not far. A man's voice, at ten paces, carries with the same volume it carries across a parlor. A man's face, at ten paces, can be read for everything that is in it. The two principals took their places. The case was opened. The pistols were presented. He chose one. He chose, the histories will say later, the one with the hair trigger, which his second had warned him against using, which he had insisted on, which would later be argued about by men who had not stood on the ledge above the river at seven in the morning on the eleventh of July with the fog thinning slowly off the water and the cliffs of Weehawken green and silver above them and the cicadas, even at that hour, beginning their long unbroken note in the trees.

He raised the pistol when he was told to raise it. He stood with his side toward the other man, as the practice required, presenting the narrowest possible aspect of his body to the ball that was about to be fired at it. He had been told, by men who had stood on such ledges before and survived them, to fix his eye on a point above and behind the other man's shoulder, because the eye of the other man was a thing that, looked at, would unsteady the hand. He looked at the point above and behind the other man's shoulder. The point was a tree. The tree, he noticed, with a clarity that came on him very suddenly the way clarity sometimes does at such moments, was the same kind of tree he had put his hand against three minutes before, at the top of the path, when he had thought of his son.

He had resolved not to fire. He had written it. He had told Pendleton. He had told himself, the night before, in the letter, in the language of a man who already saw himself from the far side of an event he had not yet permitted to occur. He had resolved it. The resolution, like the composure, like the appearance, like the steady hand and the dry eye and the pleasantly remarked-upon river, was a costume that had been on him so long he no longer remembered putting it on.

The word was given.

The histories will argue, for two centuries afterward, about what happened in the next three seconds. They will argue about who fired first. They will argue about whether he fired into the air on purpose or in reflex or in the involuntary recoil of a body that had been told to fire and could not quite hold itself back from doing so. They will argue about the angle of the ball that struck him, which entered above the right hip and passed through the liver and lodged against the second lumbar vertebra and would, the surgeon said later, have killed any man it struck, regardless of the care given afterward. They will argue about whether Burr saw him fire or did not see him fire, whether Burr's ball was aimed at the body or at the air above the body, whether Burr knew what he was doing or did not know, whether the killing was murder or duel or accident or fate, which is the word men use when they have run out of the other three.

The arguments are the arguments. They will go on. They will outlast every man who has ever taken a position in them. They will outlast, in fact, the country in which they are being conducted, although that country does not yet know that, and would not, on the morning of the eleventh of July eighteen hundred and four, have understood the sentence even if it had been spoken to it.

What can be said, on the ledge, in the moment, with the cicadas beginning their note in the trees and the fog thinning off the water and the cliffs green and silver above the river that divided the two states which between them had made him: what can be said is that he stood. He stood inside the appearance he had built. He stood inside the costume of the composed man. He stood inside the courtesy he had lifted his hat with. He stood inside the resolution he had written down the night before and could not quite remember the reason for. He stood inside the country he had invented, which was about to outlast him by a length of time he could not have estimated and would not have wished to. He stood there. He stood there with his side toward the other man and his eye on the tree above and behind the other man's shoulder, and he understood, in a way he had not permitted himself to understand at any earlier moment of the morning or of the night before or of the twenty-seven years since he had stepped onto the wharf at New York with nothing in his pockets but the letter that had sent him there, that the thing he had built was a thing that did not require him in order to keep being built.

He knew the system would survive him. He walked anyway.
