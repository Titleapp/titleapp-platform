# Chapter 25 — Optimization

His name was Lin Zhenyuan. He was sixty-three years old, in the kind of general health a man of his station was expected to be in at that age, possessed of a still mostly black head of hair which his barber, who came to him in his office on the fourth Tuesday of each month, maintained at a length that had not measurably changed in twenty-two years. He stood five feet and nine inches tall, and his weight, which was the weight a man of his height in his ministry was expected to be, had been the same weight, within a margin of two kilograms, for the last fourteen years, and his body, having long ago consented to the discipline he had imposed on it, no longer attempted any variations. He wore, on the morning the chapter opens, a dark gray suit of a single-breasted cut and a white shirt and a tie of a near-black color whose pattern was not visible at any distance greater than one meter, and his shoes were black and laced, and his face was the face he had presented at meetings in that ministry for thirty-one years, which was a face arranged to convey nothing that had not been written, in advance, into the brief in front of him.

He had been born, in a year that does not concern us, in a small inland city in a province south of the Yangtze, in a household that had been quietly punished and then quietly rehabilitated by the decade-long event whose name the rest of his life would be conducted in the long shadow of. His grandfather had been a teacher of mathematics at a provincial normal school. His father had been a clerk in a state-owned enterprise that produced, depending on the year, agricultural machinery or military components or both, and the father's career, which had begun before the great event and resumed afterward, had been arranged around a small set of survivals that the father did not, in any year of his life, describe to his son in any greater detail than was necessary for the son to understand that certain words were not used in their house and certain photographs were not on the walls and certain relatives were not visited and certain questions, when they arose at school, were to be answered with the sentences the father had taught him to answer them with. He had learned, by the age of six or seven, that survival was a matter of arranging things correctly. He had learned, by the same age, that the arranging was the central daily activity of an adult life, and that the difference between an adult who arranged well and an adult who arranged poorly was not visible to the children of either, until the moment, in a given year, when the difference suddenly became the only thing that was visible.

He was an only child. He had grown up in a two-room apartment on the third floor of a five-story building of the kind constructed in great quantity in the cities of that province in the late nineteen-sixties, and the apartment had given onto a courtyard in which other children had played the games children play in such courtyards, and he had stood at the window watching them play more often than he had gone down to play with them, because his mother had told him that the children in the courtyard were not the children with whom his life was going to be conducted, and the mother, although she had not articulated the basis for the assessment to her son, had been correct.

He was educated, from the age of six, in the schools of his city, and then, from the age of eleven, in the boarding school of the provincial capital to which a small number of boys of his year were sent on the strength of an examination he sat in a hall with two hundred and forty other boys, of whom he came out third. He had been third out of two hundred and forty at ten. He had been second out of nine hundred at thirteen. He had been first out of twelve thousand at seventeen. He had been admitted to the university in Beijing whose name does not need to be given here. He had read, in that university, engineering, then economics. He had been sent, at the end of the nineteen-eighties, to a graduate program in a country he had not previously visited, in a discipline that combined applied mathematics with the analytical instruments of macroeconomic planning, and he had returned, three years later, with a degree and a small set of acquaintances among other returning graduates of similar programs, several of whom would, by the time he was forty, be sitting at adjacent positions in adjacent ministries in the city in which he had spent his entire adult life.

He had not stayed abroad. He had been one of perhaps two hundred Chinese graduate students in his field in the country where he was studying in the years in question, and of that two hundred a measurable majority had not returned, and the not-returning had been understood by the ones who returned and the ones who did not as the central moral question of their cohort, although it had not been described in those terms by either side. He had returned. He had returned in the spring of his twenty-eighth year, with two suitcases and a wooden box of books, and he had reported to a building on a wide avenue in the city in which the rest of his life would be conducted, and the building had absorbed him, and the absorption had been the central absorption of his life, and he had not afterward been seen, in any meaningful sense, by anyone outside the building who did not work in another building of the same kind.

It is necessary now to say something about the buildings, because the chapter cannot proceed honestly without an account of the surface on which he had built his career. The buildings sat in a quarter of the capital that had been laid out for the purpose, in a grid of wide avenues and walled compounds, and inside each compound were the offices of one of the dozen ministries and the hundred state-owned enterprises and the thirty research institutes that, between them, conducted the planning of the country's economic life. The buildings were not, on the outside, distinguished from one another in any way that a visitor would have remarked on. They were of a height the regulations permitted, of a color the practice had settled on, which was a pale stone or a pale concrete or a glass curtain whose tint was the same pale gray as the stone. The interiors were arranged, with small variations, along the same plan: a lobby with a desk and two guards in soft-soled shoes, a bank of elevators ascending to floors whose numbers were not displayed in the lobby, and on the floors at the levels at which the policy was conducted, a series of small offices with desks and computers and shelves of bound volumes which were the records of decisions taken at that level in the previous decades. He had walked into one such lobby in the spring of his twenty-eighth year. He had walked out of one such lobby, on most evenings, at a time between nineteen-thirty and twenty-one-fifteen, for the thirty-one years afterward.

This was his life. The chapter records the fact before going on to the rest. He had built it. He had built it himself, in the sense that men of his class and his decade in his country who built things built them, which was to say: he had absorbed, in the small daily increments by which the absorption was permitted, the discipline of the buildings, and the discipline had absorbed him in return, and the absorbing and the being-absorbed had been the same operation, and the operation had been the work of his life. The buildings were the country's machinery. He had become, by stages and over years, one of the men who maintained it. It would not be accurate to say that he loved this work. It would not be accurate to say that he had ever, in any single day of the thirty-one years, asked the question of whether he loved it. The question was the wrong instrument. The work was the work. He did it.

The chapter opens on a morning in late spring. He woke at five-forty, three minutes after the wife in the adjacent bed had risen and gone into the bathroom, in the manner of men whose bodies have, over years, calibrated themselves to the cycles of the household around them without requiring any conscious arrangement to be made. He lay on his back in the bed. He listened, in the half-light, to the sound of the city outside the windows, which at that hour was the sound of the early trucks and the sweepers and the distant exhalation of the air-handling units on the roofs of the buildings on the avenue, and he listened, also, to the sound inside the apartment, which was the sound of his wife in the bathroom and the sound of the housekeeper in the kitchen and the sound of nothing else, because the daughter had been gone for sixteen years and was, that week, in a city on another continent, in a position at an institution whose name he had memorized but had never visited.

He did not reach for any device on the table beside the bed. The chapter wishes to record this small absence carefully, because the absence is the structural inverse of the morning of the man in the previous Modern chapter, and the inversion is one of the small mechanisms by which this book is being assembled. There was no device on the table beside the bed. There was a glass of water and a small porcelain dish in which he placed his watch at night, and the dish had been the dish of his mother and was now the dish of his bedside table, and the placement of the watch in the dish each night was one of the small ritual movements by which he had arranged the closure of each of the days of his adult life, and the lifting of the watch out of the dish in the morning was the corresponding movement at the other end. He lifted the watch. He fastened it. He did not look at his face in the small mirror above the dresser, because there was nothing he needed to know from his face that the dresser would tell him.

He went into the kitchen. The housekeeper had already prepared, on the small lacquer tray, the two bowls and the small dish of pickled vegetables and the small pot of tea that constituted the breakfast of the household, and the wife sat at the table reading a folder that she had brought home from her own building the previous evening and had not finished reading the previous evening because she had returned at twenty-one-forty and had needed to sleep. She did not look up when he entered. He did not greet her. The not-greeting was not a coldness; it was the form of address that had developed, over the long shared occupation of the apartment, into the morning configuration, and was understood by both of them as the configuration the morning was conducted in. He sat. He ate. The tea was good. He had at some point in the marriage requested, perhaps once, that the tea be of a particular preparation, and the housekeeper had absorbed the preparation, and the preparation had been the morning tea of the household ever since, in the same way the small mechanisms of any household become its inheritance.

His wife's name was Madame Wu. The chapter is going to call her Madame Wu, because in the building she worked in she was addressed as Madame Wu by everyone below the rank of vice-minister, and the address had migrated into the apartment in the way the conventions of the buildings migrated everywhere, and he had himself, on the few occasions in any given month when he needed to refer to her in the company of his driver or his secretary, used the title without first translating it back into the name his mother-in-law had given her. She was, on the morning the chapter opens, fifty-eight years old. She had been the second-ranking official of a ministry that was not his, on a matter that did not adjoin his, for the last seven years. They had been married for thirty-one. They had shared the city for thirty-one and the apartment for fifteen, and they had eaten together perhaps twice a week, on the evenings when both buildings had released them by twenty-one and neither of them had a dinner of the official kind, and the eating had usually been conducted in the same configuration as the morning, which was the configuration in which two people who knew one another extremely well sat across from each other reading separate folders and exchanging, over the course of the meal, perhaps fourteen sentences, of which six concerned the daughter and four concerned the apartment and four concerned the developments in their respective buildings which they were each permitted to communicate to the other and which they both, by long practice, communicated only at the level of the headline.

There had been, in the first years of the marriage, a different configuration. The chapter wishes to record this before going on, because the record is the only place the configuration now exists, and a record made nowhere is a thing that did not happen, and the configuration happened. There had been a period of perhaps four years, in their thirties, in which they had eaten together five nights of seven, and had gone, on weekends, to the small concerts and the small exhibitions that the city in that decade had begun to offer in greater number, and had taken, in the third of the four years, a holiday on the coast of an island in the south of the country, where he had been photographed by her, on a beach at dusk, in a posture and an expression that no later photograph of him would record, because the apparatus inside which the rest of his life would be conducted did not permit that posture and that expression in any of the rooms it produced photographs in. He had kept the photograph. It was in a drawer of his desk in the apartment, in an envelope that had not been opened in eleven years. She knew it was there. He knew that she knew. They had not, in eleven years, mentioned it.

It is necessary now to say something general about the configuration of state and economy that had organized his career, because the chapter cannot continue with the day without first acknowledging the configuration, and the acknowledgment must be made in literary language, because the language that other writers have used for the configuration in the period this chapter is concerned with is the language of analysis, and analysis is not the register in which this book has been written, and is not the register in which this book will end.

The configuration had developed, over forty years, from an early arrangement by which the economic life of the country had been organized along a small number of broad plans — five-year intervals, sectoral allocations, output targets specified in physical units of tonnage and length and area — into an arrangement by which the planning had been disaggregated into a continuous moving aggregate of decisions taken at the level of ministries and provinces and enterprises and research institutes, all of them coordinated by a set of consultative and arbitral mechanisms that had been built up gradually over the four decades and that were, by the time the chapter opens, distributed across perhaps three hundred bodies of various sizes and constituted, in their totality, the largest sustained exercise in the coordinated allocation of resources that any civilization had ever attempted. The bodies were the configuration. The configuration was the bodies. There was no single plan any longer in which the country told itself what it would do. There were several thousand small simultaneous plans, and the configuration was the arrangement that gathered the projections of all of them and presented them, at any given meeting, to the men in the rooms in which the meetings took place, in an order that had been arranged by mechanisms whose operation was not visible to the men in the rooms.

This will be the language the chapter uses. The chapter is not going to name the configuration by the names that the configuration goes by in the literature. The chapter is not going to name the bodies or the men or the small enterprises that conduct the orderings. The chapter is going to call it the apparatus, or the planning machine, or the buildings, and the chapter is going to do so partly because the names by which the configuration is presently known are not the names by which it will be known in fifty years, when the configuration is either gone or so altered that the names will read as a foreign vocabulary, and partly because the names are not the level at which the chapter wishes to operate. The level the chapter wishes to operate at is the level of what the configuration did to the interior life of the man who was, that morning, eating his bowl of millet across the table from his wife, and what the doing was, and what the doing led to.

A reader who has read the earlier chapters of this book may already have noticed the resemblance. The chapter wishes to name it, because the resemblance is the structural argument of the entire third part, and to leave it unnamed at this point would be to ask the reader to do work the chapter is now ready to do for them. What the man across the table from Madame Wu had been doing in his thirty-one years in the buildings on the avenue was, although neither he nor the country he had been doing it for would have used the word, the same work that the small man from a West Indian island had been doing in the offices of the new American treasury in the seventeen-nineties, for the same fundamental reason, which was that a country at a particular stage of its formation requires the construction of a machinery by which the flows of money and material and human attention can be coordinated, in real terms, across a territory too large for any single mind or any single family or any single dynasty to coordinate by older means, and the construction of such a machinery is a labor that consumes the men who undertake it, in measurable physical and psychological ways, and the consumption is the price the machinery extracts in return for being built. The man on the island had paid the price. The man across the table from Madame Wu had paid it as well. The form of the price had been different. The substance had not.

The chapter wishes to note also, and then to set aside, that the man across the table from Madame Wu had been raised on the writings of another man, a man who had been killed in a small clearing in a country in South America in the nineteen-sixties, whose face had hung on the wall of the schoolroom in which he had sat in the sixth grade and whose name had been the name his teachers had spoken with the small respectful pause that signaled, to the children in the classroom, that the man was one of the names by which the moral instruction of the century was conducted. He had read the writings. He had read them in his teens. He had carried, into his adult life, the small set of moral propositions that the writings had laid down in him, and the propositions had remained, in the part of himself in which propositions of that kind were stored, for the entire arc of his career, and he had believed, in any year in which he was asked the question, that he had inherited the project of which the man in the South American clearing had been one of the early conductors, and that the buildings on the avenue and the meetings in the buildings and the planning of the millions of small allocations that the buildings produced were the continuation of that project under conditions the man in the clearing had not lived long enough to encounter. He had believed this. He believed it still on the morning the chapter opens, although the believing had, in recent years, taken on the quality of a thing he held in one hand while doing other work with the other. It is one of the small ordinary deceptions by which a life of his sort is conducted to its end. The chapter records it and moves on.

He arrived at the building at seven-forty-six. The car was the car he had been collected by every morning for the last nine years, since his elevation to the rank at which a car of that kind was issued, and the driver was the driver he had had for the last seven, and the route from the apartment to the building was the route the driver had taken every morning for the seven years, and the journey occupied, by the driver's clock, twenty-one minutes, of which the man in the back seat spent the first eight reading the morning's brief and the next eleven looking out the window at the city in which he had spent his entire adult life and the last two thinking, in a way he had not granted himself permission to think at any other point of his day, about nothing in particular.

He greeted the guards in the lobby by a small nod. He greeted the woman at the desk by a small nod. He greeted, in the elevator, the deputy he shared the elevator with by a small nod and a single sentence concerning the brief, to which the deputy responded with a single sentence concerning a small fact that the brief had not contained and that the deputy had been entrusted with conveying upward in the small private exchange that the elevator was reserved for. He noted the sentence. He nodded. The elevator opened. He walked down the corridor to the office which had been his for the last six years and which had been the office of three men before him, each of whom had occupied it for between five and seven years, and each of whose photographs hung in a small frame in the small adjoining anteroom, in an arrangement that had been the arrangement of that anteroom for thirty years and that did not require any conscious thought from the people who walked past it.

He sat at his desk. He read the brief. He had already read the brief in the car. He read it again, because the second reading was the reading in which the small inconsistencies between the brief and the three earlier briefs that had not been officially distributed surfaced, and the surfacing was the work he was paid to do. He noted the inconsistencies in pencil in the margin of the brief, in the small handwriting he had developed in his late twenties and had not altered since. The inconsistencies were minor. They were within the range of the inconsistencies that briefs of that kind contained on most mornings of most weeks. He was not, that morning, alarmed.

At nine, he walked down the corridor to the conference room.

The room was the room he had been walking into for some portion of every working day of the last six years. The table was a long table of dark wood, and around the table were eighteen chairs, and behind each chair, against the wall, was a smaller chair for the deputy who attended the principal. The principals were the principals he had attended this meeting with, in various permutations, for the last six years. He knew the names of all of them and the names of all of their wives, and he knew the precise sub-portfolios within their broader portfolios that each of them was, that quarter, particularly attentive to, and he knew which of them had taken a position on the action item the meeting was about to ratify and which of them had not yet taken a position and which of them had taken a position and then quietly walked it back when the underlying figures had come in. He had read the figures. He had read the figures before any of them. He had, in the week before the meeting, been the second of three signatories on a small note which had circulated among five offices in three buildings, and which had recommended the action item, and the recommendation had been adopted by each of the five offices, and the adoptions had been transmitted, by mechanisms he had built and that he understood as well as any man in the apparatus understood any of its parts, into the brief that the eighteen principals had read in their cars on the way to the meeting.

The meeting began. The chair, who was the most senior of the eighteen, opened with a small set of formal sentences, in the cadence the chairs of such meetings used. The first principal spoke. He spoke for three minutes. He said the things he was expected to say, in the order he was expected to say them. The second principal spoke. He said the things he was expected to say. The third principal spoke. The fourth.

It was at this point, somewhere between the fourth and fifth principal, that the man in the eighth chair, whose name was Lin Zhenyuan, registered the thing he had been not-quite-registering for several years and that the chapter has been arranging to disclose since the opening sentence.

He registered that the decision had already been arrived at.

It was not a sudden registering. It was the formal acknowledgment, in a precise interior phrase that he did not afterward write down, of a thing he had known for at least three years and had not consented to name. The decision had been arrived at by the underlying processes long before any of the eighteen men in the room spoke. The underlying processes had read the figures, weighted them against the small constraints that the configuration of the apparatus exposed, generated a single recommended action item, transmitted the item upward through the five offices and into the brief, and the brief had arrived in the eighteen cars that morning with the item in its third paragraph in the same eight-word formulation in which the item would appear in the minutes the meeting was about to ratify. The room was performing the ratification of a decision that the processes beneath it had already made. The room had been performing this for some time. The room had been performing it, in fact, for longer than any of the men in the room had served on it.

He listened to the fifth principal. He listened to the sixth. He listened to himself, when his turn came in the rotation, deliver the small set of remarks he had prepared in the car, in the cadence he had developed over thirty-one years for delivering remarks of that kind. The remarks were good remarks. They added, to the eight-word formulation, a small clarification of one of the secondary mechanisms by which the action item would be implemented in the second quarter, and the clarification was, by any standard the room evaluated remarks against, a contribution. The chair noted it. The minutes-keeper made a note. The remark was, in net, well received.

The meeting ended at ten-forty-one. The eighteen men rose in the order they had been seated in. The room emptied. He walked back down the corridor to his office. He sat at his desk. He looked, through the window of the office, at the avenue and at the building opposite and at the small square of sky above the building opposite, which was, that morning, a flat pale color whose exact hue would not, in the years afterward, return to him in any specific memory.

He sat at the desk for a moment. Then he picked up the next brief.

It is necessary to introduce, before the chapter goes on, the deputy who had been seated against the wall behind his chair at the meeting that morning, because she had been there, and a chapter that did not mention her would be choosing not to.

Her name was Su Wenqing. She was thirty-two years old. She had been assigned to his office two years and four months before the morning the chapter opens, on the recommendation of an older colleague of his who taught at the university where she had taken her undergraduate degree before being sent abroad for doctoral study at a department in the country he had himself been sent to, thirty years earlier, by the analogous recommendation of an analogous colleague. She had returned, as he had returned. She had reported to a building, as he had reported. She sat now in a smaller office two doors down from his, where she read, in advance of his readings, the briefs he would later read, and prepared, in the small precise sentences her doctoral training had taught her to compose, the notes that he would, in many cases, sign without altering.

She had been, in the eighteen months of the position, the most reliable instrument his office had contained. He had told her this once, in a single sentence delivered without looking up from his desk, and she had accepted it without looking up from her notebook, and the exchange had been recorded by both of them as the closest thing to a personal communication that the structure of the office permitted them to conduct. He thought, on the rare occasions he thought about her, that she reminded him of someone he could not quite place, and the someone, the chapter wishes to record on his behalf, was the man he had himself been at the same age, in the same building, in another office that had then been occupied by another older man whose name now appeared, in a small frame, in an anteroom on another floor.

She saw him. She saw him in the small interval before the meeting that morning, in the corridor, when his face was the most legible to anyone who had been trained to read it. She had not been trained, exactly. She had developed, in the eighteen months, the equipment by which the face of a man at her superior's distance from his own retirement could be read, and the equipment was, by the time the chapter opens, the most reliable existing instrument for reading that face. She saw, in the corridor, that he had read the brief in the car. She saw that he had read the three earlier briefs that had not been officially distributed. She saw that he had noted the inconsistencies and had filed them in the back of his attention without intending to raise them. She saw, in particular, a thing she had been seeing for some months and that she had not yet decided what to do with, which was that the man in front of her was registering, somewhere in the back of his attention, that the room they were both about to walk into would conduct, that morning as on every morning, a ritual that had ceased some time ago to require any of the rituals' performers. He registered this. He did not name it. She saw the not-naming.

She did not tell him. She did not, in any of the subsequent meetings in any of the corridors in any of the months following, say to him the sentence she could have said, which would have been the sentence that named, on his behalf, the thing he was not naming. She did not say it because there is, in any apparatus of the configuration he had spent his life in, a long-established practice by which the young do not name to the old the thing the old have arranged not to name, and the practice had been the practice of his career when he was her age and would be her practice now that she was the age he had been then. She kept the seeing inside her face. She kept her face above her notebook.

She saw something else, also, which the chapter records because the chapter has been recording such things in earlier chapters and the omission of it here would be a failure of continuity. She saw that she would, in approximately twenty years, be sitting in the chair he was sitting in, and would be in the same configuration with respect to the apparatus he was in the configuration with, and would be the person at the meeting whose remarks would be received by the chair and noted by the minutes-keeper and would not, in any operational sense, alter what the underlying processes had already concluded. She saw this. She had seen it for some months. She had not adjusted her work to it, because she did not yet know what an adjustment would be, and because she was, in the meantime, doing the work very well.

The chapter is going to leave her there, for the moment. The chapter will return to her, briefly, in the chapter that follows this one, in another costume in another room, although the costume will be hers and the room will be hers and the return will be of the same kind by which the women of the earlier chapters of this book have returned in the women of the chapters that came after them.

The chapter is going to compress the rest of the day, because the rest of the day was the day, and the day was, in its general shape, the shape that every day of his recent life had taken, and a long description of the rest would not produce, in the reader, any understanding that has not already been produced by the description of the meeting.

He took meetings. He had lunch in the small dining room of the building, at a round table at which he sat with three other men of his approximate rank, on portfolios adjacent to his, and the four of them performed, over the course of the meal, a small ritual of exchange which the dining room had absorbed over the years to the point that the staff of the dining room had calibrated the cycle of the lunch to within three minutes of accuracy and presented the dishes at the calibrated intervals without any of the four men having to think about the dishes at all. He returned to his office. He read three more briefs. He took a telephone call from a counterpart in another building on the same avenue. He approved certain notes that had been left for his approval. He signed, with the small black-ink pen his daughter had given him on the occasion of his sixtieth birthday and that he used only for the signing of documents he had given internal consent to, the documents that required his signature. He left the building at nineteen-thirty-four.

He was driven home. The driver said nothing during the journey, because the driver had learned, in the seven years, that the journey home was the period of the man's day in which the man's face was the most legible to anyone in the car, and the legibility was not a thing the man wished to be observed. The driver kept his eyes on the road. The man in the back seat looked, through the window, at the city as it moved past him in the dusk, in a posture an observer would have recognized as the posture men of his rank in his country adopted at the end of the working day, which was the posture of a man who had transferred the center of his attention out of the office he had just left and had not yet placed it in the apartment he had not yet arrived at, and who was, for the duration of the journey, in a kind of intermediate territory that the buildings on the avenue had not assigned any specific use to.

The wife was not home. She was at her own building, at a meeting that would not release her until twenty-two-twenty, and would arrive at the apartment perhaps fifty minutes after that, and would, on her arrival, eat a small bowl of something the housekeeper would have left covered on the counter and would go to bed without speaking to him, because by twenty-three-fifteen he would, in the configuration the apartment was running that night, already be asleep, or would be appearing to be asleep, which in that household had, for many years, been a distinction without any operational consequence.

He entered the apartment. He took off his shoes. He placed them on the rack inside the door. He placed his coat on the hook above the rack. He walked down the corridor to the kitchen, where the housekeeper had finished her work and had left the kitchen in the small ordered configuration the housekeeper left the kitchen in every evening, with the table wiped and the dishes drying in the rack and the kettle filled to the line and resting in its station on the stove and, on the lacquer tray on the counter, the small porcelain teapot and the single cup which the housekeeper, over years, had come to leave out on evenings she had been instructed in advance not to leave dinner out, because on evenings of that kind the man preferred, after his arrival, to make his own tea, in a small private operation which the housekeeper had learned, by observation, was the operation he reserved for the end of the days he had chosen to end alone.

The chapter wishes to slow here, because the chapter is going to end now, and the ending is the moment the chapter has been moving toward, and the moment is small enough that it can be missed if the reader is not slowed.

He stood at the counter. He filled the kettle to the line, although the housekeeper had already filled it, because the filling was the first action of the operation and the operation, to be the thing he came home to perform, required all of its actions. He placed the kettle on the stove. He turned the burner. He waited.

It must be said that the waiting was the part the chapter has been arranging itself to record. He waited at the counter in the half-dark of the kitchen, with the small blue ring of the burner the only light at floor level and the small pale rectangle of the window above the sink the only light at chest level, and he stood with his hands on the counter and his eyes on the kettle and he understood, fully and without consolation, the thing he had registered at the meeting that morning.

He had spent his life building a machinery that no longer required him.

The understanding was not new. The chapter has been at pains to record that the understanding was not new, that it had been arriving in pieces for years, that he had refused at each piece to gather them into a single sentence. The arrival of the single sentence was the event of the evening. He had perhaps fifteen working years left in him, by the actuarial estimates of the men in the buildings, and none of the fifteen years would matter to the machinery he had helped to construct, because the machinery had moved past the point at which it required the judgments of any single man and was, in the year the chapter is concerned with, generating its own judgments out of the small ordered surfaces of figures that the underlying processes assembled in the night and presented, in the morning, to the rooms in which the rooms ratified what had already been concluded.

The judgments that arrived in the rooms had been arriving in the rooms for years. He had not been the first to notice. The other men in the eighteen chairs had noticed at various times in various years, and each of them had developed, in his private interior, the small set of accommodations by which a man arranges to continue performing a function the function no longer requires of him, and the accommodations were, in net, the form of the late career of a senior planner in his country in his decade, and were a form that no biographer had yet had occasion to describe and no autobiography would ever describe, because none of the men in the rooms would, on his retirement, write the kind of book in which the description would be possible.

The kettle was beginning to make the small sound a kettle makes before it boils. He listened to it. He had listened to it many times. He had been listening to kettles, in the kitchens of the various apartments his life had passed through, for fifty years, and the sound of a kettle approaching the boil was one of the sounds his body had organized itself around without his ever having decided to organize itself around it, and the sound was, at that hour in that kitchen on the evening of the meeting, the sound that the rest of his life would be organized around if the rest of his life were going to be organized around any sound at all.

He understood, standing at the counter, that the apparatus had become a thing that did not require him in the way a mother does not require, by sixty, the small interventions she once required from the son who has grown up and now lives in a city on another continent. The apparatus had become its own continuation. It had been built, in the years of his career, into a configuration that could continue itself, and the continuing-itself was the central operational fact of the country he had served, and the men who had built it into that configuration were now, in the late years of their working lives, the most thoroughly obsoleted by it of any group in the population, because they were the only ones who could see, from inside, that the configuration was now generating, on its own, the kinds of judgments their work had once consisted of.

He thought, in the way a man thinks at such a moment, which is not in fully formed sentences but in something closer to the shape sentences would have if they were permitted to form, that the man on the island whose work he had unconsciously continued had been spared this. The man on the island had been killed at forty-nine, on a ledge above a river, before the machinery he had built had matured to the point at which it could generate its own decisions. The man in the South American clearing whose project he had believed he was inheriting had been killed at thirty-nine, in a country whose machinery had never been permitted to develop to that point at all. The men in their chairs in the eighteen chairs of his meeting had not been killed. They were sixty, and sixty-three, and sixty-five, and sixty-eight, and they were sitting in the chairs of a machinery that had outgrown them, and they would sit in the chairs for the remainder of their working lives, and the machinery would, for the remainder, ratify their judgments at the surface and generate its own beneath, and the men in the chairs would understand the arrangement and would not name it, and the not-naming would be the form their late careers took.

The kettle boiled.

He took it off the burner. He poured a small portion of the water into the porcelain pot to warm it, and he poured the warming water into the sink, and he placed the leaves into the warmed pot, in the amount his hand had memorized, and he poured the remainder of the water from the kettle into the pot in the slow steady stream that the leaves required, and he replaced the lid on the pot, and he set the pot on the lacquer tray, and he waited the precise interval the leaves required to open, in the kitchen in the half-dark with the burner now extinguished and the window above the sink now the only light.

He carried the tray to the small table by the window of the living room. He sat. He poured. He lifted the cup to his face. The steam touched his face in the way steam from a small porcelain cup touches a face in a room at that hour, which is in a small narrow rising column whose path is determined by the small currents of air in the room and by the small variations in the surface of the tea in the cup, and the column rose toward him and he closed his eyes and let it touch him.

He thought, in the closed half-formed way he had been thinking all evening, that the preparation of the tea was one of the few operations he performed any longer that the apparatus could not, by any extension of its present configuration, perform on his behalf. The figures could be computed. The minutes could be drafted. The judgments could be generated. The briefs could be assembled. The actions could be ratified. The country could be coordinated. The civilization could be conducted. But the tea, in the small porcelain cup, prepared by hand in the half-dark of his own kitchen, with the steam rising in the small narrow column to a face that had spent the day being illegible to the room around it, was a thing the apparatus would not, in any version of itself he could presently imagine, do for him.

He opened his eyes. He looked at the cup. He looked at the steam.

He lifted the cup. He drank.

The tea was good. It was the tea he had been drinking, in some configuration, for the entirety of his adult life. It was the tea his mother had drunk in the two-room apartment in the inland city of his childhood. It was the tea that several thousand other men in several thousand other apartments in several thousand other buildings in the city that night were also drinking, at approximately that hour, in approximately that posture, in the half-dark of their own kitchens or of their own living rooms, each of them having returned from the building he had spent the day in, each of them with the watch in the dish and the coat on the hook and the wife at her own meeting and the daughter in another city and the apparatus moving past him in the night without requiring any further input from him before morning.

He drank a second cup. He drank slowly. The room was very quiet. The city outside the window was very quiet. The buildings on the avenue, behind their walls and their guards and their elevators, were running their underlying processes at the rate the processes were now running them at, in an arrangement that no man on the island and no man in the clearing and no man in the eighteen chairs had been in a position to imagine, and the running was the work that had once been his work, and the work had moved past him, and the moving had been the slow long achievement of his life, and the achievement was the thing he was sitting in the half-dark with the cup in his hand absorbing the full shape of.

He set the cup down. He sat at the table. The steam from the second cup rose into the lamp-light and was visible for a moment as a small thin coiling pale-white column above the rim, and then thinned, and then was not visible, and then the room was as it had been before, with the man at the table and the cup in front of him and the small porcelain pot beside the cup and the city outside the window, and the night, very still.
