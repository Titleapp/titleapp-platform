## 1. Scope and Purpose

This SOP applies to all [Operator] fixed-wing pilots and establishes standardized procedures for single-pilot Part 135 on-demand operations. It supplements the General Operations Manual (GOM) and applicable Aircraft Flight Manuals (AFM). In any conflict, the AFM takes precedence, followed by the GOM, then this SOP.

### 1.1 Intended Use

These procedures are designed as a reference tool and do NOT replace the AFM, GOM, or any regulatory requirement. Pilots are expected to be thoroughly familiar with this document and comply with its guidance.

### 1.2 Revisions

All revisions are controlled by the [Director of Operations / Chief Pilot]. Updated versions are distributed through the company document management system. Pilots are responsible for confirming they hold the current revision.

## 2. Checklist Methodology

### 2.1 Do-Verify Method

[Operator] employs the Do-Verify checklist methodology for single-pilot operations. The pilot performs each action from memory or by following the flow pattern, then uses the written checklist to verify all items have been completed.

**Flow**: The memorized sequence of actions performed from muscle memory or a logical scan pattern (e.g., left-to-right, top-to-bottom across the panel).

**Expanded Checklist**: The written or electronic list used after the flow to verify every item. This is the verification step, not the action step.

### 2.2 Challenge-Response Checklists

Certain critical phases (Before Landing, Approach) use Challenge-Response format where items are verbalized aloud even in single-pilot operations. The pilot challenges themselves and confirms each item.

### 2.3 Checklist Discipline

- Checklists shall not be interrupted. If interrupted, restart from the beginning.
- The pilot will verbally state "Checklist Complete" at the conclusion of each checklist.
- Electronic checklists (if used) are acceptable substitutes for printed versions provided they display the same content.

## 3. Verbal Callouts

Reference: AC 120-71B -- Standard Operating Procedures and Pilot Monitoring Duties for Flight Deck Crewmembers

### 3.1 Purpose

Verbal callouts provide a structured means for the single pilot to Verbalize, Verify, and Monitor critical flight parameters. Speaking actions and observations aloud creates an auditory feedback loop that enhances situational awareness.

### 3.2 Standard Callout Points

Callouts are defined in each aircraft-specific SOP supplement. At a minimum, callouts are required at:

- Takeoff (power set, airspeed alive, rotate, positive rate)
- After Takeoff (gear up, flaps up, autopilot engagement altitude)
- 10,000 ft (lights, pressurization)
- Top of Descent / VNAV capture
- Approach (approach loaded, briefing complete, minimums set)
- 500 ft AGL (stabilized approach gate -- VFR)
- 1,000 ft AGL (stabilized approach gate -- IMC)
- Minimums (runway environment in sight / go around)

### 3.3 Non-Standard Situations

Any deviation from expected parameters shall be verbalized immediately (e.g., "Airspeed low," "Glideslope one dot high," "Unusual vibration").

## 4. Sterile Cockpit

### 4.1 Policy

Sterile cockpit procedures apply below 10,000 ft AGL. During sterile cockpit, all conversation and activity shall be limited to duties directly related to the safe operation of the aircraft.

### 4.2 Applicability

Sterile cockpit applies during:
- Taxi
- Takeoff and initial climb
- Approach and landing
- Any phase the PIC deems critical

### 4.3 Non-Essential Activities Prohibited

During sterile cockpit:
- No personal electronic device use
- No non-essential radio communications
- No administrative tasks
- No EFB activities unrelated to the current flight phase

> **Mission Type: Air Medical** -- Clinical crew will be briefed on sterile cockpit procedures. The pilot will communicate intent to enter sterile cockpit. Medical discussions regarding patient care are permitted but should be minimized to essential coordination.

> **Mission Type: Charter** -- Passenger briefings and service interactions must be completed prior to sterile cockpit phases. No passenger interaction during sterile operations.

> **Mission Type: Cargo** -- Standard sterile cockpit applies. No loading/unloading communications during sterile phases.

## 5. CAS (Crew Alerting System) Responses

### 5.1 General Response Philosophy

1. **WARNING (Red)**: Immediate action required. Execute memory items without delay.
2. **CAUTION (Amber)**: Timely action required. Assess the situation, then follow the appropriate checklist.
3. **ADVISORY (Cyan/White)**: Awareness item. Note the condition, monitor, and take action as appropriate.

### 5.2 Response Procedure

1. Identify the CAS message
2. If a WARNING, execute memory items immediately
3. Verify with the QRH / AFM checklist
4. Record the CAS message in the aircraft logbook post-flight
5. Notify maintenance as required

### 5.3 Multiple CAS Messages

Address in priority order: WARNING first, then CAUTION, then ADVISORY. If multiple WARNINGs appear simultaneously, prioritize aircraft control (Aviate, Navigate, Communicate).

## 6. Automation Management

### 6.1 Four Levels of Automation

| Level | Description | When to Use |
|-------|-------------|-------------|
| Manual | Hand-flying, no autopilot | Low workload, VMC, practice, AP malfunction |
| Basic AP | Heading/altitude hold | Standard cruise, moderate workload |
| FMS Guided | Lateral/vertical navigation coupled | IFR enroute, standard arrivals/departures |
| Combination | Full FMS + AP integration | IMC approaches, high workload phases |

### 6.2 Automation Philosophy

- The pilot must always know what the automation is doing, what it will do next, and be prepared to intervene.
- If automation behavior is not understood, disconnect and fly manually.
- Autopilot shall be disengaged at or above the minimum altitude specified in the aircraft-specific SOP (typically 200 ft AGL for precision approaches, 400 ft AGL for non-precision).
- Autopilot may be engaged above 400 ft AGL after takeoff (aircraft-specific minimum applies).

### 6.3 Mode Awareness

The pilot shall verbalize FMS/autopilot mode changes (e.g., "NAV captured," "Glideslope alive," "VNAV to SPD mode"). Any unexpected mode change requires immediate assessment.

## 7. Special Emphasis Areas

### 7.1 Stabilized Approach Criteria

Reference: GOM Section [X.X]

An approach is considered stabilized when ALL of the following criteria are met:

**By 1,000 ft AGL (IMC) or 500 ft AGL (VMC):**
- On the correct lateral and vertical flight path
- Airspeed within the specified range (target approach speed plus or minus applicable tolerance)
- In the correct landing configuration (gear down, flaps as required)
- Power setting appropriate for the configuration
- All briefings and checklists complete

**Circling approaches**: Must be stabilized by 500 ft AGL or MDA, whichever is higher.

**Mandatory go-around**: If the approach is not stabilized by the applicable gate, a go-around is mandatory. There is no exception.

### 7.2 Dynamic Risk Assessment (DRA)

Reference: 14 CFR 135.617(c)

A DRA shall be completed on the crew scheduling/dispatch system prior to every flight and electronically signed. If the risk score exceeds the established threshold (typically 14 or as set by [Operator]), the pilot shall contact the Operations Control Center (OCC) and the Aviation Manager on Call (AMOC) for consultation before proceeding.

### 7.3 Static Risk Assessment (SRA)

The SRA evaluates longer-term risk factors (fatigue, unfamiliar airports, weather trends). It is completed at the beginning of each duty period and updated as conditions change.

### 7.4 CFIT Prevention

- Terrain Awareness Warning System (TAWS) shall remain operative and armed at all times during flight
- Pilots shall brief terrain hazards during approach briefings
- Minimum safe altitudes shall be verified prior to descent
- Night approaches to unlit terrain require enhanced vigilance and adherence to published procedures

### 7.5 IIMC (Inadvertent IMC) Avoidance

For VFR operations, if IMC conditions are encountered:
1. Maintain aircraft control
2. Transition to instruments
3. Climb to minimum safe altitude
4. Notify ATC
5. File a Safety Report after the flight

## 11. Maintenance Tracking -- Daily Pilot Responsibilities

### 11.1 Daily Status Check

At the beginning of each duty period, the pilot shall verify in the maintenance tracking system:
- Aircraft status is GREEN (no overdue maintenance, no open items preventing flight)
- Review the maintenance due list (typically a 30-day prediction window)
- Review any open discrepancies
- Review any deferred MEL or NEF items
- Verify aircraft location is correct in the system

### 11.2 Color Status System

| Status | Meaning | Action |
|--------|---------|--------|
| GREEN | No overdue items, aircraft airworthy | Normal operations |
| AMBER/ORANGE | Maintenance items approaching due | Plan for upcoming maintenance, notify ACP |
| RED | Aircraft not airworthy | Do NOT fly. Contact AMOC/MMOC immediately |

### 11.3 Aircraft RED Status Conditions

An aircraft is RED when any of the following exist:
- Overdue maintenance inspection
- Open log card package requiring completion before flight
- Open discrepancy that renders the aircraft unairworthy
- Missing required configuration component

### 11.4 Journey Log Entries

Journey logs shall be created in the maintenance tracking system for every flight. Required fields include:
- Departure station and date/time
- Arrival station
- Hobbs time (in/out)
- Number of takeoffs and landings

## 12. Safety and Quality Management

### 12.1 Safety Reports (SR)

A Safety Report shall be filed whenever an operational event occurs that causes or could cause damage, injury, or a hazardous condition. Safety Reports capture immediate safety concerns.

**Required Safety Reports:**
- IIMC events
- TAWS/GPWS warnings during flight
- Bird strikes causing damage
- Any incident or accident
- Near mid-air collisions
- Runway incursions

### 12.2 Quality Management Reports (QM)

Quality Management Reports address workflow improvements, patient care concerns (air medical), process issues, and non-immediate safety observations. QMs drive continuous improvement.

> **Mission Type: Air Medical** -- Required QMs include: refueling with patient on board events, flight deviations with patient on board. QMs are also appropriate for clinical/operational workflow issues.

> **Mission Type: Charter** -- QMs are appropriate for customer service issues, scheduling process improvements, and operational workflow concerns.

> **Mission Type: Cargo** -- QMs are appropriate for loading/unloading process improvements, cargo securing issues, and weight/balance workflow concerns.

### 12.3 SR vs QM Decision Guide

| Situation | Report Type |
|-----------|-------------|
| Damage to aircraft or equipment | Safety Report |
| Injury to person | Safety Report |
| IIMC, CFIT risk, near-miss | Safety Report |
| Patient care concern | Quality Management |
| Workflow or process improvement | Quality Management |
| Equipment malfunction reported to maintenance | Maintenance discrepancy (not SR/QM unless safety concern) |
| Policy non-compliance observed | Quality Management or Safety Report depending on severity |

## 13. Aircraft Airworthiness and Equipment

### 13.1 Controlling Document

The paper aircraft logbook is the controlling airworthiness document. The electronic maintenance tracking system supplements but does not replace the logbook. Any discrepancy between the two shall be resolved in favor of the logbook, and the electronic system updated accordingly.

### 13.2 Post-Maintenance Status Check

After any maintenance action, the pilot shall verify:
- Logbook entry is complete and properly signed (return to service)
- Electronic tracking system reflects the completed maintenance
- Aircraft status is GREEN
- All panels, cowlings, and access doors are secured
- Control surfaces move freely and correctly

### 13.3 Clear to Close (CTC) Inspection

A CTC inspection verifies that all maintenance work has been completed and documented before closing a maintenance event. The pilot participates by:
- Reviewing the logbook entries
- Performing a walk-around with attention to areas of recent maintenance
- Verifying the electronic tracking system matches the logbook
- Confirming the aircraft is airworthy

### 13.4 Aircraft Out of Service (OOS)

When an aircraft is removed from service:
1. Notify the OCC and MMOC
2. Record the reason in the logbook and electronic system
3. Coordinate with the ACP for operational impact (coverage, aircraft swap)
4. The aircraft shall not be returned to service until maintenance has signed off the discrepancy

### 13.5 Maintenance Away from Base

If maintenance is required at a location other than a company maintenance base:
- Contact the MMOC for guidance
- Coordinate with an approved repair station or mechanic
- Ensure proper logbook entries are made
- Verify return-to-service documentation before flying

## 14. MEL Procedures

### 14.1 Overview

The Minimum Equipment List (MEL) is derived from the FAA Master Minimum Equipment List (MMEL) and is operator-specific. It defines which equipment may be inoperative for dispatch under specified conditions.

### 14.2 MEL Item Categories

| Category | Repair Interval |
|----------|----------------|
| A | Per remarks (specific timeframe stated) |
| B | 3 consecutive calendar days |
| C | 10 consecutive calendar days |
| D | 120 consecutive calendar days |

### 14.3 (M) and (O) Procedures

- **(M) Maintenance Procedure**: Requires a certificated mechanic to perform the action before flight.
- **(O) Operational Procedure**: May be performed by the pilot or flight crew before or during flight.
- If no (M) or (O) procedure is listed, no special action is required beyond normal placarding.

### 14.4 Discovering an Inoperative Item

1. Identify the inoperative item
2. Check the MEL for the applicable ATA chapter and item
3. Determine the category and repair interval
4. If an (M) procedure exists, contact maintenance
5. If an (O) procedure exists, the pilot may complete it
6. Create a discrepancy entry in the logbook
7. Ensure the item is deferred in the electronic tracking system
8. Verify the AMOC/MMOC has approved the deferral
9. Confirm aircraft status returns to GREEN in the tracking system

### 14.5 MEL Approval Process

All MEL deferrals require AMOC or MMOC verification. The pilot shall not dispatch on a deferred item until approval is confirmed.

### 14.6 Non-Essential Furnishing (NEF) Program

Reference: FAA Order 8900.10

NEF items are Category D (120-day) deferrals for non-essential equipment and furnishings. The NEF list is maintained as a separate document. New NEF items must be vetted with the AMOC before deferral. Completed NEF items are reported to the FAA Certificate Management Team (CMT).

### 14.7 Prohibited Items

The following items are prohibited on company aircraft:
- Power strips or multi-outlet adapters
- E-cigarettes or vaping devices
- Any item specifically prohibited by the operator or the FAA

## 15. Shift Duties

### 15.1 Duty Time

Reference: 14 CFR 135.267(c)

| Limit | Requirement |
|-------|-------------|
| Maximum duty period | 14 hours |
| Minimum rest before duty | 10 hours uninterrupted |
| Maximum flight time in 24 hours | 8 hours (14 CFR 135.267(c)) |

Commuting to/from the duty station counts as rest time, not duty time.

### 15.2 Reporting for Duty

Pilots shall report for duty rested, fit, and in compliance with 14 CFR 91.17 (alcohol and drugs). Duty time begins when the pilot reports for duty or is required to be available, whichever is earlier.

### 15.3 Unforeseen Delays

Per 14 CFR 135.267, duty time may be extended for unforeseen operational circumstances. Standard ground time allowances:

> **Mission Type: Air Medical** -- Standard ground times: Medical crew transport 1.5 hours, specialty team transport 2.0 hours, ECMO transport 4.0 hours.

> **Mission Type: Charter** -- Standard ground times per company policy. Passenger delays are managed through the OCC.

> **Mission Type: Cargo** -- Standard ground times per company policy. Loading delays are managed through the OCC.

### 15.4 Extended Duty Annotation

If duty time exceeds 14 hours, the crew scheduling system will prompt for an extension reason. Valid extension reasons include:
- Drive time to/from outlying airports
- Patient/passenger transport delays
- Base coverage requirements pending relief pilot arrival
- Unforeseen maintenance

### 15.5 Deadheading

Deadheading (repositioning as a passenger or by ground) counts as duty time but NOT as flight activity time.

### 15.6 Launch Readiness

#### 15.6.1 Aircraft Parking and Positioning
Aircraft shall be parked and positioned for rapid departure. Orientation should facilitate taxi to the active runway with minimal repositioning.

#### 15.6.2 Aircraft Condition
The aircraft shall be maintained in a ready-to-fly condition during the duty period:
- Fuel at the standard ramp fuel level per company policy (aircraft-specific)
- All required equipment installed and operational
- Logbook current and accessible
- EFB charged and updated

#### 15.6.3 Moving Aircraft / Ground Handling
Use extreme caution when moving aircraft on the ground. Follow company procedures for towing, use of aircraft dollies, and ground handling equipment.

### 15.7 On-Coming Shift Change

The on-coming pilot shall complete the following within the first portion of the shift:

1. Clock in via the timekeeping system
2. Brief with the off-going pilot on:
   - Aircraft status (fuel, maintenance, discrepancies)
   - Weather conditions and forecast
   - Active MEL/NEF items
   - Operational issues or pending flights
   - Crew concerns or base issues
   - Relevant NOTAMs
   - Equipment status
   - Any other pertinent information
3. Physical asset quick-check (verify equipment, supplies)
4. Check in via the crew scheduling system
5. Verify aircraft status in the maintenance tracking system (GREEN)
6. Check weather and hazards (AIRMET, SIGMET, NOTAMs, TFRs)
7. Verify EFB/iPad updates are current
8. Check in with the OCC/Communications Center within 15 minutes of shift start, providing:
   - Pilot name and base identifier
   - Aircraft tail number
   - Aircraft status (up/down)
   - Weather conditions
   - Fuel status
   - Any operational limitations
   - Risk assessment score
   - ETE to nearest likely destinations
   - Any other relevant information
9. Conduct crew briefing (if applicable)
10. Inspect base fuel system (if applicable)

### 15.8 Night Shift Duties

The night shift pilot shall close out the maintenance logbook page for the day after midnight and carry forward totals to the next sheet.

### 15.9 Off-Going Shift Change

The off-going pilot shall:
- Be awake and ready when relief arrives
- Prepare a daily shift brief (aircraft, fuel, weather, NOTAMs)
- Ensure the aircraft is clean (cockpit, cabin, windscreen)
- Not depart until the on-coming pilot has checked in via the scheduling system
- Note critical items on the base operations board

### 15.10 Weekly Duties

- Aircraft washing during off-peak times (not during operational readiness hours)

### 15.11 Mid-Month Duties (15th of each month)

- Fuel usage log scanned and submitted to [Operator accounting email]
- First aid kit inspection verification
- Medical equipment airflow checks (if applicable)

### 15.12 End of Month Duties (1st of following month)

- Completed fuel usage log scanned and submitted (include monthly dip reconciliation if applicable)
- Completed fuel inspection form submitted to [Operator maintenance email]
- Start new fuel usage log and fuel inspection form
- Review and sign flight and duty log (no later than the 7th of the following month)

### 15.13 Quarterly Duties

Aircraft audits shall be completed for the primary aircraft and any spare/OOS aircraft at the base. Verify all required equipment is current (QRH, licenses, safety equipment, etc.).

## 17. Post-Flight Debrief

> **Mission Type: Air Medical**

### 17.1 Post-Transport Debrief (PTD)

PTD forms are submitted after every patient flight, including aborted transports. The PTD captures delay factors, timing discrepancies, and any out-of-the-ordinary events.

### 17.2 Generation, Completion, and Review

PTDs are generated by the Communications Center, completed by the line pilot, and reviewed by the ACP. They must be completed prior to leaving the shift during which the flight occurred.

### 17.3 Delay Codes

If the liftoff time exceeds 20 minutes from dispatch, the pilot must select a delay code:

**Aircraft Related Delays:**
- Hangar delay (aircraft must be removed from hangar)
- Ice/snow/frost removal
- Fuel delay
- Pre-flight required
- Mechanical delay (including MEL deferral)

**Transport Related Delays** (Air Medical):
- Transport in progress (aircraft on another flight)
- Waiting on patient
- Waiting on medical crew
- Crew consult (extended hospital coordination)

**General Delays:**
- Resupply medical/equipment
- Aircraft wash or decontamination

**Flight Specific Delays:**
- Extended weather check / flight planning
- IFR-related planning (alternates, NOTAMs, minimums)
- ATC delay
- Communications Center / dispatch delay

### 17.4 Crew Debrief

The pilot and crew must debrief all flights. The debrief should encourage open conversation and ensure all crew members voice concerns. Aborted flights should still generate a debrief and PTD.

> **Mission Type: Charter** -- Post-flight debriefs should capture customer service observations, operational delays, and any safety concerns.

> **Mission Type: Cargo** -- Post-flight debriefs should capture loading/unloading issues, weight discrepancies, and any safety concerns.

## 18. Aircraft Swap

### 18.1 Overview

Aircraft swaps are coordinated through the ACP or higher. The goal is to minimize out-of-service time while ensuring total operational readiness.

### 18.2 Procedures

1. Do not take the base OOS until ready to swap
2. Preflight the new aircraft completely (fuel, maintenance tracking, EFB, logbook)
3. Have the crew preflight as well (if applicable)
4. Maintain the primary aircraft in a ready state during the inspection
5. When ready, notify the crew and call the base OOS for the swap
6. The swap should take no more than 15 minutes
7. Call the OCC with the Return to Service (RTS) notification

### 18.3 Discrepancies

Always proactively report discrepancies on the replacement aircraft. Unreported discrepancies can interrupt operational plans.

## 20. Flight Operations

### 20.1 Receiving Flight Requests

#### 20.1.1 Accepting Flights -- General

Before accepting a flight:
1. Check weather and NOTAMs
2. Verify takeoff and landing performance (including runway condition assessment in winter)
3. Determine if duty time allows completion of the flight to the destination
4. Complete the Dynamic Risk Assessment (DRA) and sign electronically
5. If risk score exceeds threshold, consult with the OCC and AMOC
6. Prepare the aircraft for flight

#### 20.1.2 Accepting Flights Near Shift Change

The OCC will not hold flights for incoming pilots during shift change. If the current pilot cannot complete the flight within duty time:
- The next closest available asset will be dispatched
- Pilots should proactively determine remaining capability as the shift end approaches
- Accept/deny based on ability to reach the destination, not return to base
- Inform the OCC of estimated time-out location for crew transportation arrangements

#### 20.1.3 Duty Time Planning

If duty time restrictions prohibit completing a transport, present alternatives to the OCC:
- Partial transport with ground handoff
- Delivery to an alternate facility
- Combined ground/air transport

#### 20.1.4 Flight Requests

Pilots are expected to respond to flight requests as quickly as possible. Maintain vigilance on weather and operational limitations. Know which sectors (routes/airports) are available for acceptance.

#### 20.1.5 Standby Flight Requests

Standby requests reduce response time by enabling advance preparation:
- Check weather and accept/decline
- Alert crew (time-of-day appropriate)
- Gather equipment
- Prep aircraft (remove from hangar, remove tie-downs)
- Position near aircraft

#### 20.1.6 During Return Leg

If a new flight request is received while returning:
- Assess weather, fuel, and duty time
- Notify OCC if the flight can be accepted
- Close the current flight in the scheduling system and logbook
- Review maintenance due during add-on flights

### 20.2 Preflight -- Communications

- **Comm 1**: Primary airborne (ATC, CTAF)
- **Comm 2**: Secondary (weather, clearance, ground, UNICOM, monitor 121.5)
- **SATCOM** (if equipped): Monitored by OCC at all times by at least one crew member

### 20.3 Before Starting Engine

Before every engine start, the pilot shall complete a 360-degree walk-around and ensure:
- All covers, prop-ties, tail stand, and chocks are removed
- Access panels and doors are securely closed
- Fuel caps are latched
- No leaks, drips, or signs of tampering
- Wing and tail clearance from obstacles
- Clinical/cabin crew aware of engine start

> **CAUTION**: The cargo door is the responsibility of the pilot. Ensure the door handle is in the correct position and locking pins are not extended prior to closing.

The PCL/throttle cover shall not be removed until all required pre-start checks are complete.

### 20.4 Engine Starting

The aircraft before-start checklist must be complete before starting. Observe starting limitations per the AFM.

### 20.5 Taxi

Single-pilot operations require ground checklist items to be completed while the aircraft is stationary, to the maximum extent possible.

Taxi clearance considerations:
- If wing clearance is in doubt, or less than 10 ft from an object, STOP
- Obtain a marshal or verify a clear taxi line with at least 7 ft clearance
- If clearance cannot be confirmed, shut down and physically inspect the route
- On contaminated surfaces (snow, ice, slush, standing water), exercise additional caution
- For complex taxi instructions at unfamiliar airports, enter the route in the EFB before moving

> **WARNING**: Pilots shall NOT operate EFBs during critical phases of flight.

### 20.6 Takeoff

Verbalize the pre-takeoff callout card. Ensure heading and HSI align with the intended departure runway.

### 20.7 Climb and Cruise

Cruise altitude selection considers:
- Direction of travel (hemispheric rule)
- Emergency glide capability
- Performance (endurance)
- Weather
- Traffic
- Special use airspace
- Total flight leg distance

### 20.8 Approach

All pilots will adhere to the Stabilized Approach criteria (Section 7.1). EFB use shall be restricted to essential activities during critical phases.

### 20.9 Landing and After Landing

The After Landing flow may be completed while taxiing. The After Landing checklist shall be completed while stopped. Note any exceedance on the CAS panel before shutdown.

If any exceedance has occurred, the aircraft must be taken out of service (notify OCC and maintenance) until data download and assessment are complete.

### 20.10 Post Flight

- Debrief with crew verbally
- Complete a 360-degree walk-around
- Replace the appropriate PCL/throttle cover
- Check engine oil and tires
- Note obstacles on the ramp
- Record and communicate any maintenance issues promptly
- Do not start, taxi, or fly an aircraft with a recorded exceedance until discussed with maintenance
- Complete fueling and servicing as necessary
- Complete the flight summary in the scheduling system
- Record the flight in the maintenance tracking system and logbook

If leaving the aircraft overnight at a non-base location:
- Chock, gust lock, secure doors (crew, cabin, battery area)
- Pin emergency exit
- Hangar if inclement weather is forecast, or install tail stand

### 20.11 Aircraft Fueling

Aircraft fueling procedures are covered in the [Operator] Fuel Handling Procedures manual.

**Ad-Hoc Fuel** (from a source not owned or contracted by [Operator]):
- Request to see daily fuel inspection records
- If unavailable, carry a water detection kit and fuel sample jar
- Perform a water detection test and clear-and-bright test
- If fuel quality is in doubt, DO NOT dispense into the aircraft

> **NOTE**: If inspection forms are unavailable, a clear-and-bright test AND a water detection test MUST be completed. Dispose of sample fuel after testing. Do not carry sample fuel inside the aircraft cabin.

### 20.12 Bird Strikes

- Use pulse/recognition lights below 10,000 ft on equipped aircraft to decrease bird strike risk
- If a bird strike occurs, land as soon as practicable and inspect
- If a mechanic is available, they will inspect the aircraft
- If no mechanic is available, the PIC inspects and contacts the AMOC through the OCC
- If no damage is found and all parties agree, the aircraft may return to service
- If damage is found, a mechanic must respond to determine airworthiness

> **NOTE**: If a mechanic is dispatched and performs an inspection, a logbook entry must be created regardless of the outcome.

## 21. Communications Center

### 21.1 Specialists and Control

**Communication Specialists (CS)**: Handle inbound calls, customer interaction, internal asset allocation, flight planning, and flight locating. CS do NOT have operational control of flights.

**Operational Control Specialists (OCS)**: Provide Tier Two operational control. OCS have an aeronautical background and assist pilots with decision-making, route hazard assessment, mitigation strategies, and may advise diverting or terminating a flight.

### 21.2 Receiving Flight Requests

Use closed-loop communications when discussing flight requests:
- Always use the words "accepting" or "declining" followed by the route using airport identifiers
- Example: "[Base XX] is accepting the interfacility [KABC] to [KXYZ]."

### 21.3 Company Flight Plans

All flights require a flight plan. For VFR operations, the information provided to the Communications Center fulfills this requirement.

**Flight plan elements** (communicated in this order):
1. Souls on board
2. Fuel on board (HH:MM or decimal hours)
3. Risk score
4. Estimated time enroute (ETE)
5. Destination airport identifier

### 21.4 ETE/ETA Updates

Pilots must update the OCC with the most accurate ETE/ETA as soon as information is available. Any time the ETE/ETA changes by more than three (3) minutes, the pilot must update the OCC with a reason.

### 21.5 Launch Delays

Communicate launch delays via email to the OCC. This creates a documented receipt for discrepancy resolution.

### 21.6 Weather Declines

When declining a flight for weather:
- Specify WHERE the prohibitive weather is (departure, enroute, destination)
- Present options (alternate airport, ground transport, delay and re-assess)

### 21.7 Aborted Flights

An aborted flight is one that is terminated after it was previously accepted. The most common causes are weather and maintenance. Always present options to the OCC.

### 21.8 Flight Plan Deviations

If the pilot must deviate from the company flight plan, notify the OCC as soon as possible with:
- The new destination
- Updated ETA
- Reason for deviation

## 23. Specialty Operations

### 23.1 Pediatric / Neonatal Teams

Specialty teams (pediatric, neonatal, ECMO) require specialized equipment for patient care. Pilots must:
- Be familiar with the equipment loadout and weight implications
- Be able to assist with loading/unloading and cabin conversion
- Understand the response time target (typically 30 minutes from dispatch)
- Forward questions or concerns to the ACP

### 23.2 ECMO Operations

- The ECMO pump is secured to the cabin floor with straps
- ECMO patients are typically larger and the equipment adds significant weight
- Loading is a SLOW process performed incrementally
- W&B must be carefully verified
