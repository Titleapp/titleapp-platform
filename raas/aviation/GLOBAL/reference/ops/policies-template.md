# Operational Policies Manual -- Template for 14 CFR Part 135 Operators

> **Classification:** Operational Reference -- White-Label Template
> **Regulatory Basis:** 14 CFR Parts 91, 119, 120, 135; 49 CFR Parts 40, 175, 830; EMTALA; DEA Regulations
> **Applicable Mission Types:** Air Medical, On-Demand Charter (Passenger), Cargo
> **Version:** 1.0
> **Last Updated:** 2026-03-06

This template captures the policy substance of a comprehensive Part 135 operator's policies and procedures manual. All operator-specific identifiers, base names, employee names, and tail numbers have been replaced with placeholders. Operators must customize bracketed fields to match their organization, certificate, fleet, and operational environment.

## PART 1 -- ADMINISTRATIVE POLICIES (100-Series)

---

### Policy 100.01 -- Policy Development and Approval

**Purpose:** To establish the process for creating, reviewing, and approving company policies.

**Policy:**

1. All policies shall be developed with input from the relevant department heads and approved by the [Operator] Executive Leadership Team.
2. Each policy shall include:
   - Policy number and title
   - Origination date
   - Last revision date and next review date
   - Purpose statement
   - Policy body
   - Related policies, attachments, and addenda
3. Policies shall be reviewed on a cycle not to exceed [12/24] months.
4. The Quality Management department shall maintain a master policy index and track review dates.
5. Employees are responsible for reading and understanding policies relevant to their role. Acknowledgment of policy receipt and review shall be documented.

---

### Policy 100.02 -- Chain of Command and Authority

**Purpose:** To define the organizational chain of command and decision-making authority.

**Policy:**

The [Operator] chain of command for operational decisions is:

| Level | Position | Authority |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Chief Executive Officer | Organization-wide policy |
| 2 | Chief Operating Officer | Operational strategy and resource allocation |
| 3 | Director of Operations (DO) | All flight operations decisions |
| 4 | Chief Pilot (CP) | Pilot standards, training, and proficiency |
| 5 | Administrator On Call (AOC) | Real-time operational authority (management level) |
| 6 | Base Lead / Clinical Manager | Base-level operational and clinical decisions |
| 7 | Pilot in Command (PIC) | Final authority for safe conduct of the flight |

The AOC is available 24/7 for real-time decision support. The AOC rotation schedule is published monthly. When the AOC is unreachable, the next person up the chain shall be contacted.

The PIC retains final authority for the safe conduct of any flight per 14 CFR 91.3, regardless of any other person's authority or direction.

---

### Policy 100.03 -- Enterprise Risk Management

**Purpose:** To establish a framework for identifying, assessing, and mitigating organizational risk.

**Policy:**

[Operator] maintains an Enterprise Risk Management (ERM) framework aligned with the Safety Management System (SMS). Risk categories include:

- **Operational Risk** -- flight safety, maintenance, ground operations
- **Clinical Risk** -- patient care quality and outcomes (air medical)
- **Financial Risk** -- revenue, cost management, insurance
- **Regulatory Risk** -- FAA, DOT, state EMS agency, DEA, OSHA compliance
- **Reputational Risk** -- public perception, media, stakeholder confidence

Risk is assessed using a probability-severity matrix:

| | Negligible | Minor | Major | Hazardous | Catastrophic |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Frequent** | Medium | High | High | Extreme | Extreme |
| **Occasional** | Low | Medium | High | High | Extreme |
| **Remote** | Low | Low | Medium | High | High |
| **Improbable** | Low | Low | Low | Medium | High |
| **Extremely Improbable** | Low | Low | Low | Low | Medium |

Risks rated High or Extreme require immediate mitigation and executive review.

### Policy 100.04 -- Confidentiality and HIPAA Compliance

**Purpose:** To protect patient, employee, and organizational confidential information.

**Policy:**

#### Mission Type: Air Medical
1. All patient health information (PHI) is protected under HIPAA (Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act).
2. PHI may only be accessed by personnel with a legitimate need to know for treatment, payment, or healthcare operations.
3. PHI shall not be discussed in public areas, on social media, or with unauthorized persons.
4. Electronic PHI must be stored on encrypted devices and transmitted through secure channels.
5. Patient care records shall be completed in the approved electronic charting system.
6. Breaches of HIPAA shall be reported immediately per the Breach Notification procedure.

#### Mission Type: Charter (Passenger) and Cargo
1. Passenger personal information and charter itineraries are confidential.
2. Cargo manifests and shipper information are confidential.
3. Flight data and operational information shall not be shared with unauthorized persons.

## PART 4 -- INFECTION CONTROL AND OCCUPATIONAL HEALTH (104-Series)

---

### Policy 104.01 -- Communicable Disease Exposure and Prevention

**Purpose:** To protect personnel from communicable disease exposure during operations.

**Policy:**

#### Mission Type: Air Medical
1. Universal precautions (standard precautions) shall be used during all patient contacts.
2. Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) shall be available on every aircraft:
   - Gloves (multiple sizes)
   - N95 respirators
   - Eye protection (goggles or face shields)
   - Gowns
   - Hand sanitizer
3. Aircraft shall be cleaned and disinfected after every patient transport per the approved cleaning protocol.
4. Known or suspected communicable disease transports (tuberculosis, meningitis, COVID-19, MRSA, etc.) require enhanced PPE per the specific pathogen protocol.
5. Exposure incidents (needlestick, splash, unprotected contact) shall be reported immediately and documented using the Employee Injury/Exposure Report.
6. Post-exposure testing and follow-up shall be provided per OSHA Bloodborne Pathogens Standard (29 CFR 1910.1030).

#### Mission Type: Charter (Passenger) and Cargo
1. Basic PPE (gloves, hand sanitizer) shall be available to crew.
2. Aircraft interior surfaces shall be cleaned on a regular schedule and after any known illness exposure.
3. Crew who are symptomatic for contagious illness shall not report for duty.

---

### Policy 104.02 -- Immunization and Health Requirements

**Purpose:** To maintain workforce health and prevent disease transmission.

**Policy:**

#### Mission Type: Air Medical
Medical crew shall maintain current immunizations per [Operator] policy and applicable state EMS regulations. Required immunizations include:
- Hepatitis B series
- Influenza (annual)
- Tdap (tetanus, diphtheria, pertussis)
- MMR (measles, mumps, rubella)
- Varicella
- COVID-19 (per current public health guidance)
- TB screening (annual)

Declinations shall be documented. Non-vaccinated personnel may be subject to additional PPE requirements or work restrictions during outbreaks.

---

### Policy 104.03 -- Employee Injury and Exposure Reporting

**Purpose:** To ensure prompt reporting and treatment of workplace injuries and exposures.

**Policy:**

1. All workplace injuries, illness, and exposure incidents shall be reported to the supervisor immediately.
2. An Employee Injury/Exposure Report shall be completed within [24] hours.
3. The reporting flow is:
   - Step 1: Immediate notification to supervisor/AOC.
   - Step 2: Employee Injury Report (EIR) completed.
   - Step 3: Safety Report (SR) filed in the safety reporting system.
   - Step 4: Quality Management (QM) report generated if the event involves patient care.
4. First aid shall be rendered immediately. If the injury requires medical attention beyond first aid, the employee shall be directed to the designated occupational health provider.
5. Workers' compensation claims shall be filed per state requirements.
6. All injury and exposure events are reviewed by the Safety Committee as part of the SMS.

## PART 7 -- TRANSPORT OPERATIONS (200-Series)

---

### Policy 200.01 -- EMTALA Compliance

**Purpose:** To ensure compliance with the Emergency Medical Treatment and Active Labor Act.

**Policy:**

#### Mission Type: Air Medical

[Operator] transports patients in compliance with EMTALA and its implementing regulations (42 CFR 489.24).

**Transport Types:**

| Type | Description | EMTALA Applicability |
|---|---|---|
| Scene Call | Patient picked up from a non-hospital location (accident, residence, wilderness) | Receiving hospital must provide stabilizing treatment |
| Interfacility Transfer | Patient moved from one hospital to another | Sending hospital must certify benefits of transfer outweigh risks; written certification required |
| Shuttle/Repositioning | Aircraft repositioning without patient | Not applicable |
| Non-Emergent Transport | Scheduled, non-emergency patient transport | Standard consent and CMN apply |

**Key EMTALA Requirements:**
1. The sending facility must provide a medical screening exam and stabilizing treatment within its capability.
2. An appropriate transfer requires:
   - Physician certification that the medical benefits of transfer outweigh the risks
   - The receiving facility agrees to accept the patient and has the capacity to treat
   - The transferring hospital sends all available medical records
   - The transfer is conducted by qualified personnel with appropriate equipment
3. [Operator] crews shall ensure the sending physician's certification is documented (Certificate of Medical Necessity or equivalent form).

---

### Policy 200.02 -- Transport Limitations

**Purpose:** To define operational limits for patient and cargo transport.

**Policy:**

#### Mission Type: Air Medical

1. **Patient Weight Limit:** Maximum patient weight for transport is [XXX] lbs, determined by the aircraft patient loading system capacity and cabin configuration. Patients exceeding this limit require special planning and AOC approval.

2. **HAZMAT Scene Response:** Aircraft shall not land within the HAZMAT exclusion zone. If dispatched to a HAZMAT scene:
   - Maintain a safe standoff distance (minimum [1,000] ft upwind)
   - Coordinate with ground incident command for a safe LZ location
   - Medical crew shall not enter the hot zone
   - Patients must be decontaminated before being loaded onto the aircraft

3. **Infectious Disease Transport:** Patients with known or suspected highly communicable diseases require enhanced PPE, specific aircraft ventilation settings, and post-transport decontamination per Policy [104.01].

4. **Long-Range Transport:** Transports with expected flight time exceeding [3] hours require AOC/DO approval. Considerations include:
   - Crew duty time remaining
   - Fuel stops and logistics
   - Patient stability for extended transport
   - Back-haul (return flight) logistics -- back-haul with a patient is discouraged unless operationally justified
   - International transports require additional documentation (customs, patient passports, crew currency for international operations)

5. **Duty Limit for Transport:** No transport shall be accepted if it will cause the crew to exceed a [16]-hour total duty day, unless specifically approved by the AOC/DO.

#### Mission Type: Charter (Passenger)
1. Passenger weight must be within the aircraft payload capacity.
2. Passenger count must not exceed the aircraft's approved seating configuration.
3. All passengers must be able to comply with the safety briefing and follow crew instructions.

#### Mission Type: Cargo
1. Total cargo weight must not exceed the aircraft payload capacity.
2. Individual item weight must not exceed the aircraft loading system capacity or floor load limits.
3. Oversized cargo (exceeding standard cargo door dimensions) cannot be accepted unless the aircraft has a cargo door designed for outsized loads.
4. Live animals: only if specifically authorized and properly contained.
5. Perishable cargo: must be properly packaged and shall not contaminate the aircraft.

### Policy 200.03 -- Post-Transport Debrief (PTD)

**Purpose:** To promote a culture of continuous improvement through structured post-transport debriefs.

**Policy:**

#### Mission Type: Air Medical

A Post-Transport Debrief shall be completed after every transport. The PTD is a structured, blame-free discussion among the crew to identify what went well and what could be improved.

**PTD Checklist Items:**

1. Was the pre-flight risk assessment accurate?
2. Were there any unexpected hazards during the flight?
3. Was communication effective among all crew and external parties?
4. Were there any equipment issues?
5. Was patient care delivered per protocol?
6. Were there any deviations from SOP? If so, were they justified?
7. Was the landing zone / airport adequate?
8. Were there any interpersonal or CRM concerns?
9. Are there any items to report to QM or the Safety system?

**PTD Comment Examples (for guidance):**
- "Approach to the LZ was challenging due to [wires/terrain/obstacles]. Recommend documenting this LZ in the database with hazard notes."
- "Handoff from the sending facility was delayed. Consider early notification for future transfers from this facility."
- "NVG performance was degraded due to cultural lighting. Standard was maintained using instrument crosscheck."

If any crew member identifies a safety concern, a Safety Report (SR) shall be filed in addition to the PTD.

#### Mission Type: Charter (Passenger) and Cargo
A post-flight debrief is encouraged for all operations, particularly when unusual conditions were encountered (weather, mechanical, ATC, difficult loading, etc.).

### Policy 200.05 -- Long-Range Transport

**Purpose:** To establish procedures for transports exceeding [3] hours of flight time.

**Policy:**

#### Mission Type: Air Medical

1. **Threshold:** Any transport with expected total flight time exceeding [3] hours (one-way) is classified as long-range and requires additional planning.

2. **Approval:** AOC/DO approval is required before accepting a long-range transport.

3. **Planning Considerations:**
   - Crew duty time remaining (must not exceed maximum duty day upon return to base)
   - Fuel stops: identify suitable en route airports with fuel availability
   - Weather along the entire route and at fuel stop airports
   - Patient stability for extended flight time
   - Medical supplies and oxygen duration for the extended transport
   - Crew nutrition and hydration provisions

4. **International Flights:**
   - Require additional lead time for customs, immigration, and flight plan filing
   - Patient documentation (passport or equivalent) must be arranged
   - Crew must have current passports
   - Customs and Border Protection (CBP) notification is required per eAPIS

5. **Back-Haul:** Returning with a patient after completing a long-range transport (back-haul) is generally discouraged because it further extends the crew's duty day. Back-hauls require separate AOC approval.

#### Mission Type: Charter (Passenger)
Long-range charter flights follow standard Part 135 planning procedures with additional consideration for passenger comfort, fuel stops, and customs (if international).

#### Mission Type: Cargo
Long-range cargo flights require fuel stop planning that accounts for actual cargo weight. Cargo requiring time-sensitive delivery shall have contingency plans for delays.

### Policy 201.01 -- Patient Care Standards

**Purpose:** To establish minimum standards for patient care during transport.

**Policy:**

2. Medical crew shall maintain current certifications as required by their role and state licensure.

### Policy 201.02 -- Cardiac Monitoring During Patient Transfer

**Purpose:** To ensure continuous cardiac monitoring during all patient transfers.

**Policy:**

#### Mission Type: Air Medical

1. All patients shall be placed on the aircraft cardiac monitor upon loading.
2. Transfer from the sending facility's monitor to the aircraft monitor shall be performed at bedside before the patient is moved.
3. During transfer between monitors, the total time without monitoring shall not exceed [30] seconds.
4. The medical crew shall verify the cardiac rhythm on the aircraft monitor matches the rhythm documented at the sending facility.
5. 12-lead ECG capability shall be available on all aircraft.

### Policy 201.03 -- Aircraft Cabin Altitude and Oxygen Requirements

**Purpose:** To provide guidance on managing cabin altitude for patient care and oxygen administration.

**Policy:**

#### Mission Type: Air Medical

**Sea-Level Cabin Altitude Required For:**
- Pneumothorax (untreated or recently treated)
- Decompression sickness
- Gas gangrene
- Air embolism
- Recent eye surgery with intraocular gas bubble
- Bowel obstruction with significant distension
- Post-craniotomy (first 48 hours or as directed by neurosurgeon)

**Maximum 4,000 ft Cabin Altitude For:**
- Most stable interfacility transfer patients
- Post-surgical patients (unless sea-level is specifically ordered)
- Patients with moderate respiratory compromise

**Oxygen Delivery Methods:**

| Method | Flow Rate | FiO2 Range |
|---|---|---|
| Nasal Cannula | 1-6 LPM | 24-44% |
| Simple Face Mask | 6-10 LPM | 35-60% |
| Non-Rebreather Mask | 10-15 LPM | 60-90% |
| BVM (Bag-Valve-Mask) | 15 LPM | 90-100% |
| Ventilator | Variable | 21-100% |

**In-Flight Oxygen Conversion:**
Oxygen consumption increases with cabin altitude. Conversion factors:

| Cabin Altitude (ft) | Conversion Factor |
|---|---|
| Sea level | 1.0 |
| 2,000 | 1.1 |
| 4,000 | 1.2 |
| 6,000 | 1.3 |
| 8,000 | 1.5 |

Multiply the patient's ground-level oxygen flow rate by the conversion factor to determine the required in-flight flow rate.

**Oxygen Duration Calculations:**

For gaseous oxygen cylinders:

| Cylinder Size | Conversion Factor (PSI to liters) |
|---|---|
| D | 0.16 |
| E | 0.28 |
| H | 3.14 |
| M | 1.56 |

Formula: Duration (minutes) = (Cylinder Pressure PSI x Conversion Factor) / Flow Rate (LPM)

For liquid oxygen (LOX): Duration (hours) = (LOX weight in lbs x 344 liters/lb) / (Flow Rate LPM x 60)

The medical crew shall calculate oxygen duration before departure and ensure sufficient supply for the planned flight time plus a [30]-minute reserve.

### Policy 201.04 -- Patient Care Documentation and Charting

**Purpose:** To establish standards for patient care documentation.

**Policy:**

#### Mission Type: Air Medical

1. All patient transports shall be documented in the approved ePCR system.
2. The chart shall be completed before the end of the duty shift during which the transport occurred.
3. Required documentation elements:
   - Patient demographics
   - Chief complaint
   - History of present illness (HPI) -- must include a clear reason justifying the transport and justifying if an appropriate closer facility was bypassed
   - Physical examination findings (neuro, skin, HEENT, neck, pulmonary, cardiac, abdomen, pelvis, GU, back, extremities)
   - Vital signs at regular intervals:
     - Scene or unstable patients: every 5 minutes
     - Stable interfacility patients: every 15 minutes
     - Non-emergent ground: minimum 2 sets
     - Extended transport (>4 hours) with stable patient: every 30 minutes
   - Glasgow Coma Scale (initial and every 15 minutes for neurologically compromised patients)
   - All medications administered (time, solution, concentration, dosage, rate, route, IV pump usage)
   - Patient condition before and after medication administration
   - Barriers to care, if any
   - Trauma band number (if applicable)
   - Sending and receiving physician names (for interfacility transfers)
   - Patient encounter/medical record number at the receiving facility
   - Patient outcome at time of transfer to receiving team
   - Impression/diagnosis

4. **Charting In Back (During Flight):** Charting while the aircraft is in motion is permitted only when:
   - All timely patient care is complete
   - The patient and/or family have been informed
   - The PIC and one medical crew member are actively scanning for hazards
   - The aircraft is NOT within 10 miles of takeoff or landing
   - Weather and traffic conditions are favorable
   - Charting in back is NOT permitted during deteriorating weather, heavy traffic, or at any time a crew member calls for all eyes outside.
   - Charting should not take place in the presence of the patient.

### Policy 201.05 -- Peer Review and Quality Improvement

**Purpose:** To provide constructive feedback on patient care and documentation through systematic chart review.

**Policy:**

#### Mission Type: Air Medical

1. **Peer Review Cycle:** Every patient care record shall be reviewed within the following timeframes:

| Reviewer | Deadline (from transport) |
|---|---|
| Initial Peer Review | 24 hours |
| Clinical Manager | 7 days |
| Medical Director | 14 days |
| Director of Clinical Services | 21 days |

2. The peer reviewer uses the ALS/CCT CQI Checklist, which evaluates:
   - Consent form completeness and legibility
   - Certificate of Medical Necessity completeness
   - Demographic information
   - Narcotic administration records (cross-referenced with inventory tracking)
   - EKG documentation (if applicable)
   - ETCO2 waveform capnography (for intubated patients)
   - Appropriateness and completeness of care
   - Documentation quality (logical flow, completeness, accuracy)
   - Any deviations from Patient Care Guidelines

3. A "QA Flag" shall be entered on every reviewed chart, noting either "peer review CQI complete" (if no issues) or a description of the identified concern.

4. Significant deviations from Patient Care Guidelines, accepted standards of care, or narcotic discrepancies shall be immediately reported to the Clinical Manager, Director of Clinical Services, and/or Medical Director. A Quality Management report shall be entered.

5. Addenda to patient care records require management approval, except for minor corrections (e.g., typographical errors, correction of receiving hospital name, obvious vital sign transposition such as "SpO2 9%" corrected to "SpO2 99%").

### Policy 201.06 -- Two-Patient Transport

**Purpose:** To establish criteria for transporting two patients simultaneously.

**Policy:**

#### Mission Type: Air Medical

1. Two-patient transports are only permitted in aircraft configured and approved for two-patient operations.
2. The medical crew must have the staffing and skill mix to safely manage both patients.
3. A separate risk assessment shall be performed for two-patient transports.
4. Both patients must be accepted by the same receiving facility (or have compatible destinations).
5. If either patient deteriorates and requires intensive care that would compromise care of the second patient, the PIC shall divert to the nearest appropriate facility.
6. Weight and balance must account for both patients plus all medical equipment.

### Policy 201.08 -- IV Fluids and Medication Storage

**Purpose:** To establish standards for medication and IV fluid storage, administration, and expiration management.

**Policy:**

#### Mission Type: Air Medical

1. **Storage Temperature:** Medications and IV fluids shall be stored at [65-96]F ([18-36]C) per manufacturer requirements. Temperature-sensitive medications (e.g., RSI drugs, vasopressors) shall be stored in temperature-controlled compartments.

2. **Expiration Management:**
   - Medication and IV fluid expiration dates shall be checked at each shift change.
   - Expired items shall be removed from service immediately.
   - Controlled substance expiration follows the separate Controlled Substances policy.

3. **Medication Administration Cross-Check (MACC):**
   - Safe medication administration requires two providers to independently verify the medication before administration.
   - Crew 1 (administering provider) prepares the medication and states: drug name, concentration, dose, route, and indication.
   - Crew 2 (verifying provider) independently inspects the vial/package and confirms: drug name matches, concentration matches, expiration date is valid, dose calculation is correct.
   - Both crew members must visually inspect the medication container -- verbal confirmation alone is insufficient.
   - When only one provider is available (single medical attendant transport), the sole provider shall perform a thorough self-check including reading the label three times (when selecting, when preparing, when administering).
   - If Crew 2 is an EMT, they should visually verify the drug name, concentration, and expiration date.

4. **IVF Administration for Pediatric Patients:** All IV fluids for pediatric patients under [12] years of age shall be administered via an IV infusion pump per [Operator] Patient Care Guidelines.

### Policy 201.13 -- Back-Country and Wilderness Response

**Purpose:** To establish safety procedures for responses to remote or wilderness locations.

**Policy:**

#### Mission Type: Air Medical

1. Back-country and wilderness responses carry elevated risk due to terrain, weather, limited communications, and lack of ground infrastructure.
2. **Crew Consensus Rule:** "Two to go, one to say no." At least two crew members must agree to accept the mission. Any single crew member may decline without repercussion.
3. Additional risk assessment factors for back-country responses:
   - Terrain and elevation at the landing zone
   - Weather at the LZ (which may differ significantly from the base)
   - Communication availability (radio, cell, satellite)
   - Ground contact reliability and LZ preparation
   - Availability of search and rescue backup
   - Aircraft performance at the LZ elevation and density altitude
   - Daylight remaining (for day-only operations)
4. The OCC shall gather maximum information about the LZ before the crew departs.
5. If conditions at the LZ do not match the information provided, the PIC shall abort the approach and reassess.

### Policy 201.17 -- Consent Forms and Certificate of Medical Necessity

**Purpose:** To ensure proper legal documentation for patient transport authorization.

**Policy:**

#### Mission Type: Air Medical

**Patient Consent to Transport:**

1. The patient should sign the consent form (Section I) whenever possible. If a patient signature is obtained, no other section needs to be completed.
2. If the patient's signature is illegible or they sign with an "X," the crew member shall witness by signing below the patient's signature.
3. Consent shall be obtained in the patient's primary language. Translation services shall be used if needed.

   - Document the circumstances that made it impractical for the patient to sign.
7. No modifications to the consent form after the transport is completed.

**Certificate of Medical Necessity (CMN):**

1. The CMN or Physician Certification Statement (PCS) must be completed and signed by an authorized representative of the sending facility: MD, RN, Discharge Planner, PA, NP, or CNS.

## PART 9 -- CONTROLLED SUBSTANCES (201.25 Series)

---

### Policy 201.25 -- Controlled Substances Management

**Purpose:** To ensure DEA-compliant management of controlled substances.

**Policy:**

#### Mission Type: Air Medical

[Operator] is a DEA-registered entity authorized to maintain and administer controlled substances in the course of air medical operations.

**Applicable Regulations:** 21 CFR Parts 1301-1321 (DEA Regulations for Controlled Substances)

**Substances Carried (Typical Schedule II-IV):**

| Schedule | Examples |
|---|---|
| II | Fentanyl, morphine, hydromorphone, ketamine (injectable) |
| III | Ketamine (certain formulations), buprenorphine |
| IV | Midazolam, lorazepam, diazepam |

**I. Acquisition and Distribution:**
1. Controlled substances are acquired through DEA-authorized distributors using DEA Form 222 (for Schedule II) or equivalent ordering procedures.
2. Distribution between bases requires documentation per DEA regulations, including:
   - Sending and receiving base identification
   - Date and tracking number
   - Signatures of sending and receiving personnel (two signatures each)
   - Notification to the Clinical Manager

**II. Storage:**
1. Controlled substances shall be stored in a locked, tamper-evident safe (e.g., Lock View safe) at each base.
2. Only authorized personnel shall have access to the controlled substances cabinet.
3. Authorized access:
   - Flight Nurses: primary access, responsible for shift change counts at their assigned base
   - Flight Paramedics/Respiratory Therapists: access at their assigned base only when functioning in the clinical manager or designated clinical supervisor role
   - Temporary access may be granted by the Clinical Manager for a single shift when operational needs require it
4. ALS ground units: controlled substances are managed by the assigned Ground Paramedic under the direction of the Chief Clinical Officer and Medical Director.

**III. Daily Inventory and Audit:**
1. A daily inventory and audit shall be completed at each shift change by the oncoming and off-going authorized personnel.
2. The inventory includes:
   - Visual inspection of all controlled substances to verify count matches the inventory system.
   - Inspection of vials for evidence of tampering (cap spin test, rubber seal integrity, fluid appearance).
   - Scanning or manual count of each medication vial using the inventory tracking system.
   - Electronic signature by both the oncoming and off-going crew members.
3. If a scanner or barcode is inoperable, the oncoming crew member will verbalize the numbers while the off-going crew manually confirms. The Clinical Manager or Director of Clinical Services shall be notified.

**IV. Tracking and Record Keeping:**
1. All controlled substance records are maintained electronically in the inventory tracking system.
2. Records shall be retained for no less than [2] years per DEA regulations.
3. The inventory tracking log shall include for each administration:
   - Date and time
   - Patient name
   - Incident/run number
   - Dose administered
   - Dose wasted (if applicable)
   - Primary and witness electronic signatures
   - Running totals of controlled substances

**V. Administration During Transport:**
1. Controlled substances shall be administered per [Operator] Patient Care Guidelines and the MACC procedure.
2. During transport, syringes containing controlled substances shall be stored in the designated narcotic bag when not actively being administered.
3. All administrations are documented in both the patient care record and the controlled substance tracking log.
4. During peer review, narcotic administration documentation is cross-referenced with the tracking system log to ensure all amounts match.

**VI. Wasting Procedures:**
1. Wasting a partially used vial requires both the authorized individual and a partner to be physically present.
2. Both individuals must directly observe the medication being completely withdrawn from the container and disposed of in the DEA-approved pharmaceutical waste disposal system.
3. No storage container shall be discarded without completely wasting the remaining content.
4. Empty vials shall be disposed of in a sharps container.
5. Both crew members electronically sign the waste documentation.

**VII. Expired Controlled Substance Disposal:**
1. Expired medications are marked with an "X" on the cap and stored separately in a labeled container ("EXPIRED NARCOTICS") within the base safe.
2. The Clinical Manager or Director of Clinical Services, in the presence of a witness (another clinician), shall:
   - Transfer expired medications to the designated destruction system
   - Draw all medication from each vial and dispense into the approved destruction container
   - Sign the Final Destruction Form
   - Submit documentation to the clinical administration inbox
3. Records of destruction shall be maintained for no less than [5] years.

**VIII. Discrepancy or Tampering:**
1. Any discrepancy, damaged packaging, or evidence of tampering requires immediate notification to the Director of Clinical Services, Clinical Manager, or AOC (whichever is available).
2. The individual reporting the discrepancy shall file a QM report.
3. The affected controlled substances shall be immediately secured.
4. A preliminary investigation will be conducted by the DCS, CM, or AOC.
5. Confirmed diversion of controlled substances will be reported to:
   - Local law enforcement (per state regulations)
   - The DEA (per federal regulations)
6. Evidence of tampering requires transfer to a DEA-accredited independent laboratory for analysis. The chain of custody shall be documented using the Crime Lab Drug Identification Requisition Form.

**IX. Faulty Lock or Cabinet:**
1. If a faulty lock or cabinet is identified, notify the AOC/CM/DCS immediately, regardless of time of day.
2. File a QM report.

## PART 10 -- BLOOD PRODUCTS MANAGEMENT (201.26 Series)

---

### Policy 201.26 -- Blood Storage and Exchange

**Purpose:** To ensure blood products are managed per Satellite Blood Bank Standards.

**Policy:**

#### Mission Type: Air Medical

**A. Management and Documentation:**
1. Each base shall retain [10] years of blood documentation, uploaded to a secure server.
2. Blood products shall be checked at each shift change. Expiration and exchange dates shall be communicated during the shift handoff and posted on the base communications board.

**B. Storage:**
1. Blood products must be maintained at 1.0C to 6.0C at all times.
2. Blood is stored in an approved, continuously monitored blood cooler with a digital temperature monitoring system.
3. Temperature alarms are set at [1.5]C and [5.5]C.

**C. Monitoring:**
1. Daily check using the inventory tracking system to verify product integrity, date, and temperature.
2. When the crew is out for a flight exceeding [3] hours, temperature monitoring data shall be reviewed upon return.
3. Blood bank exchanges follow local hospital protocols. Transactions are recorded in the inventory tracking system.
4. Transfusions follow local hospital protocols. Transactions are recorded in the inventory tracking system.

**D. Blood Cooler Maintenance:**
1. Frozen inserts (TICs) shall be exchanged every [24] hours between the freezer and the cooler.
2. TICs must be in the freezer for a minimum of [8] hours for a full recharge.
3. TICs shall be warmed at room temperature for [20-35] minutes (varies by season and base) before being placed in the cooler with blood products.
4. Inspect the transport cooler daily for: vacuum seal integrity, bag integrity, TIC cracks/leaks.
5. Vacuum insulated panels (VIPs) have an expiration date of [30] months from manufacture.
6. Cooler thermometer must be placed between units of blood and reset before transport.

**E. Temperature Out of Range:**
If blood product temperature is below 0.9C, above 6.1C, or the color-change indicator has activated:
1. Remove the blood from service.
2. Notify the AOC/CM immediately.
3. If the supporting blood bank uses tags, deliver the tags to the blood bank.
4. Complete a QM report.

**F. Blood Product Exchange/Replenishment:**
1. Exchange blood products at the supplying hospital [7-10] days prior to expiration.
2. When exchanging blood, contact the Clinical Manager as soon as feasible and provide complete unit log series.
3. Rural/suburban bases may exchange with urban bases to extend time before expiration, with AOC approval.
4. No base should exchange blood within [10] days of expiration unless approved.
5. All exchanges are documented in the tracking system with: base names, unit numbers, expiration dates, and crew member initials.

**G. Hospital-Supplied Blood Products:**
1. Blood products from outside the operator's contracted blood bank must be accompanied by a transfer notification form.
2. Document in the patient care record: number of units, how transported, chain of custody.
3. Before administration: verify two healthcare providers confirm the blood matches the patient (name, date of birth, blood type).
4. All blood products should be infused through an approved fluid warming device.

**H. Transfusion Reactions:**
1. Immediately stop the transfusion.
2. Treat the patient per published guidelines.
3. Sequester the product in a red biohazard bag with the label.
4. Notify the CM and/or AOC.
5. Complete a QM report.

- Risk assessment using the probability-severity matrix (see Policy 100.03)
- Risk mitigation development and implementation
- Tracking of mitigation effectiveness

**3. Safety Assurance (SA):**
- Internal audits (operational, maintenance, training)
- Trend analysis of safety data
- Regulatory compliance monitoring
- Management review of safety performance

**4. Safety Promotion:**
- Safety training for all personnel (initial and recurrent)
- Safety communication (newsletters, bulletins, briefings)
- Recognition of positive safety behaviors
- Non-punitive reporting culture

### Policy 300.02 -- Safety Reporting (Non-Punitive)

**Purpose:** To encourage open reporting of safety hazards, events, and concerns.

**Policy:**

1. [Operator] maintains a non-punitive safety reporting system. All personnel may submit safety reports without fear of disciplinary action, provided:
   - The event was not the result of intentional misconduct or gross negligence
   - The event was not the result of substance abuse
   - The report is submitted in good faith
2. Safety reports may be submitted anonymously.
3. Safety reports are reviewed by the Safety Manager within [24] hours of receipt.
4. Reports resulting in immediate safety concern shall be escalated to the DO/AOC immediately.
5. Trend data from safety reports is analyzed quarterly and presented to the Safety Committee and executive leadership.
6. Feedback is provided to the reporter (if identified) on the outcome and any actions taken.

   - Clinical care concerns identified during peer review

   - Equipment failures affecting patient care
   - Customer/patient complaints
   - Regulatory findings
3. QM reports are tracked from initiation through investigation, root cause analysis, corrective action, and closure.
4. The QM Committee meets [monthly/quarterly] to review trends, pending investigations, and corrective action effectiveness.
5. QM data is integrated with SMS data for a comprehensive safety picture.

### Policy 300.04 -- Accident and Incident Response

**Purpose:** To supplement GOM Chapter 17 with detailed response procedures.

**Policy:**

1. **Immediate Response:**
   - Render aid to injured persons.
   - Secure the scene (prevent further hazard).
   - Notify emergency services (911) if applicable.
   - Notify the OCC, who activates the Emergency Response Plan.

2. **Notification Chain:**
   - OCC notifies the AOC.
   - AOC notifies the DO, CEO, and Safety Manager.
   - DO notifies the NTSB (1-844-373-9922) for applicable events per 49 CFR 830.
   - DO notifies the FAA FSDO.
   - Insurance carrier is notified by the designated executive.
   - Legal counsel is notified by the designated executive.

3. **Scene Preservation:**
   - Do not move or disturb wreckage except to prevent further injury.
   - Photograph/video the scene if safe to do so.
   - Preserve all electronic data (EFB, GPS, radio recordings, flight tracking data).
   - Preserve all documents (flight plans, weather briefings, weight and balance, maintenance records).

4. **Crew Support:**
   - Involved crew members are relieved of duty.
   - Critical Incident Stress Management (CISM) team is activated.
   - Crew members should not discuss the event on social media or with media.
   - Legal counsel should be consulted before providing statements to external parties (other than the NTSB/FAA as required).

5. **Post-Event Investigation:**
   - The Safety Manager leads the internal investigation.
   - Root cause analysis is conducted.
   - Corrective actions are developed and tracked to completion.
   - Findings are shared organization-wide (de-identified) as a safety learning opportunity.

### Policy 300.05 -- Fatigue Risk Management

**Purpose:** To systematically manage fatigue risk across operations.

**Policy:**

1. Fatigue is recognized as a significant risk factor in aviation operations.
2. [Operator] manages fatigue risk through:
   - Duty time limitations per 14 CFR 135.267 and company policy (GOM Chapter 20)
   - Minimum rest requirements
   - IMSAFE self-assessment at the start of each duty period
   - Fatigue reporting (non-punitive)
   - Education on sleep hygiene, circadian rhythm, and fatigue countermeasures
   - Base facility standards (quiet sleep rooms, climate control, blackout capability)
3. Crewmembers who become fatigued during duty may and should remove themselves from flight status by notifying the AOC. No repercussions shall result.
4. Scheduling practices shall:
   - Avoid excessive consecutive duty days
   - Provide adequate days off per month
   - Minimize circadian disruption (consistent shift start times where feasible)
   - Account for commute time (not part of duty time, but a factor in fatigue risk)
5. Fatigue events are tracked and analyzed as part of the SMS.

### Policy 300.06 -- Critical Incident Stress Management (CISM)

**Purpose:** To provide psychological support to personnel following critical incidents.

**Policy:**

1. [Operator] maintains a CISM program staffed by trained peer support members and professional counselors.
2. CISM is activated for events including:
   - Aircraft accidents or serious incidents
   - Patient deaths during transport
   - Pediatric or mass casualty events
   - Events involving crew injury or near-miss
   - Line-of-duty death of a colleague
   - Any event perceived as traumatic by crew members
3. CISM services include:
   - Defusing (immediate post-event, 20-45 minutes)
   - Debriefing (structured group discussion, typically 24-72 hours post-event)
   - Individual follow-up counseling (referral to Employee Assistance Program)
4. CISM participation is voluntary but strongly encouraged.
5. CISM conversations are confidential and are not used for disciplinary or investigative purposes.

## PART 17 -- CARGO-SPECIFIC OPERATIONAL POLICIES

> This part applies to Mission Type: Cargo only.

---

### Policy CARGO.01 -- Cargo Acceptance and Inspection

**Purpose:** To establish procedures for accepting and inspecting cargo before flight.

**Policy:**

1. All cargo must be presented with a completed cargo manifest or shipping document.
2. The PIC or designated cargo handler shall inspect all cargo before loading:
   - Verify weight matches documentation.
   - Check for damage, leaks, or unusual odors.
   - Verify proper packaging and labeling.
   - Confirm no prohibited items (per FAA regulations and company policy).
3. The PIC may refuse any cargo that appears unsafe, improperly packaged, or incorrectly documented.
4. Hazardous materials require the additional acceptance procedures described in GOM Appendix B.

---

### Policy CARGO.02 -- Cargo Weight Distribution and Floor Loading

**Purpose:** To ensure cargo is loaded within the aircraft structural limits.

**Policy:**

1. Cargo must be distributed to maintain the aircraft CG within approved limits.
2. Floor loading shall not exceed the published limit (lbs per square inch) for any portion of the cargo floor.
3. Heavy items shall be placed:
   - As close to the aircraft CG as practical
   - On the cargo floor (not stacked on lighter items)
   - Over structural members where possible
4. CG checks shall be performed for both takeoff and landing conditions.
5. If cargo shifts during flight, the PIC shall adjust speed and configuration as needed and report the shift for investigation upon landing.

---

### Policy CARGO.03 -- Tiedown Procedures

**Purpose:** To ensure all cargo is properly restrained for flight.

**Policy:**

1. All cargo must be restrained to withstand the load factors specified in GOM Appendix B.
2. Tiedown equipment (straps, nets, chains, shackles) must be:
   - Rated for the load (working load limit must be documented)
   - Inspected before each use
   - Free of damage, fraying, or corrosion
   - Properly routed and tensioned
3. Tiedown attachment points must be aircraft-approved (factory or STC).
4. Cargo nets must cover the entire exposed surface of the cargo.
5. Palletized cargo must have perimeter straps in addition to nets.
6. Rolling stock (wheeled equipment) must be chocked and strapped.
7. Loose items must be placed in bins or bags -- no loose items in the cargo compartment.

---

### Policy CARGO.04 -- Dangerous Goods Operations

**Purpose:** To establish procedures for transporting hazardous materials.

**Policy:**

1. [Operator] [is / is not] authorized to transport dangerous goods.
2. If authorized, all dangerous goods operations shall comply with:
   - 49 CFR Parts 171-180
   - ICAO Technical Instructions for the Safe Transport of Dangerous Goods by Air
   - [Operator] Hazardous Materials Program
3. All personnel involved in DG acceptance, handling, and transport must be trained and current per 49 CFR 172.704.
4. The acceptance checklist (GOM Appendix B.3) must be completed for every DG shipment.
5. The PIC must receive the Notification to Captain (NOTOC) before departure.
6. In the event of a DG incident in flight (leak, spill, fumes, fire):
   - Follow the emergency procedures for the specific hazard class.
   - Declare an emergency.
   - Ventilate the cargo area if possible.
   - Land at the nearest suitable airport.
   - Notify hazmat response upon landing.
7. DG incident reports must be filed per 49 CFR 171.15 (immediate telephonic report) and 49 CFR 171.16 (written report within 30 days).
