## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Purpose

This General Maintenance Manual (GMM) establishes the policies, procedures, and standards for the maintenance, preventive maintenance, and alteration of [Operator] aircraft. It ensures compliance with 14 CFR Parts 43, 91, and 135.

### 1.2 Applicability

This manual applies to all [Operator] maintenance personnel, inspectors, and flight crew members who perform maintenance-related duties.

### 1.3 Document Control

Revisions to this manual require approval from the Senior Director of Aviation Maintenance (or equivalent). Revised pages are distributed through the company document management system. Personnel shall verify they hold the current revision.

## 4. Electronic Maintenance Tracking System

### 4.1 General

All maintenance entries shall be recorded in the company electronic maintenance tracking system. This includes:
- Inspections and their results
- Airworthiness Directive compliance
- MEL items and deferrals
- Life-limited component tracking
- Discrepancies and corrective actions
- Journey log entries

### 4.2 Pilot Responsibilities

Pilots are responsible for:
- Daily aircraft status check (verify GREEN status)
- Reviewing the maintenance due list
- Reviewing open discrepancies
- Creating journey log entries for every flight
- Entering discrepancies discovered during operations

### 4.3 Mechanic Responsibilities

Mechanics are responsible for:
- Recording all maintenance actions in the system
- Updating component tracking after installations and removals
- Closing discrepancies with corrective action entries
- Verifying the electronic system matches the paper logbook

## 6. Parts Management

### 6.1 Approved Parts

Only parts conforming to 14 CFR 21 (approved parts) shall be installed on [Operator] aircraft. Parts must have appropriate documentation:
- FAA Form 8130-3 (Airworthiness Approval Tag)
- OEM release documentation
- Equivalent approved documentation

### 6.2 Receiving Inspection

All parts received shall undergo receiving inspection to verify:
- Part number matches the order
- Condition is acceptable (no damage, corrosion, contamination)
- Documentation is complete and correct
- Shelf life (if applicable) has not expired
- Part is properly identified and tagged

### 6.3 Parts Identification Tags

| Tag Color | Form | Purpose |
|-----------|------|---------|
| RED | Rejection Tag | Rejected (scrap) articles. Red tag must clearly state the reason for rejection. Red-tagged parts shall be segregated as soon as practical. Used only by Maintenance Quality. |
| YELLOW | Serviceable Tag | Serviceable articles. Indicates the component has no known discrepancies and must be inspected/tested upon installation or prior to return to service. |
| GREEN | Repairable Tag | Repairable articles or items requiring further inspection. The discrepancy must be clearly stated. |

### 6.4 Identification of Unapproved Parts

Suspected unapproved parts (SUPs) shall be immediately quarantined, tagged with a RED rejection tag, and reported to the Director of Maintenance and the FAA per Advisory Circular 21-29.

## 7. Calibrated Tools

### 7.1 Calibration Program

All precision measuring equipment and torque tools shall be calibrated at intervals specified by the manufacturer or industry standard. Calibration records shall include:
- Tool identification (serial number)
- Calibration date
- Next calibration due date
- Calibration standard used
- Technician performing calibration

### 7.2 Out-of-Calibration Tools

Any tool found out of calibration shall be immediately removed from service. All work performed with the tool since the last known good calibration shall be reviewed for potential impact.

## 8. Technical Records

### 8.1 Record Retention

Maintenance records shall be retained per 14 CFR 91.417:
- Records of maintenance, preventive maintenance, and alteration: Until the work is repeated, superseded, or the aircraft is sold
- Records of the 100-hour, annual, and other inspections: Until the inspection is repeated
- Records of total time in service for the airframe, each engine, each propeller, and each rotor: Indefinitely

### 8.2 Record Entries

All maintenance record entries shall comply with 14 CFR 43.9 and include:
- Description of the work performed (or reference to data acceptable to the FAA)
- Date of completion
- Name of the person performing the work
- Signature, certificate number, and type of certificate held

## 10. Maintenance Procedures

### 10.1 General Work Flow

1. Identify the maintenance requirement (scheduled inspection, AD, discrepancy, SB)
2. Create a work order in the maintenance tracking system
3. Obtain required parts and documentation
4. Perform the maintenance per approved data
5. Document all work in the work order and logbook
6. Perform required inspections (CTC, AIR as applicable)
7. Return the aircraft to service per Section 14

### 10.2 Work Order Numbering

Work orders are numbered using one of the following formats:
- Year-Month-Day-Aircraft Registration (e.g., 2024-0426-[N-number])
- System-generated log card number (e.g., LC-123456-xx)
- System-generated visit package number (e.g., VP-123456-xx)

### 10.3 Work Order Completion

- All SBs, ADs, 337s, and component changes noted on the cover page
- All fields filled out (enter N/A or a line through any non-applicable field)
- NO blank fields on a completed work order or CTC

## 11. Inspection Program

### 11.1 Approved Airworthiness Inspection Program (AAIP)

[Operator] operates under an AAIP approved by the FAA. The current AAIP for each aircraft type is maintained as a standalone document. Inspection intervals, task cards, and compliance tracking are managed through the electronic maintenance tracking system.

### 11.2 Clear to Close (CTC) Inspection

The CTC inspection verifies that all maintenance work has been completed and properly documented before closing a maintenance event. The CTC must confirm:
- All work orders are complete
- All logbook entries are correct
- The electronic tracking system matches the logbook
- All panels, cowlings, and access covers are secure
- No tools or foreign objects remain in the aircraft

### 11.3 Additional Inspection Requirement (AIR)

Certain maintenance tasks require an additional independent inspection by an AIR-authorized individual. AIR items are identified in the AAIP and on individual work cards.

**AIR items typically include:**
- Flight control installations and rigging
- Engine installations and control rigging
- Propeller installation
- Landing gear retraction system
- Fuel system components
- Any task involving dual/redundant systems where an error could compromise both
- Items specifically designated by the operator or FAA

### 11.4 AIR Procedure

1. Mechanic completes the maintenance task
2. Mechanic requests an AIR inspection from an authorized individual
3. AIR inspector independently verifies the work (not the same person who performed it)
4. AIR inspector signs off the inspection in the work order
5. If a buy-back is required (work interruption during an AIR item), it is documented

## 12. AIR Inspection Flow

1. Maintenance task completed
2. Mechanic documents work and requests AIR
3. AIR inspector reviews work order documentation
4. AIR inspector physically inspects the work
5. If satisfactory: AIR inspector signs off
6. If unsatisfactory: discrepancy noted, corrective action required, re-inspect
7. CTC completed after all AIR items are signed off

## 13. Return to Service and Airworthiness Release

### 13.1 Conditions for Return to Service

An aircraft may be returned to service when ALL of the following conditions are met:
- A) All maintenance was performed in accordance with this GMM and approved data
- B) Approved instructions were followed for all tasks
- C) AIR inspections are complete for all required items
- D) No known unairworthy conditions exist
- E) The aircraft is safe for operation
- F) A 14 CFR 43.9 record entry has been made

### 13.2 14 CFR 43.9 -- Maintenance Record Entries

Each person who performs maintenance shall make an entry in the maintenance record containing:
1. A description of the work performed (or reference to FAA-accepted data)
2. The date of completion of the work
3. The name of the person performing the work
4. The signature, certificate number, and kind of certificate held

The signature constitutes approval for return to service ONLY for the work described in that entry.

### 13.3 14 CFR 43.11 -- Inspection Records

For inspections performed under 14 CFR 135, the record entry shall include:
1. The type and extent of the inspection
2. The date of the inspection and total time in service for the aircraft
3. The signature, certificate number, and kind of certificate held by the person approving the aircraft for return to service
4. A statement that the aircraft has been inspected and found airworthy (or a list of discrepancies)

### 13.4 Example Return to Service Entries

**43.9 Example:**
"[Date]. Complied with [AD/SB/task description]. [Work performed description]. Aircraft total time: [XXXX.X] hours. Returned to service. [Name], A&P Certificate #[XXXXXXX]."

**43.11 Example:**
"[Date]. Completed [Operator] AAIP [XXX]-hour routine inspection per [Operator] AAIP Rev [X]. Aircraft total time: [XXXX.X] hours. Aircraft inspected and found airworthy. [Name], IA Certificate #[XXXXXXX]."

**Unairworthy Discrepancy Example:**
"[Date]. During [XXX]-hour inspection, found [description of discrepancy]. Aircraft is NOT airworthy. See discrepancy #[XX] for corrective action required."

**Corrective Action Clearing a Deferred Item:**
"[Date]. [Description of corrective action performed]. This clears MEL [XXXX-XX]. Returned to service. [Name], A&P Certificate #[XXXXXXX]."

## 14. Airworthiness Directives

### 14.1 Compliance

All applicable ADs shall be complied with within the time frame specified. AD compliance is tracked in the electronic maintenance tracking system.

### 14.2 AMOC (Alternative Method of Compliance)

An AMOC may be used when an alternative means of compliance with an AD has been approved by the FAA. The AMOC must be documented and referenced in the compliance record.

**AD Compliance Write-Up Example:**
"[Date]. Complied with AD [XXXX-XX-XX], Amendment [XX-XXXX], by [description of action]. This is [initial compliance / repetitive compliance / terminating action]. Aircraft total time: [XXXX.X] hours. [Name], A&P Certificate #[XXXXXXX]."

### 14.3 Terminating Actions

Terminating actions for ADs require dual verification by an AIR-listed individual. Both the performing mechanic and the AIR inspector must verify compliance.

### 14.4 Deferred Equipment Subject to ADs

Equipment deferred under the MEL that is subject to an AD must still comply with AD limitations and time frames. The deferral does not suspend AD requirements.

## 15. Major Repair and Alteration

### 15.1 Decision Matrix

A Major/Minor decision matrix shall be used to classify all repairs and alterations:

1. Is the repair/alteration on a structure, powerplant, propeller, or appliance?
2. Does it meet the criteria for "major" per 14 CFR 43 Appendix A?
3. If major: requires FAA Form 337 and approval from the Senior Director of Aviation Maintenance
4. If minor: may be performed per approved data without Form 337

### 15.2 Major Alterations

All major alterations require prior approval from the Senior Director of Aviation Maintenance (or equivalent). Documentation includes:
- FAA Form 337 (Major Repair and Alteration)
- Engineering data (DER approved, STC, or equivalent)
- Weight and balance update (if affected)

---

## 16. Minor Alterations

Minor alterations may be performed per approved data (manufacturer's instructions, AC 43.13-1B, or equivalent) without FAA Form 337. Documentation in the maintenance record is still required per 14 CFR 43.9.

## 17. Operational Maintenance Procedures

### 17.1 Post-Maintenance Functional Checks

After maintenance that affects aircraft systems or performance, functional checks may be required. These include ground runs and/or functional check flights.

### 17.2 Functional Check Flights (FCF)

An FCF is required when ground testing alone cannot verify that a system or repair is functioning correctly in flight.

**FCF Procedures:**
1. Pre-flight briefing with maintenance personnel on the specific items to be checked
2. The FCF is documented in the Aircraft Flight Maintenance Log
3. Duration is as required to complete all test items
4. Any additional faults found during the FCF are documented as new discrepancies
5. Upon satisfactory completion, a completion statement is entered in the logbook

> **NOTE**: An FCF is NOT used to sign off in-flight maintenance requirements that can be verified on the ground.

### 17.3 Maintenance Ground Runs

Ground runs are performed within the aircraft operating limits by properly trained pilots. They are used to verify engine parameters, system functionality, and leak checks after maintenance.

**Ground Run Procedures:**
1. Inspection of the aircraft and surrounding area before engine start
2. Briefing on specific items to monitor
3. Documentation of all parameters observed
4. Any additional faults are documented as new discrepancies
5. Completion statement entered in the logbook

## 19. Weight and Balance

### 19.1 Aircraft Weighing Requirements

Per 14 CFR 135.185, aircraft shall be weighed:
- After any major modification that may affect weight or balance
- After configuration changes (seat additions/removals, equipment changes)
- When a W&B error is suspected
- Every 36 calendar months (or as specified by company policy)

### 19.2 Weighing Procedure

Aircraft weighing shall be performed per the OEM weighing instructions using calibrated scales. The results are documented on the OEM weighing forms and entered into the company W&B records.

### 19.3 Weight and Balance Records

The following records are maintained:
- **Chart A**: Equipment checklist (lists all installed equipment and weight)
- **Chart C**: Weight and balance history (running record of all changes)
- OEM weighing forms from each weighing event

### 19.4 Negligible Weight Changes

Weight changes of 1 lb (0.45 kg) or less per item are considered negligible and do not require an immediate W&B update, provided the total accumulated negligible changes do not exceed the company threshold. All negligible changes are captured at the next scheduled W&B revision.

### 19.5 W&B Audits and Corrections

W&B records are subject to periodic audit. Any discrepancies found shall be corrected immediately and the affected records updated. Copies of current W&B data shall be retained at the aircraft and at the maintenance records office.

## 20. Forms and Reports

### 20.1 Aircraft Flight Maintenance Log (AFML)

The AFML is the primary daily operational record for the aircraft. It records:
- Total time and cycles
- Discrepancies found during operations
- Corrective actions
- Flight times and destinations

### 20.2 Completion Instructions

The AFML consists of two sheets (color-coded for original and copy). It tracks:
- Duty cycle information
- Open discrepancies carried forward
- Corrective actions completed

### 20.3 Closing the AFML

The log page is closed at the end of each duty day (or at midnight for night shifts). Totals are carried forward to the next page.

### 20.8 Maintenance Training Form

Documents completion of all initial and recurrent training modules. Signed by the trainee, trainer, Maintenance Training Manager, and Director of Maintenance.

## 21. SDR Reporting

Per 14 CFR 135.415, Service Difficulty Reports shall be filed for:
- Fires during flight and whether the fire extinguishing system functioned
- Engine failures or shutdowns during flight
- System or component failures resulting in the taking of emergency action
- Any other failures, malfunctions, or defects the operator considers could be hazardous
