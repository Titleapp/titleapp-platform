## 1. Smoking Restrictions

### 14 CFR 135.117 -- Prohibition Against Carriage of Weapons and Smoking

- **No smoking is permitted in the aircraft at any time** -- whether on the ground or in flight, with or without patients/passengers aboard.
- **No smoking within 50 feet of the aircraft** -- this applies to all persons (crew, passengers, ground personnel, bystanders) at all times when the aircraft is being operated, serviced, loaded, or unloaded.
- Crew members are responsible for enforcing the 50-foot no-smoking perimeter. If ground personnel or bystanders are smoking within 50 feet of the aircraft, the crew must request they move beyond the perimeter or cease smoking before operations continue.

## 2. Medical Bed Operations -- Oxygen (CAMTS Standards)

### Sled Oxygen Shutoff

- The medical sled (stretcher/litter) oxygen supply shutoff valve is located at the **aft end of the medical bed** (under the bed, accessible from the foot end).
- To shut off medical oxygen: **turn the valve clockwise** to close.
- All crew members -- not just medical crew -- must know the location and operation of the sled oxygen shutoff. In an emergency (fire, rapid decompression, emergency landing), any crew member may need to secure the medical oxygen supply immediately.

### When to Secure Medical Oxygen

- **Emergency landing or evacuation** -- Close the medical oxygen shutoff as part of the emergency checklist. Oxygen flow into a damaged cabin creates an extreme fire hazard.
- **Onboard fire or smoke** -- Secure all oxygen sources immediately. Oxygen will accelerate any fire.
- **Rapid decompression** -- Secure supplemental medical oxygen and transition patient to aircraft emergency oxygen system if appropriate per medical crew judgment.

## 3. Emergency Operations

### Distress Frequency

- **121.5 MHz** -- The international aeronautical distress frequency. Monitored by ATC facilities, military, and search-and-rescue.
- If declaring an emergency, transmit on 121.5 if unable to reach ATC on the assigned frequency.
- All crew members should know how to tune 121.5 on the aircraft communication radios.

### Emergency Oxygen Masks (Crew and Passengers)

- **Emergency oxygen masks are located next to each seat** in the cabin (crew seats and any passenger/attendant positions).
- In the event of cabin depressurization or smoke/fumes:
  1. **Don your own mask first** before assisting patients or other crew members. You cannot help others if you are incapacitated.
  2. Pull the mask from its stowage, place over nose and mouth, and ensure the oxygen flow indicator shows oxygen delivery.
  3. Assist patients and other crew members after your own mask is secured.

### Equipment Stowage

- All loose equipment in the cabin must be stowed in **cabin lockers** or secured on the **brown equipment rails** (or equivalent cabin rail system).
- Unsecured equipment becomes a projectile hazard during turbulence, emergency maneuvers, or impact.
- Pre-flight the cabin to verify all equipment is properly secured before every departure.

## 4. Passenger Oxygen System -- PC-12 Cabin Masks

### System Description

- The PC-12 cabin passenger oxygen system uses **airline-style drop-down masks** installed inside the cabin sidewall panels.
- Masks are deployed by pulling **red lanyards** that are visible and accessible near each mask location.
- Mask locations are marked on the cabin interior. All crew members should familiarize themselves with mask positions during pre-flight.

### Activation

- **Automatic deployment:** The system activates automatically when cabin pressure altitude exceeds **13,000 feet** (cabin altitude, not aircraft altitude). This is a barometric trigger -- if the cabin pressurization fails or the aircraft loses pressurization, the masks will deploy without crew action.
- **Manual / Pilot override:** The pilot can manually activate the cabin oxygen system from the cockpit at any time, regardless of cabin altitude. Use manual activation if:
  - Smoke or fumes are detected in the cabin.
  - Cabin pressure altitude is rising toward but has not yet reached 13,000 feet.
  - Medical crew requests supplemental oxygen for non-patient cabin occupants.
  - Any other situation where the PIC determines cabin occupants need supplemental oxygen.

### Crew Awareness

- Know the difference between the **cabin oxygen system** (masks in the sidewall for passengers/crew, barometric trigger at 13,000 feet) and the **crew oxygen system** (cockpit-mounted quick-donning masks with continuous flow, independent of the cabin system).
- The cabin oxygen system is a **supplemental** system designed for emergency use. It is not a substitute for maintaining proper cabin pressurization.

## 5. Post-Transport Debrief (PTD)

### Purpose and Requirement

The Post-Transport Debrief is a **mandatory** safety and quality management activity completed after every patient transport mission. The PTD is critical for:

- **Safety:** Identifying hazards, near-misses, and safety events while details are fresh.
- **Quality Management (QM):** Capturing operational data, identifying trends, and driving continuous improvement.
- **Regulatory compliance:** Supporting the operator's Safety Management System (SMS) requirements.

### Procedure

1. The **Pilot in Command is responsible** for initiating and completing the PTD in the operator's safety reporting system (e.g., Baldwin, ASAP, or equivalent safety reporting platform) after every patient transport mission.
2. **All crew members provide input** -- The PTD is not a pilot-only activity. Medical crew, co-pilots, and any other crew members aboard the flight must contribute their observations, concerns, and feedback.
3. Complete the PTD as soon as practical after the transport while details are still fresh. Do not defer PTDs to the end of the shift or the next duty day if avoidable.

### Culture

> **"Speak Up, Speak Often, Question."**

- The PTD is a non-punitive reporting opportunity. All observations -- positive and negative -- are valued.
- No concern is too small to report. Near-misses and "that seemed unusual" observations are often the most valuable safety data points.
- If something did not feel right during the transport, report it. If everything went well, report that too -- positive reporting validates that procedures are working.
- The PTD feeds directly into the operator's Safety Management System. Trends identified from PTD data drive training, procedure changes, and safety alerts across the organization.

## 6. ADM Framework Application

### Decision-Making in Air Ambulance Operations

Air ambulance operations involve unique pressures that can degrade aeronautical decision making:

- **Patient condition urgency** -- The pressure to "get the patient to the hospital" can bias crews toward accepting higher operational risks than they would on a non-medical flight.
- **Crew fatigue** -- Extended duty periods, nighttime operations, and physically demanding patient loading/unloading compound fatigue effects.
- **Weather pressure** -- Requests to fly in marginal conditions are more frequent in air ambulance operations because alternative transport (ground ambulance) may significantly delay patient care.
- **Communication dynamics** -- Medical crew may not understand aviation risk factors, and pilots may not understand medical urgency factors. Clear, respectful cross-discipline communication is essential.

### Mitigations

- Use the operator's approved risk assessment tool (flight risk assessment tool / FRAT) before every flight.
- Apply personal minimums that exceed regulatory minimums.
- Use the PTD system to report pressure or decision-making concerns.
- Remember: **A late patient is better than a dead crew.** The safest transport is one that arrives.
