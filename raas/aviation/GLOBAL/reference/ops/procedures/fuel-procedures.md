# Fuel Procedures -- Safe Handling, Storage & Use of Jet Fuel

## 1. Terms and Definitions

**Bonding** -- The process of connecting two or more conductive objects together by means of a conductor so that they are at the same electrical potential. Bonding prevents sparking between separate metallic components during fueling operations. Both the aircraft and the fuel source must be bonded before any fuel transfer begins.

**Dissolved Water** -- Water that is held in solution within the fuel at the molecular level. Dissolved water is not visible to the naked eye and cannot be removed by filtration or sumping. It becomes a concern when temperature drops cause it to precipitate out of solution, at which point it becomes free water.

**Entrained Water** -- Tiny water droplets suspended within the fuel that give the fuel a hazy or cloudy appearance. Entrained water has not yet settled to the bottom of the tank and may not be caught by a single sump check. Fuel must pass a "clear and bright" inspection before use.

**Free Water** -- Water that has separated from the fuel and is visible as a distinct layer, typically settled at the bottom of the tank or storage vessel. Free water is the most immediately detectable form of contamination. It is caught by sumping and must be drained completely before fueling operations.

**FSII (Fuel System Icing Inhibitor) / PRIST** -- A fuel additive (trade name PRIST) that serves as a freezing point depressant. It prevents the formation of ice crystals in fuel lines and filters at altitude and in cold conditions. FSII also discourages the growth of organic contaminants (microbial growth) in fuel tanks. Added per manufacturer specifications during fueling when required by operating conditions or company policy.

**Grounding** -- The process of connecting a conductive object to the earth (ground) to dissipate static charge buildup. Grounding provides a path for electrical charge to flow safely to earth, preventing the accumulation of static electricity that could cause a spark during fuel transfer.

## 2. Company Policies and Regulatory Framework

### Governing Documents

- **Fuel Handling Procedures Manual** -- Governed by applicable FARs and ATA 103 (Standard for Jet Fuel Quality Control at Airports). The manual defines all fuel handling, storage, quality control, and training requirements.
- Manual storage location: Operator's document management system (e.g., Content Locker > Managed Content > Fueling, or equivalent).
- The Safety / Director of Operations (or equivalent safety officer) is responsible for manual content, updates, and revision control.

### Chain of Responsibility

1. **Chief Pilots / Base Managers** -- Ensure all flight crew receive initial and recurrent fuel handling training. Verify compliance with fuel procedures at assigned bases.
2. **Assistant Chief Pilots (ACPs) / Base Supervisors** -- Conduct quarterly fuel facility inspections at their assigned bases. Document inspection findings and route discrepancies to maintenance and safety.
3. **Pilot in Command (PIC)** -- Ultimately responsible for fuel quality, quantity, and safe fueling operations for each flight. The PIC may not delegate this final authority.

### Pilot Responsibilities

- Check fuel quality and grade before every fueling event. Confirm fuel grade matches aircraft requirements.
- Sump fuel tanks at the beginning of every shift. Follow the daily fuel check procedure using the operator's standardized fuel check form (e.g., Form A04 or equivalent).
- Sumped fuel samples must be stored in a flammable materials locker per NFPA and local fire code requirements.

> **WARNING**: Never dispense fuel from an unchecked source. If the fuel source has not been inspected, tested, or its quality cannot be verified, do not use it. Report the condition to your ACP/Base Manager and Safety department.

## 3. Fuel Facilities -- Types and Inspection Requirements

### Type 1: In-Ground Fuel Farm

- Large-capacity underground or above-ground storage installation.
- Typically activated remotely or by electronic card access.
- Requires daily facility checks by the assigned base crew: tank level, leak inspection, sump drain, filter differential pressure, and bonding/grounding continuity.
- Most common at primary base locations with high fuel throughput.

### Type 2: Trailer / Bowser

- Smaller portable fuel storage unit, often trailer-mounted.
- Common at remote or rotorwing-only bases.
- Equipped with electric pumps (no gravity feed).
- Same daily check requirements as in-ground farms: sump, filter differential, bonding verification.
- Must be secured (chocked, leveled) when parked.

### Type 3: Truck -- Operator-Operated / FBO-Maintained

- Medium-capacity fuel truck maintained by the FBO but operated by the operator's crew.
- Fuel quality checks are the responsibility of the operator's crew before each use.
- Verify FBO maintenance logs are current (filter changes, calibration, hose inspection).
- Standard daily checks apply.

### Type 4: Truck -- FBO-Operated

- Fuel truck fully operated and maintained by the FBO.
- FBO is responsible for fuel quality checks. Operator crew should verify that FBO inspections have been completed within the last 4 days.
- Crew must still perform a **Clear and Bright test** on a fuel sample before accepting fuel from an FBO truck.
- If inspection records are not available or are out of date, treat as ad hoc fuel (see Section 4).

## 4. Ad Hoc Fueling (Non-Base Locations)

When fueling at locations outside the operator's established fuel facilities:

1. **Use branded fuel vendors** whenever possible (Shell, Phillips 66, Avfuel, World Fuel, etc.).
2. **Request the fuel vendor's inspection log** -- Verify filter changes, water checks, and quality testing are current.
3. If no inspection log is available, use a **Shell water detection kit** (or equivalent field test kit) to check for free water contamination before accepting fuel.
4. Check **filter differential pressure** -- Maximum allowable filter differential is **15 PSI**. If the differential exceeds 15 PSI, do not accept fuel from that source.
5. Inspect fuel hoses -- Hoses have a **10-year expiration** from date of manufacture. Check the date stamp on the hose. Do not use expired hoses.
6. If fuel contamination is discovered or suspected, **do not use the fuel**. File a safety report (e.g., Baldwin Safety Report or equivalent) immediately. Notify the Safety department and your ACP/Base Manager.

## 5. Aircraft Fueling Operations

### Pre-Fueling Requirements

- Fueling training must be completed before any crew member participates in fueling operations (initial + recurrent per company training schedule).
- The **PIC must be present** during all fueling operations or must have specifically delegated oversight to a qualified crew member.

### Fueling Procedure

1. **Ensure bonding** -- Connect the bonding cable between the aircraft and the fuel source before opening any fuel caps or connecting nozzles.
2. **No hot fueling** -- Do not fuel the aircraft with engines running unless specifically authorized by an approved hot-fueling procedure (rare in Part 135 operations).
3. **Add FSII/PRIST as required** -- Per aircraft manufacturer specifications and operator policy. Document FSII addition on the fuel log.
4. **During fueling, the following are prohibited**:
   - Connection or disconnection of external electrical power
   - Oxygen servicing
   - Smoking or open flames within 50 feet of the aircraft
   - Passenger boarding/deplaning (unless approved by operator SOP)
5. Monitor the fueling process. Watch for leaks, overflow, or unusual odors.
6. After fueling, disconnect the nozzle, replace fuel caps, and disconnect the bonding cable (in that order).

### Fuel Use Log (Form A05 or Equivalent)

Record every fueling event on the operator's fuel use log. Each entry should include:

- Date, time, and location
- Aircraft tail number
- Fuel quantity (gallons or pounds)
- Fuel source identification
- Flight type code:
  - **Type 1** -- Medical / Revenue Flight
  - **Type 2** -- Training Flight
  - **Type 3** -- Maintenance (repositioning for MX, test flights, etc.)
  - **Type 4** -- Public Relations / Patient Experience Transport (PET/PR)
  - **Type 5** -- Reposition (non-revenue ferry flight)

Submit completed fuel logs to the ACP/Base Manager at the end of each month. Copy fuel receipts to the designated accounting address per operator policy.

## 6. Fuel Quality and Testing

### Clear and Bright Test

Every fuel sample taken (whether from sumping or before accepting fuel from an external source) must pass the Clear and Bright standard:

- **Clear** -- No visible particulate matter, sediment, or debris.
- **Bright** -- No haze, cloudiness, or discoloration. The fuel should have a transparent, slightly yellow or straw-colored appearance with a visible sheen.

If a sample fails Clear and Bright, do not use the fuel. Sump again and retest. If repeated failures occur, take the fuel source out of service and notify maintenance and safety.

### Water Detection

- Use approved water detection paste or capsules (e.g., Shell water detection kit) when field-testing fuel at non-base locations.
- Any positive indication of free water requires draining the sump until the sample runs clean, then retesting.

### Contamination Reporting

Any suspected or confirmed fuel contamination event (water, particulate, wrong fuel grade, microbial growth, odor, or discoloration) must be reported immediately through the operator's safety reporting system. The affected fuel source must be taken out of service pending investigation.

## 7. Review and Compliance

- Fuel handling procedures are reviewed annually as part of the operator's safety management system (SMS) review cycle.
- ACPs/Base Supervisors conduct **quarterly fuel facility inspections** and document findings.
- All crew members complete **initial fuel handling training** before participating in fueling operations and **recurrent training** per the operator's approved training program.
- The Safety / Director of Operations maintains the Fuel Handling Procedures Manual and issues revisions as regulations, equipment, or procedures change.
