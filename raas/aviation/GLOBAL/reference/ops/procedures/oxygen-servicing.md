# Oxygen Servicing -- Medical Oxygen and Aviator's Breathing Oxygen (ABO)

> Part 135 Reference Template -- White-labeled for generic operator use.
> Source knowledge derived from operational training materials for PC-12 aircraft.
> All safety protocols and pressure specifications retained verbatim.

---

## 1. Safety Precautions

Oxygen servicing is a high-hazard operation. The following safety rules apply without exception:

- **Oxygen supports combustion aggressively.** Oxygen-enriched environments cause materials that would not normally burn to ignite and burn violently. Treat all oxygen equipment and areas as fire hazards.
- **No grease, oils, or petroleum-based lubricants** may be used on or near oxygen fittings, regulators, valves, or lines. Even trace amounts of hydrocarbon contamination on oxygen equipment can cause spontaneous ignition under pressure.
- **No aircraft fueling during oxygen servicing.** Fuel vapors and oxygen are an extreme ignition hazard. Complete all oxygen servicing before or after fueling, never simultaneously.
- **No open flames, sparks, or smoking** within 50 feet of oxygen servicing operations.
- **Compressed gas handling:**
  - Open all valves slowly and deliberately. Rapid valve opening can cause pressure spikes, adiabatic heating, and equipment failure.
  - Bleed all lines before disconnecting fittings. Residual pressure in lines can cause fittings to separate violently.
  - Always install safety caps on cylinders when not in use or during transport.
  - **Never leave a cylinder unsecured.** A falling or rolling cylinder is a projectile hazard. Secure cylinders in approved racks, carts, or restraints at all times.

## 2. Types of Gaseous Oxygen

### Medical Oxygen (USP Grade)

- Labeled: **"OXYGEN"** (USP -- United States Pharmacopeia)
- Purity: 99.0% minimum per USP standards.
- Intended for patient care and medical use.
- May contain trace moisture within USP limits.
- Used for onboard patient oxygen delivery systems (medical bed oxygen, portable patient regulators).

### Aviator's Breathing Oxygen (ABO)

- Labeled: **"OXYGEN, Aviator's Breathing"**
- Purity: **99.5% minimum**, with strict moisture limits (dry oxygen).
- The dryness specification prevents ice crystal formation in oxygen regulators and masks at altitudes where ambient temperatures reach **-30 C** or below.
- Used for crew supplemental oxygen systems (cockpit and cabin emergency masks).
- **ABO and Medical Oxygen are NOT interchangeable.** ABO's dryness specification is critical for high-altitude flight safety. Medical oxygen's higher moisture content can freeze in crew oxygen regulators at altitude.

## 3. Oxygen Storage Locations (PC-12)

### Aviator's Breathing Oxygen (ABO)

- Located in the **right forward service bay** (forward of the wing, right side of the fuselage).
- The ABO filler port has a **capped fitting** -- remove the cap to access the fill connection.
- The filler connection size is specific to ABO and will not mate with medical oxygen fill equipment. This size difference is an intentional safety measure to prevent cross-filling.

### Medical Oxygen

- Located **under the medical bed** (aft cabin area).
- The medical oxygen fill line has a **threaded cap** -- unscrew the cap to access the fill connection.
- The filler connection size is specific to medical oxygen and will not mate with ABO fill equipment.

> **Note:** The different filler connection sizes between ABO and Medical Oxygen are a deliberate design safety feature. If a fill line does not connect easily, you are attempting to connect to the wrong system. Do not force connections.

## 4. Servicing Equipment

### Oxygen Cart Components

The standard oxygen servicing cart contains:

| Item | Quantity | Purpose |
|------|----------|---------|
| Oxygen cylinders | 2 | Supply bottles (one ABO, one Medical, or two of the same type depending on cart configuration) |
| Wrenches | 2 | For cylinder valve and fitting connections |
| Teflon tape | 1 roll | For sealing threaded connections (oxygen-safe tape only) |
| Marker | 1 | For labeling cylinders and recording pressures |
| Regulator assembly | 1 | Controls delivery pressure from supply cylinders to aircraft |
| Fill line / hose | 1 | Connects regulator output to aircraft fill port |

### Regulator Assembly Components

The regulator is the primary control interface during oxygen servicing. Know every component:

1. **Line Pressure Gauge** -- Displays the pressure in the fill line between the regulator and the aircraft. This is the pressure being delivered to the aircraft system.
2. **Bottle Pressure Gauge** -- Displays the pressure remaining in the supply cylinder currently in use.
3. **Pressure Relief Valve** -- Automatic safety valve that vents if line pressure exceeds safe limits. Do not tamper with or adjust.
4. **Pressure Control T-Handle** -- The primary flow control. Turn to regulate the pressure and flow rate from the supply cylinder into the fill line. **Must be fully closed after servicing.**
5. **Filling Knob** -- Opens and closes the flow path from the regulator to the fill line. Used in conjunction with the T-Handle to control filling.
6. **Manifold** -- The central body connecting both supply cylinders, the regulator, gauges, and output.
7. **Panel Mount Nut** -- Secures the regulator assembly to the cart frame.

## 5. Servicing Principles

### Pressure Differential Filling

Oxygen servicing works by **pressure differential** -- the supply cylinder (cart) must be at a higher pressure than the aircraft system in order for oxygen to flow from the cart into the aircraft. Flow stops when pressures equalize.

### Critical Rules

- **NEVER open both supply bottles simultaneously.** If both bottles are open at the same time, oxygen will flow from the higher-pressure bottle into the lower-pressure bottle until they equalize. This wastes oxygen, creates unpredictable pressures, and makes it impossible to track individual bottle levels.
- **Use the lower-pressure bottle first.** This maximizes the total usable oxygen from both bottles. Example: If the aircraft medical bed reads 1400 PSI, Cart Bottle 1 reads 1700 PSI, and Cart Bottle 2 reads 2000 PSI -- use Bottle 1 (1700 PSI) first. It will fill the aircraft to approximately 1700 PSI. Then close Bottle 1, open Bottle 2 (2000 PSI) to top off to the target pressure.

## 6. Servicing Procedure (9 Steps)

1. **Position the cart** adjacent to the aircraft fill port. Ensure the cart is on level ground, wheels chocked or brakes set, and cylinders secured.

2. **Verify cylinder contents.** Read the label on each cylinder (not just the cart label -- see Section 9). Confirm the cylinder contains the correct type of oxygen (ABO or Medical) for the system you are servicing.

3. **Connect the fill line** to the aircraft fill port. Hand-tighten the connection. Do not over-torque. Verify the connection is secure with no cross-threading.

4. **Open the aircraft fill port valve** (if equipped). Some installations have a shutoff valve at the fill port; ensure it is fully open.

5. **Select the lower-pressure supply bottle.** Open the cylinder valve slowly (one full turn, then back a quarter turn per standard compressed gas practice).

6. **Slowly open the Pressure Control T-Handle** to begin flow. Monitor the Line Pressure Gauge. Control the fill rate -- rapid filling generates heat and can give a false high-pressure reading.

7. **Fill to the target pressure.** The standard fill target is approximately **1900 PSI**. The gas will cool after filling and the pressure will settle to approximately **1800 PSI** at ambient temperature. Maximum bottle pressure is **2250 PSI** -- never exceed this value.

8. **Close the T-Handle fully.** Close the supply cylinder valve. Bleed the fill line by briefly opening the filling knob to release residual pressure. Disconnect the fill line from the aircraft.

9. **Replace the aircraft fill port cap.** Verify the cap is secure. Record the servicing on the aircraft logbook or daily inspection form (date, type of oxygen, final pressure, crew member initials).

> **CRITICAL: Ensure the T-Handle is fully closed after every servicing event.** A T-Handle left open with a pressurized supply bottle creates a risk of high-pressure line failure and uncontrolled oxygen release.

## 7. Pressure Minimums

### Medical Oxygen (Patient Flights)

| Condition | Minimum PSI | Normal Operating Range |
|-----------|-------------|----------------------|
| Patient flight dispatch | **1500 PSI** | 1800 -- 2000 PSI |

If medical oxygen is below 1500 PSI and a patient flight is assigned, the system must be serviced before departure. Do not dispatch below minimums.

### Crew / Aviator's Breathing Oxygen

| Condition | Minimum PSI | Normal Operating Range |
|-----------|-------------|----------------------|
| Flight dispatch | **800 PSI** | 1500 -- 1800 PSI |

If ABO is below 800 PSI, the system must be serviced before departure regardless of planned flight altitude.

## 8. Ship (Crew) Oxygen -- Specific Notes

The crew oxygen (ship oxygen) servicing procedure is the same as described in Section 6, with the following differences:

- **No shutoff valve on the fill side** for some installations. The fill port connects directly to the aircraft oxygen system with no intermediate valve.
- After servicing, **verify that the cockpit oxygen pressure gauge and the filler port gauge agree** (within normal gauge tolerance). A significant discrepancy between the two gauges indicates a leak, a gauge malfunction, or an incomplete fill. Investigate before flight.

## 9. Bottle Exchange Procedure (13 Steps)

When a supply cylinder is depleted and must be replaced:

1. Close the cylinder valve on the empty bottle.
2. Close the T-Handle on the regulator.
3. Bleed residual pressure from the line connected to the empty bottle.
4. Disconnect the regulator fitting from the empty bottle.
5. Install the safety cap on the empty bottle valve.
6. Mark the empty bottle clearly (e.g., "EMPTY" in marker on the label or tape).
7. Remove the empty bottle from the cart and secure it in the empty bottle storage area.
8. Retrieve a full replacement bottle. Verify the label matches the required oxygen type (ABO or Medical).
9. Remove the safety cap from the full bottle.
10. Inspect the bottle valve and fitting for damage, contamination, or corrosion. Do not use a damaged bottle.
11. Connect the regulator fitting to the full bottle. Hand-tighten, then snug with a wrench (do not over-torque).
12. Slowly crack the bottle valve to pressurize the connection. Check for leaks (listen and feel -- do not use soapy water or other liquids on oxygen fittings).
13. Secure the full bottle in the cart. Update the cart pressure log with the new bottle pressure.

## 10. Cart Identification and Cross-Contamination Prevention

Oxygen carts must be clearly labeled with the type of oxygen they carry:

- **"MEDICAL OXYGEN"** -- Cart dedicated to Medical (USP) oxygen cylinders.
- **"AVIATOR'S BREATHING OXYGEN"** -- Cart dedicated to ABO cylinders.

> **CRITICAL: Check the labels on the individual cylinders (tanks), not just the cart label.** Cylinders can be placed on the wrong cart by mistake. The cylinder label is the authoritative identification of the oxygen type. If a cylinder label does not match the cart label, remove the cylinder and investigate before use.

**Details matter. Never mix the two oxygen types on the same cart.** Cross-contamination between Medical and ABO systems can compromise crew oxygen system performance at altitude (moisture freezing in ABO regulators) or violate USP medical oxygen standards for patient care.
