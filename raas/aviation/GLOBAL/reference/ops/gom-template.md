This General Operations Manual (GOM) establishes the policies, procedures, and standards governing flight operations conducted under 14 CFR Part 135 by [Operator]. It serves as the primary reference for all operational personnel and is required to be accessible to every crewmember during duty.

### 1.5 Mission Types

#### Mission Type: Air Medical
Transport of patients requiring medical care during flight. Includes scene responses, interfacility transfers, and organ/tissue transport. Subject to all Part 135 requirements plus CAMTS standards (if accredited) and state EMS regulations.

#### Mission Type: Charter (Passenger)
On-demand transport of passengers. Subject to Part 135 requirements including passenger briefing (14 CFR 135.117), carry-on baggage stowage (14 CFR 135.87), and smoking restrictions (14 CFR 135.127).

#### Mission Type: Cargo
Transport of freight and goods. Subject to Part 135 requirements plus 49 CFR 175 (Transportation of Hazardous Materials) when applicable. Cargo-specific procedures for weight distribution, restraint, and dangerous goods acceptance are detailed in Chapter 7 and the Cargo Operations Supplement.

## Chapter 2 -- Administration

### 2.1 Organizational Structure

[Operator] maintains a management structure compliant with 14 CFR 119.69 (for certificate holders using single PIC) and 119.71 (for those requiring management personnel). Required management positions:

- **Director of Operations (DO)** -- Overall authority for flight operations conduct
- **Chief Pilot (CP)** -- Pilot training, checking, proficiency standards
- **Director of Maintenance (DM)** -- Airworthiness, maintenance programs, MEL administration
- **Chief Inspector** -- Quality assurance of maintenance (if applicable)

### 2.2 Operational Control -- Two-Tier System

Operational control is exercised through a two-tier structure:

**Tier 1 -- Management Authority:**
The DO, CP, and AOC hold broad authority over operations policy, fleet management, weather minimums, and go/no-go authority at the organizational level.

**Tier 2 -- Real-Time Operational Authority:**
The OCC and PIC share real-time operational control for each flight. The PIC retains final authority for the safe conduct of the flight per 14 CFR 91.3. The OCC provides dispatch support, flight following, weather monitoring, and risk assessment assistance.

No person may coerce or pressure a PIC into conducting a flight the PIC considers unsafe.

### 2.3 Duty and Availability

#### Mission Type: Air Medical
Crewmembers shall be on duty and available at the assigned base during their scheduled shift. Duty begins at shift check-in and ends at shift relief.

#### Mission Type: Charter (Passenger)
Pilots shall report for duty at least 60 minutes prior to the scheduled departure time for domestic flights and 90 minutes for international flights. Duty time begins at the scheduled report time.

#### Mission Type: Cargo
Pilots shall report for duty per the published cargo schedule or at least 60 minutes prior to first scheduled departure. Pre-flight cargo inspection time shall be included in the duty period.

### 2.4 Records and Reporting

[Operator] maintains the following records per 14 CFR 135.63:
- Flight time, duty time, and rest records for each crewmember
- Aircraft mechanical irregularity and maintenance release records
- Dispatch/release records (or equivalent for on-demand operations)
- Load manifests per 14 CFR 135.63(c)

## Chapter 3 -- Duties and Responsibilities

### 3.1 Director of Operations

The DO has final authority over the initiation, continuation, diversion, and termination of flights. Responsibilities include:
- Ensuring compliance with OpSpecs, the GOM, and all applicable FARs
- Authorizing flights in conditions beyond standard minimums
- Overseeing pilot and crewmember training and qualification
- Maintaining operational control systems
- Interfacing with the FAA on operational matters

### 3.2 Chief Pilot

The CP is responsible for:
- Pilot hiring, training, and proficiency checking
- Establishing and maintaining pilot qualification records
- Conducting or supervising competency checks and line checks
- Recommending operational policy changes to the DO
- Acting as DO in the DO's absence (if so designated)

### 3.3 Director of Maintenance

The DM is responsible for:
- Ensuring aircraft airworthiness per Part 43, Part 91 Subpart E, and Part 135 Subpart J
- Managing the Continuous Airworthiness Maintenance Program (CAMP) or equivalent
- Administering the MEL program
- Overseeing maintenance personnel training and qualifications
- Coordinating with the DO on aircraft availability and scheduling

### 3.4 Pilot in Command

The PIC has final authority and responsibility for the safe operation of each flight per 14 CFR 91.3. PIC responsibilities include:
- Pre-flight planning and weather evaluation
- Aircraft pre-flight inspection
- Compliance with all ATC clearances and instructions
- Go/no-go decisions (the PIC may refuse any flight without repercussion)
- Ensuring passengers and/or cargo are properly secured
- Completing all required documentation post-flight
- Reporting any mechanical irregularities, safety events, or hazards

### 3.5 Second in Command (When Required or Used)

The SIC assists the PIC and shall be current and qualified in the aircraft type. SIC duties include:
- Assisting with navigation, communications, and systems monitoring
- Performing PIC duties if the PIC becomes incapacitated
- Monitoring approach procedures and callouts per SOP

### 3.6 Operational Control Center / Dispatch

The OCC provides 24/7 operational support including:
- Receiving and processing mission requests
- Conducting or supporting pre-flight risk assessments
- Flight following and position reporting per 14 CFR 135.79
- Weather monitoring and dissemination to flight crews
- Coordinating diversions, refueling stops, and alternate destinations
- Maintaining communication with crews throughout duty periods
- Activating emergency response procedures when required

#### Mission Type: Air Medical
The OCC (also referred to as the Communications Center or Comm Center) receives transport requests from hospitals, 911 centers, and other agencies. The OCC collects patient acuity information, weather data, and landing zone details before offering the mission to the assigned crew.

#### Mission Type: Charter (Passenger)
Scheduling and dispatch coordinates with the PIC on passenger counts, baggage estimates, route planning, and fuel requirements. The PIC and dispatch share operational control responsibility per 14 CFR 135.77.

#### Mission Type: Cargo
Dispatch coordinates with shippers on cargo manifests, hazmat declarations (if any), weight and balance data, and delivery schedules. The PIC has authority to refuse any cargo that cannot be safely loaded or restrained.

### 3.7 Medical Crew (Air Medical Operations)

#### Mission Type: Air Medical
Medical crewmembers (Flight Nurses, Flight Paramedics, Flight Respiratory Therapists) are responsible for:
- Patient care from acceptance through transfer to the receiving facility
- Pre-flight medical equipment checks using the challenge/response checklist
- Assisting the pilot with hazard identification during takeoff, landing, and taxi
- Completing patient care documentation
- Participating in the crew risk assessment
- Complying with all safety directives from the PIC

Medical crew are NOT to interfere with the pilot's operation of the aircraft. Requests to change altitude, heading, or destination for patient care reasons must be communicated to the PIC, who retains final authority.

### 3.8 Fitness for Duty -- IMSAFE

All crewmembers shall evaluate their fitness for duty before each shift using the IMSAFE checklist:

| Factor | Consideration |
|---|---|
| **I** -- Illness | Am I feeling ill or showing symptoms of illness? |
| **M** -- Medication | Am I taking any medication that could impair performance? |
| **S** -- Stress | Am I experiencing unusual personal or professional stress? |
| **A** -- Alcohol | Have I consumed alcohol within the preceding time limits (8 hours/0.04 BAC per 14 CFR 91.17)? |
| **F** -- Fatigue | Have I had adequate rest? Am I fatigued? |
| **E** -- Eating/Emotion | Have I eaten properly? Am I emotionally prepared to fly? |

Any crewmember who cannot answer satisfactorily shall remove themselves from duty. No repercussions shall result from a fitness-for-duty stand-down.

## Chapter 4 -- Flight Preparation and Planning

### 4.1 Pre-Flight Risk Assessment

A two-part risk assessment shall be completed before every flight:

**Part 1 -- Static Risk Assessment:**
Completed at the beginning of each duty period. Evaluates baseline conditions:
- Crew experience levels and currency
- Weather trends and forecasts
- Aircraft status and MEL items
- Base/route familiarity
- Time of day (day vs. night operations)

**Part 2 -- Dynamic Risk Assessment:**
Completed for each individual flight/mission. Evaluates mission-specific factors:
- Current weather at departure, en route, and destination
- Specific route hazards (terrain, obstacles, TFRs)
- Patient acuity / passenger needs / cargo characteristics
- Crew fatigue state at time of mission
- Landing zone or airport conditions
- Time pressure or operational pressure

Risk scores are calculated by assigning numerical values to each factor. Scores exceeding established thresholds require consultation with the AOC/DO before the flight may proceed.

### 4.2 Weather Planning

#### 4.2.1 Weather Minimums

Weather minimums vary by operation type, aircraft category, and pilot qualification level. All minimums meet or exceed 14 CFR 135 requirements.

**VFR Minimums (Day):**

| Airspace | Ceiling | Visibility |
|---|---|---|
| Controlled (Class C/D/E) | Per 14 CFR 135.205 | Per 14 CFR 135.205 |
| Uncontrolled (Class G) | Per 14 CFR 135.209 | Per 14 CFR 135.209 |
| [Operator] Company Minimums | [XXX ft AGL] | [X SM] |

**VFR Minimums (Night):**

| Airspace | Ceiling | Visibility |
|---|---|---|
| All | Per 14 CFR 135.207 | Per 14 CFR 135.207 |
| [Operator] Company Minimums (Night) | [XXX ft AGL] | [X SM] |

**IFR Minimums:** Per published approach procedures. Company minimums may be higher than published minimums for certain airports or during certain conditions.

**Newly Hired / Restricted Pilots:** Pilots at lower proficiency levels (e.g., initial or restricted status) shall operate to higher weather minimums as specified in the Training Program section. Typical restrictions include ceiling and visibility minimums 500 ft / 1 SM above standard company minimums until the pilot achieves full qualification.

#### 4.2.2 Weather Sources

Pilots shall obtain weather from approved sources including:
- FSS (Flight Service Station) briefings
- NWS/AWC products (METARs, TAFs, AIRMETs, SIGMETs, PIREPs)
- OCC weather displays and briefings
- Approved EFB weather applications

### 4.3 NOTAMs and TFRs

The PIC shall review all applicable NOTAMs and TFRs along the planned route of flight. The OCC shall provide current NOTAM information as part of the mission brief.

### 4.4 Fuel Planning

Fuel planning shall comply with 14 CFR 135.209 (VFR) and 14 CFR 135.223 (IFR) requirements:

**VFR:** Fuel to fly to the first point of intended landing and, assuming normal cruising fuel consumption, to fly after that for at least 30 minutes (day) or 45 minutes (night).

**IFR:** Fuel to fly to and execute an approach at the destination, then fly to the alternate (if required), and then fly for 45 minutes at normal cruising speed.

#### Mission Type: Cargo
Cargo operations require fuel planning that accounts for the actual cargo weight, not estimates. The PIC shall verify cargo weights before calculating fuel requirements. Heavy-cargo flights may require reduced fuel loads with planned fuel stops.

### 4.5 Weight and Balance

A weight and balance calculation shall be completed before every flight. The calculation must show the aircraft is within approved CG limits for all phases of flight (takeoff, cruise, landing).

#### Mission Type: Air Medical
Standard patient weight shall be obtained or estimated. Medical equipment weights are pre-calculated per aircraft configuration. When patient weight is unknown, a default weight of [XXX] lbs shall be used.

#### Mission Type: Charter (Passenger)
Standard passenger weights per AC 120-27 (or current edition) shall be used unless actual weights are obtained. The PIC may require actual weights for operations near maximum gross weight.

#### Mission Type: Cargo
Actual cargo weights are required -- estimated weights are not acceptable. The shipper must provide an accurate weight for each piece of cargo. The PIC shall verify that cargo is distributed within CG limits and that no single item exceeds the aircraft floor loading limit.

## Chapter 5 -- Airworthiness and MEL

### 5.1 Aircraft Airworthiness Determination

No aircraft shall be operated unless it is in an airworthy condition. The PIC is responsible for determining airworthiness before each flight per 14 CFR 91.7.

An aircraft is considered airworthy when:
- All required inspections are current (annual, 100-hour if applicable, progressive)
- All applicable Airworthiness Directives (ADs) are complied with
- All life-limited components are within their limits
- All required placards and markings are in place and legible
- All inoperative equipment has been addressed per the MEL or 14 CFR 91.213

### 5.2 Minimum Equipment List (MEL)

[Operator] maintains an FAA-approved MEL for each aircraft type. The MEL is the only approved method for operating with inoperative equipment.

#### MEL Categories

| Category | Repair Interval |
|---|---|
| A | As specified (e.g., 1 calendar day, 1 flight, etc.) |
| B | 3 consecutive calendar days |
| C | 10 consecutive calendar days |
| D | 120 consecutive calendar days |

#### MEL Procedures

1. The PIC identifies an inoperative item.
2. The PIC consults the MEL to determine if the item is listed.
3. If the item is listed, the PIC verifies all conditions and limitations (O and M procedures) are met.
4. The "O" (Operations) procedures are completed by the flight crew.
5. The "M" (Maintenance) procedures are completed by qualified maintenance personnel.
6. The item is placarded "INOPERATIVE" and the aircraft logbook is annotated.
7. The maintenance tracking system is updated with the MEL deferral and its expiration date.
8. Items not listed in the MEL must be repaired before further flight, or the PIC must determine that the item is not required by the Type Certificate, 14 CFR 91.205, or any applicable regulation.

### 5.3 Non-Essential Furnishings (NEF) Deferrals

Interior furnishings that are not essential for safe flight may be deferred when inoperative. NEF deferrals follow a similar process to MEL deferrals but are tracked separately.

### 5.4 Maintenance Reporting

Pilots shall report all mechanical irregularities in the aircraft discrepancy log. Items reported include:
- Engine anomalies (unusual sounds, vibrations, indication fluctuations)
- Avionics malfunctions
- Structural concerns (bird strikes, lightning strikes, hail damage)
- Hydraulic or flight control anomalies
- Landing gear irregularities
- Cabin equipment defects

The DM or designee reviews all discrepancy reports and determines corrective action. An aircraft shall not return to service until all discrepancies have been either corrected or properly deferred per the MEL.

## Chapter 6 -- Weight and Balance

### 6.1 General Requirements

The PIC shall ensure that the aircraft is loaded within the approved weight and center-of-gravity (CG) envelope before every flight. Weight and balance data sheets or electronic calculations shall be completed and retained per 14 CFR 135.63(c).

### 6.2 Basic Operating Weight

The Basic Operating Weight (BOW) for each aircraft includes:
- Aircraft empty weight (from the most recent weight and balance report)
- Crew weight (standard or actual)
- Standard equipment and furnishings
- Unusable fuel and oil

### 6.3 Payload Calculations

#### Mission Type: Air Medical
Payload includes patient weight, medical crew weight, medical equipment, and any accompanying persons. Standard equipment weights for each aircraft configuration are maintained by the DM and posted in each aircraft.

#### Mission Type: Charter (Passenger)
Payload includes passenger weights, baggage, and any incidental cargo. Standard weights per AC 120-27 may be used. Summer and winter standard weights apply per the current AC.

#### Mission Type: Cargo
All cargo must be weighed -- estimated weights are not acceptable for cargo operations. The PIC shall verify:
- Total cargo weight does not exceed the aircraft payload capacity (after accounting for fuel and crew)
- Individual item weights do not exceed floor loading limits
- CG remains within limits throughout the flight (accounting for fuel burn)
- Cargo is distributed to maintain CG within the forward and aft limits

### 6.4 Maximum Takeoff and Landing Weights

Takeoff and landing weights must not exceed the aircraft Flight Manual limits adjusted for:
- Airport elevation and density altitude
- Runway length and condition
- Obstacle clearance requirements
- One-engine-inoperative climb performance (if multi-engine)

## Chapter 7 -- Passengers, Cargo, and Hazardous Materials

### 7.1 Passenger Briefing

Per 14 CFR 135.117, the PIC or designee shall brief all passengers before each flight on:
- Smoking restrictions
- Seat belt use and location
- Emergency exit location and operation
- Fire extinguisher location
- Use of flotation devices (if applicable, overwater flights)
- Normal and emergency procedures
- Oxygen equipment use (if applicable)
- Any other information the PIC deems necessary

#### Mission Type: Air Medical -- Patient Companion/Family Member
Family members or patient companions may be transported per [Operator] policy. Before transport:
1. The companion must be screened for fitness to fly (medical condition, emotional state).
2. The companion must receive a full safety briefing including egress procedures.
3. The companion must sign a consent/release form.
4. The companion must wear hearing protection and seat belt restraints at all times.
5. The PIC has final authority on whether a companion may be transported.
6. No companion transport during scene responses or when the companion's presence would interfere with patient care.

### 7.2 Carry-On Baggage

All carry-on items must be stowed under a seat, in an approved storage compartment, or otherwise secured so they do not become a projectile during turbulence or an emergency.

### 7.3 Cargo Loading and Restraint

#### Mission Type: Charter (Passenger)
Passenger baggage is loaded in approved compartments. The PIC verifies proper restraint and weight distribution.

#### Mission Type: Cargo -- General Procedures
1. All cargo must be loaded and restrained so that it does not shift during any phase of flight, including turbulence.
2. Tiedown devices must meet TSO-C172 (Cargo Restraint Systems) or equivalent strength standards.
3. Restraint calculations: cargo tiedown must withstand the following load factors:
   - Forward: 9g (or as specified in aircraft Flight Manual)
   - Aft: 1.5g
   - Lateral: 1.5g
   - Upward: 2g (or as specified)
   - Downward: As specified in the aircraft Flight Manual
4. Heavy items must be placed on the floor, not on top of lighter items.
5. Cargo must not block emergency exits, obstruct access to fire extinguishers, or impede crew movement.
6. Cargo must not exceed published floor loading limits (lbs per square inch).
7. Load manifests per 14 CFR 135.63(c) must be completed for every cargo flight.

#### Mission Type: Cargo -- Cargo Nets and Restraint Systems
- Cargo nets must be inspected before each use for tears, fraying, and hardware damage.
- Net attachment points must be factory-installed or STC-approved.
- Palletized cargo must have restraint straps or nets over the entire pallet.
- Loose items must be placed in bins or bags before being loaded.

### 7.4 Hazardous Materials

#### 7.4.1 General Policy
[Operator] [does / does not] accept hazardous materials for transport. If hazardous materials are accepted, all operations shall comply with 49 CFR 171-180 and the ICAO Technical Instructions for the Safe Transport of Dangerous Goods by Air.

#### 7.4.2 Hazmat Training (If Applicable)
Per 49 CFR 175.20, all personnel involved in accepting, handling, loading, or transporting hazardous materials must complete initial and recurrent hazmat training. Training records must be maintained per 49 CFR 172.704.

#### 7.4.3 Dangerous Goods Acceptance (Cargo Operations)
When [Operator] accepts dangerous goods for cargo transport:
1. The shipper must provide a properly completed Dangerous Goods Declaration (Shipper's Declaration for Dangerous Goods) per 49 CFR 172.204.
2. The shipment must be properly classified, packaged, marked, labeled, and documented per 49 CFR 172-173.
3. An acceptance check must be performed by a trained and authorized employee.
4. The PIC must be provided with written notification of the dangerous goods onboard (NOTOC) per 49 CFR 175.33.
5. Incompatible materials must be segregated per the segregation table in 49 CFR 177.848.
6. Quantities must not exceed the limits in 49 CFR 175.75.

#### 7.4.4 Forbidden Items
The following are forbidden on all flights regardless of mission type:
- Explosives (unless specifically authorized under 49 CFR 175.10)
- Flammable gases and liquids (except as permitted for air medical equipment)
- Radioactive materials above de minimis levels (unless specifically authorized)
- Any item that could endanger the aircraft or persons aboard

#### Mission Type: Air Medical
Medical oxygen, battery-powered medical devices, and certain medications (including controlled substances in approved containers) are permitted per DOT/FAA exemptions applicable to air ambulance operations. HAZMAT scene responses shall follow [Operator] HAZMAT standoff procedures -- the aircraft shall not land within the HAZMAT exclusion zone.

### 7.5 Transport Limitations

#### Mission Type: Air Medical
- Maximum patient weight: [XXX] lbs (limited by aircraft loading system and configuration)
- Long-range transport (greater than 3 hours flight time): requires AOC/DO approval
- Maximum crew duty day for transport operations: per 14 CFR 135.267 and company policy (typically 14-16 hours)
- Two-patient transport: requires specific aircraft configuration approval and documented medical crew agreement
- Back-country/wilderness response: minimum two crewmembers must agree to accept ("two to go, one to say no")

## Chapter 8 -- Aircraft Fueling

### 8.1 Fueling Procedures

All fueling operations shall comply with NFPA 407 (Standard for Aircraft Fuel Servicing) and [Operator] policies.

### 8.2 Fuel Quality

- Only fuel meeting the aircraft manufacturer's specifications shall be used.
- Fuel sumps shall be drained and checked before the first flight of the day and after each refueling.
- Fuel samples showing contamination (water, sediment, discoloration) shall be reported and the aircraft shall not be fueled from that source until cleared.

### 8.3 Hot Refueling (Rotors Turning)

#### Mission Type: Air Medical (Rotor Wing)
Hot refueling (refueling with engines running and rotors turning) may be conducted when:
1. The procedure is approved in the aircraft Flight Manual.
2. A trained ground crew or fueler is available.
3. The PIC remains at the flight controls.
4. All passengers and non-essential personnel are clear of the refueling zone.
5. Fire extinguishing equipment is readily available.
6. Ground bonding is established before the fuel nozzle is connected.

## Chapter 9 -- Normal Operating Procedures

### 9.1 Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs)

All flight operations shall be conducted in accordance with established SOPs. SOPs are designed to standardize procedures, reduce errors, and enhance safety. Deviations from SOPs are permitted only when necessary for safety and must be reported to the CP.

### 9.2 Checklists

All checklists prescribed by the aircraft Flight Manual and [Operator] SOPs shall be completed. Challenge-and-response format is required for all multi-crew operations. Single-pilot operations shall use the read-and-do method.

### 9.3 Sterile Cockpit

Per 14 CFR 135.100 (sterile cockpit rule), during critical phases of flight (taxi, takeoff, landing, and all operations below 10,000 feet MSL except cruise), only duties required for the safe operation of the aircraft shall be performed. Non-essential conversation and activities are prohibited.

#### Mission Type: Air Medical
Medical crew shall not communicate non-essential information to the pilot during sterile cockpit periods. Patient care requests that require pilot action (altitude change, expedited routing) shall be communicated clearly and concisely.

### 9.4 Crew Resource Management (CRM)

All crewmembers are trained in CRM principles. CRM applies to every flight and includes:
- Open communication and assertiveness
- Shared situational awareness
- Workload management
- Decision-making processes
- Conflict resolution

Any crewmember may and should speak up about safety concerns regardless of rank or role.

### 9.5 Stabilized Approach Criteria

#### Fixed Wing
An approach is considered stabilized when, by 1,000 ft AGL (IMC) or 500 ft AGL (VMC):
- The aircraft is on the correct flight path (lateral and vertical)
- The airspeed is within the target approach speed +10/-5 knots
- The aircraft is in the landing configuration (gear down, flaps as required)
- Power is stabilized at an appropriate setting
- All briefings and checklists are complete
- Sink rate does not exceed 1,000 fpm

If these criteria are not met, a go-around shall be executed.

#### Rotor Wing
An approach is considered stabilized when:
- The aircraft is on the correct approach path to the intended landing point
- Airspeed and rate of descent are appropriate for the approach type
- The pilot has the landing area in sight and has identified hazards
- Power is adequate for a go-around at any point

If the pilot loses sight of the landing area, encounters unexpected obstacles, or determines the approach is unsafe, a go-around shall be executed.

### 9.6 Altitude Callouts

Standard altitude callouts during approach:
- "One thousand" (1,000 ft AGL)
- "Five hundred" (500 ft AGL)
- "Three hundred" (300 ft AGL)
- "Two hundred" (200 ft AGL)
- "One hundred" (100 ft AGL)
- "Minimums" (at DA/DH or MDA for instrument approaches)

### 9.7 Ground Operations

- Taxi speed shall not exceed a brisk walk.
- External lighting (navigation, beacon, taxi lights) shall be used per SOP.
- The PIC shall verify the taxi route before movement.
- Hold short instructions shall be read back to ATC.

## Chapter 10 -- Emergency Procedures

### 10.1 General Emergency Authority

The PIC may deviate from any rule in 14 CFR to the extent required to meet an emergency per 14 CFR 91.3(b). The PIC shall file a written report if requested by the FAA.

### 10.2 Emergency Equipment

Each aircraft shall carry emergency equipment as required by 14 CFR 135.167-177:
- Fire extinguisher(s)
- First aid kit
- Emergency locator transmitter (ELT)
- Flashlights (for night operations)
- Flotation devices (for overwater operations per 14 CFR 135.167)
- Survival equipment (for extended overwater or remote operations)

### 10.3 Engine Failure Procedures

Engine failure procedures are conducted per the aircraft Flight Manual. General principles:

**Single-Engine Aircraft:**
1. Maintain best glide speed (Vg).
2. Select a suitable landing area.
3. Attempt engine restart if conditions permit.
4. Declare an emergency.
5. Prepare for forced landing (secure passengers, brief medical crew if applicable).

**Multi-Engine Aircraft:**
1. Identify the failed engine (dead foot = dead engine).
2. Verify by retarding the throttle on the suspected failed engine.
3. Feather the propeller / secure the failed engine per Flight Manual.
4. Maintain Vyse or better.
5. Declare an emergency.
6. Proceed to the nearest suitable airport.

### 10.4 Inadvertent IMC (IIMC) -- Rotor Wing

IIMC recovery is trained and practiced regularly. The standard IIMC recovery procedure:

1. **Immediately** transition to instrument flight references.
2. Level the wings (or zero the bank angle for helicopters).
3. Maintain or establish a positive climb rate.
4. Activate the autopilot if equipped and functional.
5. Inform the OCC and declare an emergency with ATC.
6. Navigate to VMC or proceed to an IFR alternate (if IFR equipped and current).
7. Do NOT attempt to descend below clouds unless terrain clearance is assured.

### 10.5 Fire -- In-Flight

**Engine Fire:**
1. Execute engine shutdown per Flight Manual.
2. Activate fire extinguisher (engine fire extinguishing system, if equipped).
3. Declare an emergency and land as soon as practicable.

**Cabin/Electrical Fire:**
1. Identify the source (smoke, smell, visible flame).
2. De-energize suspected circuits (pull circuit breakers per Flight Manual).
3. Use the cabin fire extinguisher if accessible and safe to do so.
4. Ventilate if possible (open vents/windows per Flight Manual).
5. Declare an emergency and land immediately.

### 10.6 Emergency Communications

- Declare emergencies on the current ATC frequency or 121.5 MHz.
- Squawk 7700 for emergencies, 7600 for communication failure, 7500 for hijacking.
- The OCC monitors emergency frequencies and can relay communications.

### 10.7 Emergency Evacuation

The PIC shall direct evacuation procedures. Steps:
1. Shut down engines and secure the aircraft (fuel, electrical, ignition).
2. Command passengers/patients to evacuate via the nearest exit.
3. Crewmembers assist with evacuation and account for all persons.
4. Move at least 200 feet upwind of the aircraft.
5. Do not re-enter the aircraft.

### 10.8 Ditching (Overwater Emergency Landing)

If ditching is required:
1. Declare an emergency and transmit position.
2. Brief passengers/crew on ditching procedures and flotation equipment use.
3. Secure loose items.
4. Ditch into the wind (or along the swell, per conditions).
5. After touchdown, evacuate promptly and deploy life rafts (if equipped).

## Chapter 11 -- Helicopter-Specific Procedures

> This chapter applies to rotor-wing operations only. Fixed-wing operators may omit this chapter.

### 11.1 Helicopter Landing Zone (LZ) Operations

#### Mission Type: Air Medical
LZ procedures for scene responses:
1. The OCC obtains LZ coordinates and ground contact information.
2. The PIC conducts a high reconnaissance pass to evaluate the LZ for hazards (wires, trees, terrain, slope, surface conditions).
3. Ground personnel shall be briefed on LZ safety (stay clear of the aircraft, approach from the front only when signaled by the pilot, no smoking, secure loose items).
4. LZ minimum dimensions: [XXX] ft x [XXX] ft (per aircraft type).
5. LZ lighting (for night operations): minimum of [X] light sources at corners, no spotlights directed at the aircraft.

### 11.2 Rooftop / Helipad Operations

- Verify helipad weight limits before landing.
- Be aware of turbulence from adjacent buildings.
- Maintain escape routes during approach and departure.
- Hospital helipad operating procedures are maintained in the OCC database.

### 11.3 Off-Airport / Remote Area Operations

- High reconnaissance is mandatory before landing at unimproved sites.
- Two-pilot operations are recommended for off-airport landings at night.
- Back-country/wilderness operations require crew consensus: "two to go, one to say no."
- Wires are the primary threat during low-altitude operations. Assume wires exist between any two poles or structures.

### 11.4 Helicopter Night Vision Goggle Operations (HNVGO)

If [Operator] is authorized for HNVGO:
1. NVG operations require specific training, currency, and OpSpecs authorization.
2. NVG currency: [X] hours goggle time per [X]-day period.
3. Lighting restrictions: internal and external lighting must be NVG-compatible.
4. Pre-flight NVG equipment inspection is required.
5. NVG operations are subject to higher weather minimums than unaided night flight.
6. HNVGO training includes: aircraft emergencies while wearing goggles, degraded visual environment recognition, transition to instruments (goggle failure / IIMC).

### 11.5 Power Line / Wire Awareness

- Wires are the number one obstacle hazard for low-altitude helicopter operations.
- Assume wires are present wherever poles, towers, or structures exist.
- When crossing wires, fly over the highest visible support structure (not between supports).
- Report new wire installations observed during flight to the OCC.

## Chapter 12 -- Fixed Wing Operations

> This chapter applies to airplane operations only. Helicopter-only operators may omit this chapter.

### 12.1 Part 135 Airplane Requirements

All airplane operations comply with 14 CFR 135 Subpart I (Airplane Performance Operating Limitations) as applicable:
- Takeoff and landing performance calculations per 14 CFR 135.379-399
- Obstacle clearance per 14 CFR 135.381
- En route performance per 14 CFR 135.383-387

### 12.2 Instrument Approaches

Instrument approaches shall be conducted per published procedures and [Operator] SOP:
- Briefings shall be completed before the initial approach fix (IAF).
- The PIC shall call out crossing altitudes, MDA/DA, and missed approach point.
- Circling approaches require extra caution for terrain and obstacle avoidance.
- Approaches to airports with limited lighting or in remote areas may require higher minimums per company policy.

### 12.3 Fixed Wing Patient Loading (Air Medical)

#### Mission Type: Air Medical
Patient loading procedures for fixed-wing aircraft:
1. Position the aircraft for loading (nose into wind if possible).
2. Deploy the patient loading system (e.g., electric lift, ramp).
3. The PIC operates the loading system -- no other person is authorized to operate the lift mechanism.
4. The medical crew guides the patient litter onto the loading system.
5. The litter is secured to the cabin restraint system.
6. Verify all restraint pins, latches, and locking mechanisms are properly engaged.
7. Stow the loading system and secure the cabin door.

### 12.4 Cabin Pressurization and Altitude Management

#### Mission Type: Air Medical
Cabin altitude must be managed for patient care:
- Sea-level cabin pressure is required for conditions including: pneumothorax, decompression sickness, gas gangrene, air embolism, recent eye surgery with gas bubble, bowel obstruction.
- 4,000 ft maximum cabin altitude for most stable patients.
- The PIC shall coordinate with medical crew on altitude restrictions before departure.
- Oxygen requirements increase with cabin altitude -- see oxygen duration calculation tables.

#### Mission Type: Charter (Passenger)
Per 14 CFR 135.89, cabin altitude shall not exceed 10,000 ft MSL without supplemental oxygen available for passengers. Above 15,000 ft MSL cabin altitude, oxygen must be provided and used.

#### Mission Type: Cargo
Cargo with altitude-sensitive contents (biological specimens, pressurized containers, lithium batteries) may require altitude restrictions. The PIC shall review cargo documentation for altitude limitations.

## Chapter 13 -- Ground De-Ice/Anti-Ice Program

### 13.1 Clean Aircraft Concept

No aircraft shall depart with frost, ice, snow, or slush adhering to any critical surface (wings, stabilizers, control surfaces, engine inlets, propellers, rotor blades, windshield, pitot-static system, antennas, or any other surface that could adversely affect aircraft performance or control).

### 13.2 De-Ice/Anti-Ice Fluid Types

| Type | Purpose | Holdover Time |
|---|---|---|
| Type I | De-icing (removal of existing contamination) | Shortest (typically 3-22 min depending on conditions) |
| Type II | Anti-icing (prevention of ice formation) | Medium (up to 45 min depending on conditions) |
| Type IV | Anti-icing (extended holdover) | Longest (up to 80 min depending on conditions) |

### 13.3 De-Icing Procedures

1. De-icing shall be performed by trained personnel only.
2. Type I fluid is applied heated to remove contamination.
3. Anti-icing fluid (Type II or IV) may be applied after de-icing to extend holdover time.
4. The PIC shall verify the holdover time using the current Holdover Time Tables (from the FAA or TC Canada) based on the fluid type, OAT, and precipitation type.
5. If the holdover time expires before takeoff, the aircraft must be re-inspected and re-treated if contamination has formed.

### 13.4 Pre-Takeoff Contamination Check

A pre-takeoff contamination check must be performed by a qualified person (pilot, trained ground crew) within 5 minutes of takeoff. This check shall confirm no frozen contamination is present on critical surfaces. The PIC has final authority on aircraft cleanliness.

## Chapter 14 -- Operational Control Center (OCC) Procedures

### 14.1 OCC Staffing and Equipment

The OCC shall be staffed 24/7 with qualified personnel. OCC equipment includes:
- Weather monitoring systems (including radar, satellite imagery, and MOS data)
- Flight tracking and communication systems
- Radio and telephone communication capability with all bases and aircraft
- Computerized flight planning and risk assessment tools
- Emergency notification systems

### 14.2 Flight Following

Per 14 CFR 135.79, [Operator] maintains a flight-following system to monitor the progress of each flight. The OCC:
- Receives position reports from aircraft at defined intervals or via ADS-B/satellite tracking.
- Monitors flight progress against the planned route and estimated times.
- Initiates overdue aircraft procedures when position reports are not received.

### 14.3 Overdue Aircraft Procedures

If an aircraft fails to report within [X] minutes of the expected time:
1. The OCC attempts to contact the aircraft on all available frequencies.
2. The OCC contacts ATC facilities along the route.
3. The OCC notifies the DO/AOC.
4. If the aircraft cannot be located within [X] minutes, the OCC activates the emergency response plan.
5. The AFRCC (Air Force Rescue Coordination Center) is notified at 1-800-851-3051 per NTSB Part 830 and company policy.

### 14.4 Mission Acceptance and Dispatch

#### Mission Type: Air Medical
1. The OCC receives the transport request.
2. The OCC determines aircraft and crew availability.
3. The OCC evaluates weather, route, and risk factors.
4. The mission is offered to the crew.
5. The crew conducts their own risk assessment.
6. If accepted, the OCC provides mission details (destination, patient information, weather, NOTAMs).
7. If the risk score exceeds the threshold, the AOC/DO is consulted.

#### Mission Type: Charter (Passenger)
1. The charter request is received with passenger count, baggage, departure, and destination.
2. Scheduling coordinates with available aircraft and crew.
3. The PIC receives the trip sheet and conducts pre-flight planning.
4. The PIC and dispatch share operational control per 14 CFR 135.77.

#### Mission Type: Cargo
1. The cargo request is received with weight, dimensions, hazmat classification (if any), and pickup/delivery locations.
2. The dispatcher verifies aircraft weight/volume capacity.
3. The PIC receives the cargo manifest and any dangerous goods documentation.
4. The PIC inspects the cargo before loading and may refuse any item.

Pilots at different experience levels may operate to different weather minimums:

## Chapter 17 -- Accident, Incident, and Safety Reporting

### 17.1 NTSB Reporting Requirements (Part 830)

The PIC and/or DO shall immediately notify the NTSB (1-844-373-9922) in the event of:
- An aircraft accident (as defined in 49 CFR 830.2)
- Any of the incidents listed in 49 CFR 830.5, including:
  - Flight control system malfunction or failure
  - Crewmember inability to perform duties due to illness or injury
  - In-flight fire
  - Aircraft collision in flight
  - Damage to property other than the aircraft estimated over $25,000
  - Release of all or a portion of a propeller blade
  - Turbine engine failure with damage outside containment
  - In-flight shutdown of an engine (multi-engine aircraft)
  - Loss of situational awareness / IIMC event (if resulting in damage or near-miss)

### 17.2 FAA Notification

The DO shall notify the FAA FSDO of any accident, incident, or enforcement action per 14 CFR 135.417 and 14 CFR 830.

### 17.3 Internal Safety Reporting

[Operator] maintains a non-punitive safety reporting system. All personnel are encouraged to report:
- Hazards and unsafe conditions
- Near-miss events
- Equipment malfunctions
- Procedural deviations
- Any event that could compromise safety

Reports are reviewed by the Safety Manager and/or SMS committee. De-identified trend data is shared with all personnel during safety meetings.

### 17.4 Post-Event Procedures

Following an accident or serious incident:
1. Secure the scene and render aid to injured persons.
2. Notify emergency services (911) if applicable.
3. Notify the OCC, who will activate the Emergency Response Plan.
4. Do NOT disturb wreckage except to prevent further injury or damage.
5. Preserve all records, communications, and electronic data.
6. The DO notifies the NTSB, FAA, and company leadership.
7. Crewmembers involved shall be relieved of duty and offered Critical Incident Stress Management (CISM) resources.

### 18.2 EFB Classes

| Class | Description |
|---|---|

- Performance calculations

2. If no backup is available and IFR, request ATC vectors to a suitable airport.
3. Report the EFB failure in the aircraft discrepancy log.

### 19.1 Regulatory Basis

[Operator] maintains an anti-drug and alcohol misuse prevention program per 14 CFR Part 120 and 49 CFR Part 40.

### 19.2 Covered Employees

All safety-sensitive employees are covered, including:
- Pilots
- Flight attendants (if any)
- Dispatchers
- Aircraft maintenance personnel
- Any person performing safety-sensitive functions as defined in 14 CFR 120.105

### 19.3 Testing Requirements

| Test Type | When Conducted |
|---|---|
| Pre-employment | Before performing any safety-sensitive function |
| Random | Unannounced, per DOT random testing rates (currently 25% drug, 10% alcohol per year) |
| Post-accident | After a qualifying accident per 49 CFR 40 |

### 19.4 Alcohol Restrictions

Per 14 CFR 91.17:
- No person may operate or attempt to operate an aircraft within 8 hours after consuming alcohol.
- No person may operate an aircraft with a blood alcohol concentration (BAC) of 0.04% or greater.
- No person may operate an aircraft while under the influence of alcohol.
- [Operator] policy: no alcohol consumption within [X] hours of duty (company standard is typically 12 hours, exceeding the FAA 8-hour rule).

### 19.5 Prohibited Substances

The DOT/FAA five-panel drug test screens for:
1. Marijuana (THC)
2. Cocaine
3. Opiates (codeine, morphine, heroin, hydrocodone, hydromorphone, oxycodone, oxymorphone)
4. Amphetamines (amphetamine, methamphetamine, MDMA, MDA)
5. Phencyclidine (PCP)
